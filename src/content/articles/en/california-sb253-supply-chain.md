> **TL;DR** — California's SB 253 passed its first-phase implementation regulation on Feb 26, 2026, forcing large companies to disclose full-scope emissions. Once Scope 3 is included, Apple's peer ranking drops 35 percentile points and Tesla's drops 50. This law doesn't care what country you're in — if you're in Apple, Walmart, or Microsoft's supply chain, your carbon data is your entry ticket for next year's orders.

I was once in a client meeting about electronic waste recovery when the client suddenly asked: "If our customer — a U.S. public company — asks us to provide carbon emissions data per kilogram of waste processed, can we deliver that?"

I paused. Not because I didn't know how to answer, but because the question arrived about two years earlier than I expected.

In conversations with executives about circular economy opportunities and challenges, the question I hear most often isn't about emission reduction targets or ESG report formats. It's a much more concrete one: "What data do I actually need to prepare, to keep doing business with my international customers?"

The source of that question is a California law whose implementation regulation was just adopted in February 2026. It has an awkward name: SB 253.

## Apple's Own Operations Only Emit 0.38%. Where Are the Other 99.6% Hiding?

Start with a number.

In FY2024, Apple reported roughly 15.5 million metric tons of CO₂ equivalent in total emissions. Scope 1 (its own factories and vehicles) plus Scope 2 (purchased electricity) add up to less than 60,000 tons — only **0.38%** of the total. The other 99.6% — 15.2 million tons — is hidden in places Apple doesn't directly operate: Foxconn's assembly lines, TSMC's fabs, Samsung's display plants, cobalt mines in the DRC.

This isn't Apple's unique problem. A [March 2026 paper in Nature's *Communications Sustainability*](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44458-026-00051-9) shows that across large corporations, Scope 3 on average accounts for **86%** of total emissions, while Scope 1 and 2 combined are only 14%.

In other words, when we've been reading ESG reports and comparing "which company is greener," 86% of the truth has been legally excluded from the picture.

What SB 253 does is bring that 86% back onto the table.

## What Is SB 253, and Why Does It Matter Now?

SB 253's full name is the Climate Corporate Data Accountability Act. California Governor Newsom signed it into law in October 2023. But what really brought it to life was February 26, 2026 — the day CARB formally adopted the first-phase implementation regulation.

Two and a half years passed between "words on paper" and "executable system." Now, it's real.

**Who's covered?** Three thresholds must be crossed simultaneously:

1. A business entity formed under U.S. law (non-U.S. companies with U.S.-registered subsidiaries may also trigger)
2. Doing business in California (annual sales over roughly $757,000, or California sales exceeding 25% of total sales)
3. Total global annual revenue over $1 billion

CARB's preliminary list published in September 2025 covers over 3,100 companies; industry estimates put the final scope at over 5,000. This includes **private giants, not just publicly listed companies**.

**What data has to be filed?**

- **August 10, 2026**: First report on Scope 1 and 2 (covering FY2024 or FY2025 data, depending on fiscal year-end)
- **Starting 2027**: Scope 3 included (exact filing date still being determined in CARB's ongoing rulemaking)
- All data must follow the GHG Protocol and undergo third-party assurance
- For Scope 1 and 2: limited assurance from 2026–2029, upgrading to reasonable assurance from 2030
- Scope 3 assurance requirements are still in CARB's pre-rulemaking phase

**What happens if you don't report?** The statutory maximum penalty is $500,000 per year. But CARB has publicly stated it will apply good-faith enforcement discretion in the first year, prioritizing capacity-building over penalties. This is the classic "slow tightening" regulatory playbook — get everyone used to it, then increase pressure.

## Why Can California Pull This Off When Others Can't?

There's an interesting pattern over the past few years: the major global climate disclosure regimes are all retreating.

- **U.S. federal SEC**: Passed a climate disclosure rule in March 2024, but to reduce pushback, **it dropped Scope 3 entirely**. Within a month, multiple states sued, the SEC paused the rule itself, and by 2025 the new administration stopped defending it in court.
- **EU CSRD**: Originally the most ambitious sustainability reporting directive globally, covering 1,073 data points across climate, biodiversity, labor rights, and more. Industry pushback led to the 2025 Omnibus revision — removing nearly 90% of covered entities and scrapping the upgrade to reasonable assurance.
- **California SB 253**: Asks one question only — "How much carbon did you emit?"

California's smartness lies in dropping the "double materiality" philosophical debate that weighed down the EU, and narrowing the scope to **quantifiable carbon emissions**. No social responsibility, no biodiversity, no labor issues. Just one thing: turning carbon emissions from PR talk into auditable, comparable, accountable data.

Then SB 261 (its sister law, covering climate financial risk) got sued by the U.S. Chamber of Commerce to the Ninth Circuit and caught a preliminary injunction — **but SB 253 is unaffected and remains fully in effect**.

## Apple Drops 35 Percentile Points, Tesla 50: Why the Big Reshuffle?

The Nature paper I mentioned earlier did something that really jolted the industry: they re-ranked companies using full-scope (including Scope 3) carbon intensity.

The results:

- **Apple**: Top 8% of peers using only Scope 1 and 2 (very green); drops to top 43% with Scope 3. **Down 35 percentile points.**
- **Tesla**: Top 14% using only Scope 1 and 2 (auto industry's star pupil); drops to the 64th percentile with Scope 3. **Down a full 50 percentile points** — from front-runner to back of the pack.

The paper's conclusion: when investors allocate capital based on full-scope emissions, **$290,000 out of every $1 million gets reallocated**.

That's a big number. Once regulation forces full-scope disclosure, investors can no longer rely on polished ESG reports to judge carbon performance — they look at the data directly. Carbon footprint stops being a PR tool and becomes a hard metric for capital allocation.

That said, the paper has a limitation worth acknowledging: it uses **estimated data** from S&P Global Sustainable1, not company-reported actuals. Estimations depend heavily on sector-average factors and statistical modeling, so the specific "drop by X percentile points" numbers have precision caveats.

But the **direction** is correct. Once SB 253 enforces full-scope disclosure, this reshuffle stops being an academic simulation and becomes something that actually happens.

## Five Concentric Ripples: How Does a California Law Reach a Small Factory in Vietnam's Bac Ninh Province?

This is what I find most interesting about SB 253 — it's not "direct regulation," it's **market power completing cross-border enforcement**.

Take an iPhone as an example. Trace how many rings this law's reach extends:

**Ring 1: Apple itself.** Apple is a direct target of SB 253 and must report Scope 3 starting 2027. The largest Scope 3 bucket is "Purchased Goods and Services" (Category 1), accounting for 61.75% or 8.2 million tons. To report this, Apple has to request data from its suppliers.

**Ring 2: Foxconn.** When Apple asks Foxconn for carbon data, the request doesn't come in the form of a "law" — it comes as a **procurement contract clause**. Written into the Supplier Code of Conduct, it becomes a condition for maintaining supplier status. If Foxconn doesn't comply, it's not violating California law — it's losing orders from the world's largest tech company.

This is the elegance of long-arm jurisdiction — **the law only reaches Apple, but market power completes the cross-border enforcement**.

**Ring 3: Key component makers.** Foxconn assembles iPhones, but the core components sit further upstream: chips (TSMC), displays (Samsung), camera modules (Largan Precision), passive components (Japan's Murata). When Foxconn realizes its own factory emissions are only part of the picture and "embedded carbon" in purchased chips and displays is the bigger chunk, it has to ask its upstream too. The transmission chain extends from California to Hsinchu, Suwon, Kyoto.

**Ring 4: Southeast Asian assembly plants.** By end of 2023, Vietnam hosted 35 Apple supplier facilities. Foxconn built new plants in Bac Giang and Quang Ninh; Luxshare assembles AirPods in Bac Giang; Goertek makes acoustic components in Bac Ninh. The carbon data request cascades from tier 1 to tier 2 to tier 3, and may eventually land at a 200-worker micro connector factory in Bac Ninh.

**Ring 5: Raw materials and mining.** The 14 key materials in Apple products (aluminum, cobalt, copper, lithium, rare earths, etc.) account for nearly 90% of shipped weight; the DRC supplies roughly 70% of the world's cobalt. A typical cobalt chain runs: DRC artisanal mine → local trader → smelter → battery material supplier → battery maker → assembly line → Apple. When Apple has to quantify this chain for compliance, the data demand reaches the diesel consumption of a specific mine in Katanga, the pump energy at a lithium extraction plant in Chile's Atacama, rare earth smelting emissions in Bayan Obo, Inner Mongolia.

**The whole chain spans 15 countries and 4 continents.** And the trigger point is just one law passed inside California's state capitol.

## What Does This Mean for Taiwanese Companies?

Which ring are Taiwanese companies in?

If you're a tier-one supplier directly serving global brands — TSMC, UMC, ASE, Delta, Hon Hai — you're in Ring 2. You'll be in the first batch to receive carbon data requests.

If you're a mid-sized component maker, passive component maker, or module maker, you're in Ring 3. Your customers will pass the requirement down to you.

If you're an SME in mechanical parts, packaging, logistics, or even waste processing, you're in Ring 4 or further out. The request hasn't arrived yet, but it will — within the next 18 to 24 months.

Back to that client's question from the opening — "Can you give us carbon emissions data per kilogram of waste processed?"

My answer at the time: **not yet, but we will.** Because this isn't a one-off customer request — it's a whole-industry request. Whoever builds this capability first holds the entry ticket for the next round.

In the past, companies reported Scope 3 mostly using the spend-based method or generic sector-average emission factors — translated: "pick a number close enough." But SB 253's third-party assurance mechanism will close that shortcut. Multinationals will be forced to demand their suppliers provide **precise carbon footprint data rooted in actual production activities**.

Suppliers who can't deliver won't receive rejection letters. They'll simply be **quietly dropped from the list** at the next procurement review. That's scarier than a fine.

## Stepping Back: The Lever of Global Climate Governance Is Changing Hands

One bigger observation I want to share.

For the past few decades, global climate governance has relied mostly on top-down sovereign treaties — the Kyoto Protocol, the Paris Agreement — nation-to-nation negotiations, state-level emission pledges. But this machinery has visibly weakened in recent years: trade and political conflicts are constant, multilateralism is sluggish, federal-level climate policies have been shelved or reversed in multiple countries.

Yet carbon emissions haven't declined accordingly, and the governance pressure hasn't disappeared. It's just **changed levers**.

SB 253 demonstrates a bottom-up path: not sovereign treaties but commercial contracts; not national enforcement but multinational procurement power. California's law effectively outsources climate regulation to brand owners sitting at the top of supply chains — Apple, Walmart, Microsoft — letting market power complete the cross-border enforcement. The medium of long-arm jurisdiction is data.

By this logic, **Scope 3 will be the real battleground of the next decade**. And the core of Scope 3 is, essentially, product carbon footprint.

The hardest part about carbon footprint was never measurement — it's **information asymmetry**. Supply chain carbon data doesn't flow between nodes, isn't transparent, isn't verifiable. This is the biggest structural obstacle in global climate governance. From this angle, SB 253 isn't so much a "carbon disclosure law" as it is an **anti-carbon-information-asymmetry law**. It doesn't directly tell you how much to emit — it first makes "how much you emit" an unambiguous, accountable institutional object.

Being honest about one thing: the Nature paper I cited has a flaw. It uses S&P Global Sustainable1 estimated data, not company-reported actuals. Estimations rely on sector averages and statistical modeling. Using that kind of data to argue "35 percentile points of ranking reshuffle" comes with precision caveats.

But the **direction of the paper's argument is right**, and that's exactly what SB 253 is designed to fix: once full-scope disclosure shifts from "estimation" to "measurement plus third-party assurance," the reshuffle stops being academic simulation and becomes what actually happens in the next round of capital markets.

This is why I believe Taiwanese companies shouldn't treat SB 253 as "just another international regulation." It's the first concrete sample of a shift in how global climate governance operates. A second and third will follow in the coming decade. Whoever builds traceable carbon data capabilities first secures a position in the new governance architecture.
