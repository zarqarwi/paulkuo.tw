> **TL;DR** — California SB 253 passed Phase I implementation rules on 2/26/2026, mandating large corporations to disclose full-scope carbon emissions. After including Scope 3, Apple's ranking dropped 35 percentiles, Tesla dropped 50 percentiles. This law doesn't care which country you're in—as long as you're in Apple's, Walmart's, or Microsoft's supply chain, carbon data becomes your entry ticket for next year's orders.

Once, while discussing e-waste recycling with a client, they suddenly asked me: "If our client—a US-listed company—requires us to provide carbon emission details for every kilogram of waste during processing, can you deliver that?"

I was stunned for a moment. Not because I couldn't answer, but because this question appeared earlier than I expected.

When discussing circular economy opportunities and challenges with corporate leadership, the most common question from clients isn't about emission reduction targets or how to write ESG reports. It's more specific: "What data should I actually prepare to continue doing business with my international clients?"

The source of this question comes from a California law that just passed implementation rules in February 2026. The name is quite awkward—SB 253.

## Apple Only Accounts for 0.38% Internally, Where Is the Remaining 99.6% Hidden?

Let me start with a number.

Apple's total carbon emissions for fiscal year 2024 were approximately 15.5 million tons of CO₂ equivalent. Of this, Scope 1 (own factories/vehicles) plus Scope 2 (purchased electricity) totaled less than 60,000 tons, accounting for only **0.38%** of the total. The remaining 99.6%—that's 15.2 million tons—are all hidden in places Apple "can't see": Foxconn's assembly lines, TSMC's fabs, Samsung's display factories, cobalt mines in Congo.

This isn't unique to Apple. [A paper published in March 2026 in *Communications Sustainability*, a Nature subsidiary journal](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44458-026-00051-9), shows that in global large corporations' carbon emission structures, Scope 3 accounts for an average of **86%** of total emissions, while Scope 1 and 2 only account for 14%.

In other words, when we've been looking at corporate ESG reports and comparing "which companies are greener," 86% of the truth has been legally excluded.

California SB 253 aims to bring this 86% back to the surface.

## What Exactly Is SB 253? Why Is It Important Now?

SB 253's full name is the Climate Corporate Data Accountability Act, signed into law by California Governor Newsom in October 2023. But what truly brought it to life was February 26, 2026—when the California Air Resources Board (CARB) officially passed the first phase implementation rules.

From "legal text" to "enforceable system," there was a gap of over two years of rule-making. Now, it's real.

**Who's covered?** Three thresholds must all be crossed simultaneously:

1. Commercial entities incorporated in the United States (non-US companies' US-incorporated subsidiaries may also trigger coverage)
2. Doing business in California (annual sales exceeding approximately $757,000, or California revenue accounting for 25% or more of total revenue)
3. Global annual revenue exceeding $1 billion

CARB's preliminary list published in September 2025 covers over 3,100 companies; industry estimates suggest the final impact could reach over 5,000 companies. This includes **not just public companies, but also private giants**.

**What data must be submitted?**

- **August 10, 2026**: First report on Scope 1 and 2 (reporting FY2024 or FY2025 data depending on fiscal year end date)
- **Starting 2027**: Include Scope 3 (exact filing date to be determined by subsequent CARB rules)
- All data must follow GHG Protocol and undergo third-party assurance
- Scope 1 & 2: "Limited assurance" from 2026–2029, upgrading to "reasonable assurance" in 2030
- Scope 3 assurance requirements still under discussion in CARB pre-rulemaking phase

**What happens if you don't report?** Maximum statutory penalty is $500,000 per year. However, CARB has publicly stated it will take a good-faith approach to enforcement in the first year, focusing on helping companies build capacity rather than penalties. This is regulators' common "gradual tightening" strategy—first get you used to it, then apply pressure.

## Why Can California Do It When Others Can't?

There's been an interesting phenomenon in recent years: major global climate disclosure systems are all retreating.

- **US Federal SEC**: Passed climate disclosure rules in March 2024, but to reduce resistance, **proactively removed Scope 3**. One month after implementation, it was sued by multiple states, and the SEC suspended implementation. After the new administration took office in 2025, it didn't even defend the rules.
- **EU CSRD**: Originally the world's most ambitious sustainability reporting directive, covering 1,073 data points including climate, biodiversity, and labor rights. Later, due to massive industry pushback, the 2025 Omnibus amendment significantly scaled back—removing nearly 90% of applicable companies and canceling reasonable assurance upgrade requirements.
- **California SB 253**: Asks only one question—"How much carbon did you emit?"

California's cleverness lies in abandoning the EU's "Double Materiality" philosophical debates and directly limiting scope to **quantifiable carbon emissions**. No talk of social responsibility, biodiversity, or labor issues. It does one thing: transform carbon emissions from PR rhetoric into auditable, comparable, accountable data.

Meanwhile, SB 261 (sister law governing climate financial risk) was sued by the US Chamber of Commerce in the Ninth Circuit and received a temporary injunction, **but SB 253 remains unaffected and fully in effect**.

## Apple Drops 35 Percentiles, Tesla Drops 50 Percentiles: Why the Ranking Reshuffle?

The Nature paper mentioned earlier did something quite provocative for the industry: they used full-scope (including Scope 3) carbon intensity to re-rank companies.

Results:

- **Apple**: When looking only at Scope 1 & 2, ranked in the top 8% among peers (very green); after including Scope 3, dropped to the 43rd percentile. **Down 35 percentiles**.
- **Tesla**: When looking only at Scope 1 & 2, ranked in the top 14% (automotive industry honor student); after including Scope 3, dropped to the 64th percentile. **Down a full 50 percentiles**—from front of class to back.

The paper's conclusion: when investors allocate capital based on full-scope emissions, **$290,000 out of every $1 million in investment would be reallocated**.

This is a significant number. When regulations force companies to disclose full-scope emissions, investors can no longer rely on pretty ESG reports to judge carbon performance, but look directly at data. Carbon footprint transforms from a PR tool into a hard metric for capital allocation.

However, this paper has its own limitation: they used **estimated data** from the S&P Global Sustainable1 database, not companies' own reported actual measurements. Estimation itself heavily relies on industry average factors and statistical modeling, so specific numbers like "dropped X percentiles" have limited precision.

But the **directional trend** is correct. When SB 253 mandates full-scope disclosure, this reshuffling game will no longer be academic simulation—it's what will actually happen.

## Five Ripple Circles: Why Does California Law Reach a Small Factory in Bac Ninh Province, Vietnam?

This is what I find most interesting about SB 253—it's not "direct regulation" but **uses market forces to achieve cross-border enforcement**.

Take an iPhone as an example to see how many circles this law's power travels:

**Circle 1: Apple itself**. Apple is directly subject to SB 253 and must report Scope 3 starting in 2027. The largest component of Scope 3 is "purchased goods and services" (Cat. 1), accounting for 61.75%, or 8.2 million tons. To report this figure, Apple must request data from suppliers.

**Circle 2: Foxconn**. When Apple requests carbon data from Foxconn, it doesn't appear in "legal" form but as **procurement contract clauses**. Written into supplier codes of conduct, becoming a condition for maintaining supplier qualification. If Foxconn doesn't comply, it's not violating California law—it's losing orders from the world's largest tech company.

This is the elegance of long-arm jurisdiction—**the law only governs Apple, but market forces enforce the law across borders**.

**Circle 3: Key component manufacturers**. Foxconn assembles iPhones, but core components are further upstream: chips (TSMC), displays (Samsung), cameras (Largan Precision), passive components (Japan's Murata). When Foxconn discovers that its direct factory emissions are only part of the story—the "embedded carbon" in purchased chips and displays is the bigger piece—it too must request data upstream. The conduction chain extends from California to Hsinchu, Suwon, and Kyoto.

**Circle 4: Southeast Asian contract manufacturers**. By end-2023, Vietnam hosted production bases for 35 Apple suppliers. Foxconn built new facilities in Bac Giang and Quang Ninh provinces; Luxshare Precision assembles AirPods in Bac Giang; GoerTek produces acoustic components in Bac Ninh. Carbon data requests cascade from first-, second-, third-tier suppliers, potentially reaching a micro-connector factory with only 200 workers in Bac Ninh Province, Vietnam.

**Circle 5: Raw materials and minerals**. Apple products contain 14 key materials (aluminum, cobalt, copper, lithium, rare earths, etc.) accounting for nearly 90% of shipping weight; Congo supplies about 70% of global cobalt. A typical cobalt chain is: artisanal mines in eastern Congo → local traders → smelters → battery material companies → battery manufacturers → assembly lines → Apple. When Apple must quantify this chain for compliance, data requirements will touch diesel consumption at a mine in Congo's Katanga Province, water pump energy consumption at Chile's Atacama Salt Lake, and rare earth smelting emissions in Inner Mongolia's Bayan Obo.

**The entire chain spans 15 countries across 4 continents.** The starting point that drives it all is just a law passed in California's state capitol.

## What Does This Mean for Taiwan Companies?

Which circle are Taiwan companies in this chain?

If you're TSMC, UMC, ASE Group, Delta Electronics, Hon Hai—direct suppliers to global brands—you're in Circle 2. You'll be among the first to receive carbon data requests.

If you're a mid-sized component manufacturer, passive component maker, or module manufacturer, you're in Circle 3. Your customers will pass requirements down to you.

If you make structural components, packaging, provide logistics, or even recycling services as an SME, you're in Circle 4 or beyond. The requests haven't arrived yet, but they will within the next 18–24 months.

Back to my client's original question—"Can you provide carbon emission details for every kilogram of waste during processing?"

My answer then: **Not currently, but we definitely will in the future.** Because this isn't just one client's requirement—it's the entire industry chain's requirement. Whoever builds this capability first gets tomorrow's entry ticket.

Previously, when companies reported Scope 3, most used spend-based methods or generic industry average emission factors—essentially "estimating a roughly correct number." But SB 253's third-party assurance mechanism will block this path. Multinational companies will be forced to require suppliers to provide **precise carbon footprint data based on actual production activities**.

Suppliers who can't provide data won't receive rejection letters. They'll simply be **silently removed from lists** during the next procurement evaluation. This is scarier than fines.

## Stepping Back Further: The Handover of Global Climate Governance

Writing to this point, there's actually a bigger observation I want to share.

For the past few decades, global climate governance has primarily relied on top-down sovereign treaties—the Kyoto Protocol, Paris Agreement, nation-to-nation negotiations, national emission reduction commitments. But this mechanism has been visibly weakening in recent years: ongoing political conflicts between countries, trade conflicts, multilateral fatigue, and federal-level climate policies being shelved or reversed in multiple countries.

However, carbon emissions haven't decreased because of this, nor has governance pressure disappeared. It has simply **changed hands**.

SB 253 demonstrates a bottom-up path: relying not on sovereign treaties but on commercial contracts; not on national enforcement but on multinational corporations' procurement power. This California law essentially "outsources" climate regulatory power to brand companies at the top of supply chains—Apple, Walmart, Microsoft—letting them use market forces to enforce the law across borders. The medium of long-arm jurisdiction is data.

Following this logic, **Scope 3 will be the real battlefield for the next decade**. And the core of Scope 3 is actually product carbon footprint.

The biggest difficulty with carbon footprint has never been inability to measure, but **information asymmetry**—supply chain carbon emission information doesn't flow, isn't transparent, isn't verifiable between nodes. This is also the biggest structural obstacle to global climate governance over the long term. From this perspective, the essence of SB 253 is less a "carbon disclosure law" than **anti-carbon emission information asymmetry legislation**. It doesn't directly regulate how much you emit; it first transforms "how much you emit" into an unambiguous, accountable institutional object.

Of course, I must be honest—the Nature paper I cited earlier has flaws. It uses S&P Global Sustainable1's estimated data, not companies' actual measurements. The estimation itself relies on industry average factors and statistical modeling. Using such data to argue "ranking reshuffled by 35 percentiles" naturally has reduced precision.

But **the directional claim of the paper is correct**, and this is exactly what SB 253 aims to solve: when full-scope disclosure transforms from "estimation" to "actual measurement + third-party assurance," ranking reshuffles won't be academic simulation but what will actually happen in the next round of capital markets.

This is also why I believe Taiwan companies shouldn't treat SB 253 as "yet another international regulation"—it's the first concrete sample of a global climate governance route change. There will be second and third followers in the next decade. Whoever first builds traceable carbon data capabilities gains position in the new governance architecture.

## Concise SB 253 FAQ for Taiwan Suppliers

**Q1: I'm just a Taiwan supplier—will SB 253 really affect me?**

Yes, but it's not the California government directly knocking on your company door, but indirectly "governing" you through customer procurement and contract requirements. As long as your major clients include companies meeting the "operating in California, global annual revenue ≥ $1 billion" criteria, they'll likely be required to report under SB 253, and you'll be pulled into their Scope 3 inventory.

**Q2: What's the timeline? How long do I have to prepare?**

SB 253 requires these large companies to disclose Scope 1 & 2 emissions starting in 2026, then add entire value chain Scope 3 emissions starting in 2027. The California Air Resources Board (CARB) has set the first actual deadline in stone: first batch of Scope 1 & 2 emission reports must be submitted by August 2026 at latest, then Scope 3 comes online in phases. In other words, suppliers have roughly only two to three fiscal years left to practice.

**Q3: What data will clients actually request from me? Will it get tough quickly?**

Initially, most clients will start with coarser information: procurement amounts, items, basic product-category carbon estimates, plus some standardized questionnaires. But as 2026-2027 reporting years approach, combined with third-party verification pressure, more brands will directly require you to provide "specific product line carbon footprints," "process energy consumption data," "raw material and transportation data," and even specify that inventory and calculation methods must comply with international standards like GHG Protocol, ISO 14064/14067.

**Q4: If I don't cooperate in providing data, what's the worst that could happen?**

SB 253 won't directly fine Taiwan suppliers—it fines those companies required to report in California. But practically, if you consistently cannot provide carbon data clients need, or data quality remains poor, your risk rating within client organizations will increase: short-term you'll be listed as a "high uncertainty" supplier; long-term, during contract renegotiation or introduction of new procurement policies, you might be replaced by competitors with more "carbon data mature" capabilities without explicit announcement.

**Q5: So what should my first step be now?**

Don't fantasize about perfect carbon footprint reports for all products at once. A more pragmatic starting approach: organize your company's existing energy, raw material, transportation data into a "searchable, calculable" state; select 1–2 product lines most important to key clients for a complete "end-to-end" pilot inventory; survey existing partners among your accountants, testing agencies, consultants to see if any teams are already doing greenhouse gas inventory or SB 253/IFRS S2 work, and schedule an initial discussion. Get these three things done first, so when clients start sending SB 253/Scope 3 questionnaires and templates between 2025–2027, you won't be that supplier completely unprepared, only able to respond with "let me look into it."
