I work with AI agents every day. Translation, scheduling, social media publishing, cover image generation—an automated pipeline that kicks off the moment I finish writing an article, completing the entire workflow without requiring a second human.

So when Microsoft's Work Trend Index 2025 claims Taiwan "leads globally" in AI readiness, my first reaction isn't pride—it's confusion.

Because looking around, I struggle to find a second Taiwanese company doing what I'm doing.

## The Numbers Are Indeed Beautiful

Let's lay out Taiwan's highlights from the report. Microsoft's survey covers 31 countries and 31,000 respondents, with Taiwan performing at the front of the pack across multiple dimensions.

88% of Taiwan's leaders consider 2025 a pivotal year for rethinking strategy and operations, compared to a global average of 82%. 82% of leaders are confident about expanding their workforce with AI agents within 12-18 months. 58% of organizations are already using agents to fully automate workflows, versus only 46% globally. Most striking is AI agent familiarity—81% of leaders and 60% of employees claim familiarity with AI agents, compared to global figures of 67% and 40% respectively.

These numbers would certainly look impressive on any presentation slide.

## But Numbers Reflect Awareness, Not Action

The problem is that "familiar with" and "actively using" are entirely different things.

Among the Taiwanese business executives I know, quite a few use ChatGPT as a search engine, but those who've actually built AI agent workflows and integrated agents as digital employees into their organizations are few and far between. The Microsoft report introduces a concept called "Frontier Firms"—companies using AI and agents to redesign organizational structures and work methods. Not just installing Copilot, but fundamentally changing who does what, how they do it, and with whom they collaborate.

71% of frontier firm employees believe their company is thriving, compared to only 37% at regular enterprises. 92% find their work meaningful, versus 77% at regular companies.

The difference isn't in tools—it's in mindset.

## Three Signals Worth Deep Consideration

The report contains three structural observations with very practical implications for Taiwanese enterprises.

**First, intelligence becomes infrastructure like water and electricity.** Microsoft uses the term "Intelligence on Tap"—intelligence available on demand. Where you once needed to hire someone for market analysis, you can now have an AI agent deliver a first draft in thirty minutes. This means "having knowledge" is no longer competitive advantage—"being able to assemble knowledge into action" is.

**Second, org charts will be replaced by work charts.** The report proposes the concept of "Work Charts"—future teams won't organize around departments but around tasks, like film crews that disperse after shooting and regroup for the next project. This poses significant challenges for Taiwanese enterprises, as our organizational culture heavily relies on stable hierarchical structures.

**Third, everyone will become an agent manager.** 42% of leaders expect to build multi-agent systems within five years, 41% need to train agents, and 36% need to manage agents. Future work capabilities won't just be "can you use AI" but "can you direct AI to accomplish your defined objectives."

## Taiwan's Real Advantages and Real Risks

Taiwan's impressive numbers have several structural reasons. Technology sensitivity from the semiconductor industry, high density of tech workers, rapid adoption of new tools—these are all real.

But Taiwan also has a deep structural risk: we're too good at "catching up quickly" but not good at "redefining the game."

Installing Copilot, opening ChatGPT, attending AI workshops—these are catching up. But what frontier firms do is: redefine "how much one person can accomplish," redesign "which decisions need humans versus which go to agents," and even rethink "how many people an organization actually needs."

My own experience: one person plus AI agents can achieve output that previously required five to eight people. But this isn't because AI is amazing—it's because I'm willing to completely dismantle and reassemble work processes. Most Taiwanese enterprises haven't reached this stage yet.

## Three Things You Can Do Right Now

Microsoft's recommendations are actually quite straightforward. Let me translate them into my own terms.

**Hire your first digital employee.** Not buying software, but seriously considering a scenario: if your team had one more member who never sleeps, never needs meetings, and can operate 24/7, what would you have it do? Start there.

**Set human-machine ratios.** For every process, ask once: which parts of this must be done by humans? Which parts can be delegated? It's not about layoffs—it's about letting humans do what only humans can do.

**From pilot to full deployment—don't get stuck in POC.** The most common problem with Taiwanese enterprises is perpetual POC mode. Run a pilot for three months, write the report, then what? The difference with frontier firms is they treat AI as organizational strategy, not as a technology project to test.

## Numbers Are Just Entry Tickets

Taiwan's AI readiness numbers are indeed among the world's best. But readiness is an entry ticket, not the finish line.

The real question isn't "have you heard of AI agents" but "has your organization actually changed because of AI."

Microsoft makes it clear: the next few years are critical for either establishing leadership or being overtaken by AI-native competitors. Taiwan is moving in the right direction, but not fast enough, and not deep enough.

That beautiful survey scorecard is waiting to be validated by real organizational transformation.
