Last June, I compiled JD's AI supply chain case study presented at Stanford University. My impression then was: this is a solid record of internal corporate transformation, worth learning from but somewhat distant from us.

Looking back nine months later, my assessment has changed. Not because JD has done anything remarkable since then, but because what they've done is becoming a "standard"—when AI-driven supply chains evolve from competitive advantage to infrastructure, enterprises that don't keep up aren't just "missing a tool," they're "missing a layer of infrastructure."

This transformation demands serious attention from Taiwan's manufacturing and service industries.

## From Stanford Podium to Academic Paper: The JD Model Gets Formally Documented

Let me start with a notable signal. In 2025, Stanford Business School's supply chain management authority Hau L. Lee and UC Berkeley's Zuo-Jun Max Shen wrote JD's AI supply chain practices into a formal academic paper, published on SSRN. The paper uses the classic "Triple-A" framework—Agility, Adaptability, Alignment—to analyze three specific JD cases.

What does this represent? It means JD's approach is no longer just "internal corporate optimization," but has been recognized as an industry standard with universal value. The distance from corporate presentation to academic case study is significant.

## Three-Layer Capabilities: Prediction, Explanation, Optimization

JD's AI supply chain isn't one system, but a stack of three-layer capabilities.

The first layer is intelligent prediction. JD has shopping behavior data from over 700 million users, real-time inventory from over 1,600 self-operated warehouses, plus external variables like weather, holidays, and geopolitics. According to JD's published data, their prediction models trained on this data achieved nearly 15% accuracy improvement. More critically, they use "synthetic data"—simulating successful historical transactions through deep learning—to enhance training samples, enabling models to maintain stability even when facing unprecedented market fluctuations.

The second layer is explainability. This is often overlooked when enterprises implement AI. No matter how accurate predictions are, if business teams don't trust, understand, or dare to use them, it's worthless. JD breaks down each prediction result into "baseline value + contribution values from various factors," making it comprehensible for sales, marketing, and logistics departments to understand why AI makes certain recommendations. They even provide three promotional strategy options for individual products, each corresponding to different costs and expected benefits, letting brand teams choose for themselves.

The third layer is operations optimization. Traditional logistics optimization requires specialized operations research (OR) background, with complex modeling and difficult adjustments. JD has made this layer accessible through natural language—you input "deliver a thousand items to three warehouses at lowest cost," and the system automatically converts it to mathematical models, fetches latest transportation costs, and generates solutions. It can also perform what-if analysis: if a warehouse fails or a route is disrupted, what are the alternatives?

These three layers combined don't just mean "AI helps you work"—they mean "AI helps you think."

## Nine Months Later: From Internal Tools to External Platforms

When I wrote the original piece last year, JD's AI supply chain primarily served itself. Now it has clearly moved toward platform-based export.

After the "Logistics Brain 2.0" upgrade, it's no longer just an automation tool. Through triple mechanisms of global perception, model evolution, and human-machine collaboration, it transforms originally experience-dependent operational processes into data-driven dynamic decision-making. According to JD's published data: operational standardization improved 15%, dispatch efficiency for people, vehicles, goods, and facilities increased nearly 20%, and human-machine collaboration efficiency improved over 20%.

For robotics, JD's "Wolf Pack" series—Smart Wolf, Sky Wolf, Ground Wolf, Flying Wolf, Lone Wolf, Wing Wolf—plus automated sorting walls, have been deployed in over 500 warehouses globally according to JD. In July 2025, JD also released its self-developed unmanned light truck "JD Logistics VAN," featuring 400km full-load range and L4-level autonomous driving on public roads.

More noteworthy is the business model transformation. JD Industrial (JDi) received Hong Kong Stock Exchange IPO approval in 2025, positioned as an "industrial supply chain technology and service platform," with first-half revenue of 10.3 billion RMB, up nearly 19% year-over-year. This essentially packages JD's accumulated AI supply chain capabilities into products for sale to other enterprises.

Meanwhile, JD Logistics' international brand JoyExpress officially entered Europe (UK, Netherlands, Germany, France), JoyLogistics deepened its Middle East presence, and signed strategic cooperation with FII Institute (Future Investment Initiative Institute), covering digital transformation, logistics infrastructure, and cross-border commerce. JD was selected for Gartner's Global Supply Chain Top 25 for the second consecutive year.

The model is clear: first use AI to optimize your own supply chain, accumulate data and models, then transform these capabilities into platforms for external export. Supply chains are no longer just cost centers, but profit centers.

## Why Taiwan Enterprises Should Care

At this point, some might say: JD is a Chinese e-commerce giant with 700 million users and over 1,600 warehouses—what does this have to do with Taiwan's SMEs?

The relationship is significant, but it's not about imitating JD—it's about understanding trends.

The first signal: AI supply chain capabilities are shifting from "build" to "rent." JD Industrial's IPO, SaaS-based supply chain services, API-based logistics interfaces—all point to the same thing: future SMEs won't need to build their own AI teams, but will plug into others' pre-built AI supply chain platforms. The question is: whose platform will you plug into?

The second signal: explainability determines whether AI can truly land. JD invested heavily in "making business teams understand AI decisions"—this isn't a technical issue, it's a trust issue. Many Taiwan enterprises fail at AI implementation not because models are inaccurate, but because frontline staff don't trust or dare follow AI recommendations.

The third signal: the competitive dimension of supply chains is changing. Previously, competition was about "whose logistics is faster and cheaper." Now it's about "whose predictions are more accurate, reactions faster, and exception handling more automated." When JD's AI system can automatically redistribute routes the instant a warehouse fails, this isn't efficiency advantage—it's structural advantage.

For Taiwan's manufacturing and supply chain service providers, the real question is: when your customers start interfacing with you using AI-driven supply chains while you're still managing inventory with Excel, you're not just "slower"—you're "speaking different languages."

## The Essence Revealed by JD

Returning to the core of JD's case. When they shared at Stanford last year, they emphasized four success conditions: cross-disciplinary talent, leadership support, flat organization, massive data.

Looking back nine months later, I think only one truly matters: whether organizations are willing to let AI participate in decision-making, not just generate reports.

Many enterprises' AI implementation stops at the "AI analyzes, humans decide" stage. JD has reached "AI recommends, humans choose," and in some areas already "AI decides, humans monitor exceptions." The gap between these three stages isn't technological—it's about trust.

JD's case tells us: AI supply chains aren't something you can achieve by buying software and hiring a data scientist. It's a systematic transformation involving organizational culture, decision-making processes, and data governance. Technology is the easiest part, talent is harder, and hardest is getting an organization accustomed to "human authority" to learn to trust data and models.

This challenge faces every organization, regardless of size or industry.
