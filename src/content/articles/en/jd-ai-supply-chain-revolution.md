Last June, I compiled an analysis of JD's AI supply chain case shared at Stanford University. My impression then was: this is a solid record of internal corporate transformation, worth learning from but somewhat distant from us.

Nine months later, my assessment has changed. Not because JD did something shocking, but because what they did is becoming a "standard"—when AI-driven supply chains transform from competitive advantage to infrastructure, companies that don't keep up aren't missing "one tool," they're missing "a layer of infrastructure."

This transformation demands serious attention from Taiwan's manufacturing and service industries.

## From Stanford Podium to Academic Paper: JD's Model Gets Formally Documented

First, a noteworthy signal. In 2025, Stanford Business School's supply chain management authority Hau L. Lee and Zhejiang University's Professor Shen Zuyun formalized JD's AI supply chain practices into an academic paper published on SSRN. The paper uses the classic "Triple-A" framework—Agility, Adaptability, Alignment—to analyze three specific JD cases.

What does this represent? It means JD's approach is no longer just "internal enterprise optimization," but recognized as an industry template with universal value. The distance from corporate presentation to academic case study is significant.

## Three-Layer Capabilities: Prediction, Explanation, Optimization

JD's AI supply chain isn't one system, but a stack of three-layer capabilities.

The first layer is intelligent prediction. JD has shopping behavior data from over 700 million users, real-time inventory from over 1,600 self-operated warehouses, plus external variables like weather, holidays, and geopolitics. According to JD's published data, prediction models trained on this data improved accuracy by nearly 15%. More critically, they use "synthetic data"—deep learning simulations of historical successful transactions—to augment training samples, enabling models to maintain stability when facing unprecedented market volatility.

The second layer is explainability. This is what many companies overlook when implementing AI. No matter how accurate predictions are, if business teams don't trust, understand, or dare use them, it's worthless. JD breaks down each prediction result into "baseline value + contribution values from various factors," making AI recommendations comprehensible to sales, marketing, and logistics departments. They even provide three promotional strategies for individual products, each corresponding to different costs and expected returns, letting brands choose themselves.

The third layer is operational optimization. Traditional logistics optimization requires specialized operations research (OR) background—complex modeling, difficult adjustments. JD has made this layer accessible through natural language—input "distribute 1,000 items to three warehouses at lowest cost," and the system automatically converts to mathematical models, fetches latest transportation costs, and generates solutions. It also does What-if analysis: if a warehouse fails or a route is disrupted, what are the alternatives?

These three layers combined don't just mean "AI helps you work," but "AI helps you think."

## Nine Months Later: From Internal Tools to External Platform

When I wrote the original piece last year, JD's AI supply chain primarily served itself. Now it has clearly moved toward platform-based export.

The upgraded "Logistics Brain 2.0" is no longer just an automation tool. Through global perception, model evolution, and human-machine collaboration mechanisms, it transforms operations processes that previously relied on human experience into data-driven dynamic decisions. According to JD's published data: operational standardization improved 15%, human-vehicle-cargo-facility scheduling efficiency improved nearly 20%, and human-machine collaboration efficiency improved over 20%.

In robotics, JD's "Wolf Pack" series products—Smart Wolf, Sky Wolf, Ground Wolf, Flying Wolf, Lone Wolf, Wing Wolf—plus automated sorting walls have been deployed in over 500 warehouses globally according to JD. In July 2025, JD also released its self-developed unmanned light truck "JD Logistics VAN," featuring 400km full-load range and L4-level autonomous driving on public roads.

More noteworthy is the business model transformation. JD Industrial (JDi) received Hong Kong Stock Exchange IPO approval in 2025, positioned as an "industrial supply chain technology and service platform," with first-half revenue of 10.3 billion RMB, up nearly 19% year-over-year. This essentially packages JD's accumulated AI supply chain capabilities into products for other enterprises.

Meanwhile, JD Logistics' international brand JoyExpress officially entered Europe (UK, Netherlands, Germany, France), JoyLogistics deepened Middle East presence, and signed strategic cooperation with FII Institute covering digital transformation, logistics infrastructure, and cross-border commerce. JD was selected for Gartner's Global Supply Chain Top 25 for the second consecutive year.

The model is clear: first optimize your own supply chain with AI, accumulate data and models, then transform this capability into an external platform. Supply chains are no longer just cost centers, but profit centers.

## Why Taiwanese Companies Should Care

At this point, some might say: JD is a Chinese e-commerce giant with 700 million users and over 1,600 warehouses—what does this have to do with Taiwan's SMEs?

The relationship is significant, but not about imitating JD—it's about understanding trends.

The first signal: AI supply chain capabilities are moving from "build" to "rent." JD Industrial's IPO, SaaS-based supply chain services, API-based logistics interfaces—all indicate the same thing: future SMEs won't need their own AI teams, but will connect to others' pre-built AI supply chain platforms. The question is: whose platform will you connect to?

The second signal: explainability determines whether AI can truly land. JD invested heavily in "making business teams understand AI decisions"—this isn't a technical issue, it's a trust issue. Many Taiwanese companies' AI implementations fail not because models are inaccurate, but because frontline people don't trust or dare follow AI recommendations.

The third signal: supply chain competition dimensions are changing. Previously it was about "whose logistics is faster and cheaper," now it's "whose predictions are more accurate, responses faster, anomaly handling more automated." When JD's AI system can automatically reallocate routes the instant a warehouse fails, this isn't an efficiency advantage—it's a structural advantage.

For Taiwan's manufacturing and supply chain service providers, the real question is: when your clients start interfacing with you using AI-driven supply chains while you're still managing inventory with Excel, you're not "slower"—you're speaking different languages.

## The Essence Seen Through JD

Returning to the core of JD's case. When sharing at Stanford last year, they emphasized four success conditions: cross-disciplinary talent, leadership support, flat organizations, and massive data.

Nine months later, I think the most important factor is actually just one: whether organizations are willing to let AI participate in decision-making, not just generate reports.

Many companies' AI implementations stop at the "AI does analysis, humans make decisions" stage. JD reached "AI makes recommendations, humans select options," and in some areas even "AI makes decisions, humans monitor anomalies." The gap between these three stages isn't a technology gap—it's a trust gap.

JD's case tells us: AI supply chains aren't achievable by buying software and hiring a data scientist. It's systemic transformation involving organizational culture, decision processes, and data governance. Technology is the easiest part, talent is harder, and hardest is teaching an organization accustomed to "humans having the final say" to trust data and models.

This challenge faces every company, regardless of size or industry.
