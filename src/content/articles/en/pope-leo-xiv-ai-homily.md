February 19, 2026. The Paul VI Hall in the Vatican. Pope Leo XIV sat down with priests from the Diocese of Rome for a forty-five-minute conversation. Closed doors, no cameras — just a dialogue among shepherds.

A few days later, Vatican News published its report, and global media exploded: "Pope tells priests not to use ChatGPT for homilies!"

Great headline. But most coverage stopped right there — as if this were just another story about an old man who doesn't understand technology.

It wasn't.

## Use It or Lose It

The Pope's first argument was remarkably down-to-earth.

He used an analogy: "Like all the muscles in the body, if we do not use them, if we do not move them, they die. The brain needs to be used, so our intelligence must also be exercised a little so as not to lose this capacity."

This isn't about efficiency. It's about maintaining capacity. Preparing a homily — reading Scripture, wrestling with the text, searching for connections in prayer, translating abstract theological concepts into language the congregation can understand — that process itself is a form of spiritual exercise. Skip it, and you don't just save time. You skip the very training that makes you a pastor.

When I was in seminary, what consumed the most time wasn't writing papers — it was "not understanding." Reading Barth, reading Moltmann, reading theologians who made you feel stupid, then stopping at the places you couldn't comprehend and asking yourself: What is he really saying? Does this connect with my own experience of faith?

That process of "not understanding," in hindsight, was the real learning. If ChatGPT had existed then, I probably would have pasted it in and asked for a summary, then moved on. More efficient, yes — but something would have been lost.

The "atrophy" the Pope described is exactly this.

## AI Will Never Be Able to Share Faith

The second argument cuts deeper.

"To give a true homily is to share faith," the Pope said, "and artificial intelligence will never be able to share faith."

To feel the full weight of that statement, you need to understand how the Catholic Church defines "homily." In Catholic liturgy, a homily is not a speech, not knowledge transfer, not even exhortation. It is a pastor standing before the congregation, drawing on his own experience of encountering God, connecting the Word with the reality of this moment.

The key phrase is "his own experience."

A priest may have visited a dying parishioner that week. He may have heard a heartbreaking story in the confessional. He may have been struck by a Scripture passage during prayer at dawn. These experiences shape the unique voice he brings to the pulpit.

AI can analyze tens of thousands of homilies, identify optimal structures, the most moving rhetoric, the best angles for this week's readings. It can even produce a homily no one could find fault with. But what it cannot do is: witness.

To witness means: I have lived through this, and therefore I can tell you. This is not information. This is existence.

I use AI every day for work — writing articles, running analyses, managing projects, even developing the [CircleFlow service platform](/articles/circleflow-ai-platform). But I'm crystal clear on one thing: AI handles the information layer of my work, not the meaning layer. When I write an article about faith, the thing that gives it life isn't something AI can provide. It's the road I've walked myself.

This is precisely the line the Pope is drawing.

## Don't Chase Likes on TikTok

In the same conversation, the Pope issued another warning: don't chase likes and followers on platforms like TikTok. He called it an illusion — "thinking one is offering oneself" through the internet.

These two things — using AI to write homilies and chasing social media metrics — seem unrelated on the surface but share the same underlying logic: letting external metrics define the value of pastoral work.

AI optimizes quality metrics for your homily: complete structure, clear argument, precise language. Social algorithms optimize influence metrics: reach, engagement, follower growth. But the Pope's question is: "People want to see your faith, your experience of having known and loved Jesus Christ." No metric can measure that.

He urged priests to go out of their churches, to build genuine friendships with young people through sports, art, and cultural activities. He emphasized this requires "time and sacrifice," because many young people live in isolation, trapped in lives of drugs, crime, and violence. Real accompaniment is low-frequency, high-cost, and impossible to scale.

This completely defies Silicon Valley logic. But the Pope doesn't care.

## From Leo XIII to Leo XIV: Between Two Industrial Revolutions

Here's a context that's easy to overlook.

In his very first address to the College of Cardinals after his election, Leo XIV explained that he chose his name to honor Leo XIII. In 1891, Leo XIII published the encyclical Rerum Novarum — the foundational document of Catholic social teaching, written in response to the labor exploitation, wealth disparity, and social fractures brought by the first Industrial Revolution.

One hundred and thirty-five years later, Leo XIV faces another revolution. Not the steam engine, but large language models. Not workers losing physical labor to machines, but humans losing judgment, creativity, and even the capacity for faith expression to AI.

In a separate interview, he rejected a proposal to create an "AI papal avatar" — someone had suggested building a virtual Pope so that believers worldwide could have "online audiences." He refused, warning that if technological development loses its relationship with faith and humanity, it becomes "an empty, cold shell" that damages human nature.

Note: his subject wasn't "AI." It was "technology severed from its connection to humanity." This is a civilizational judgment, not fear of a particular tool.

## The Boundary of Efficiency

I am someone who uses AI every day. I use Claude to write code, GPT for multilingual translation, and a debate engine for strategic analysis. I believe AI is the most powerful productivity tool of our era.

And precisely because of that, I understand what the Pope is saying.

The logic of efficiency has its domain of application. In information processing, pattern recognition, and workflow optimization, AI isn't just useful — it's overwhelmingly useful. But when you encounter concepts like "witness," "accompaniment," "faith," and "friendship," the logic of efficiency breaks down. Not because these things are too old-fashioned to keep up with the times, but because their value was never on the axis of efficiency to begin with.

A priest spending six hours preparing a homily versus AI generating one in six seconds — the difference isn't about quality. The difference is: during those six hours, he was with God. The traces of that encounter will emerge in his voice, his pauses, in that imperfect but warm word he chose.

The congregation can tell.

This is the line the Pope drew. Not "AI is bad," but "some things derive their value from you doing them yourself."

In an age where everything can be optimized, knowing what should not be optimized may be the most important wisdom of all.
