February 19, 2026, Paul VI Audience Hall, Vatican. Pope Leo XIV sat down for a forty-five-minute conversation with priests of the Roman diocese. Behind closed doors, no cameras, just dialogue between pastors.

A few days later, Vatican News released a report on the Pope's discussion of AI usage, sparking intense international media discussion.

The headlines were catchy, but most coverage stopped there. As if this were just another "old man doesn't understand technology" story.

That's not what this is.

## Muscles Atrophy When Not Used

The Pope's first argument was very down-to-earth.

He used an analogy: "The muscles of the body, if not used, if not moved, die. The brain also needs to be used, our intelligence must be exercised, so as not to lose this capacity."

This isn't about efficiency—it's about maintaining capability. Preparing a homily—reading scripture, repeatedly contemplating, seeking connections in prayer, transforming abstract theological concepts into language the congregation can understand—this process itself is a spiritual discipline. Skip this process, and you're not just saving time; you're skipping the training that makes you a pastor.

When I was training at seminary, I spent the most time not writing reports, but in the agony of "not understanding." Reading Barth, reading Moltmann, reading theologians who made you feel stupid, then stopping at the parts you couldn't understand and asking yourself: what is he really saying? Does this align with my faith experience?

That process of "not understanding," in retrospect, was the real learning. If ChatGPT had been available then, I probably would have just pasted it in and asked "summarize this for me," then quickly skipped ahead. Efficiency improved, but something in the soul would have been lost.

This is the "atrophy" the Pope speaks of.

## AI Cannot Share Human Faith

The second argument is more fundamental.

"True preaching is sharing faith," the Pope said, "and artificial intelligence can never share faith."

To fully feel the weight of this statement, you must understand the Catholic definition of "homily." In Catholic liturgy, a homily is not a "speech," not "knowledge transfer," not even "exhortation." It is the pastor standing before the congregation, based on his own experience of encounter with God, connecting the Word with present life.

The key phrase is "one's own experience."

A priest might have visited a dying parishioner that week, might have heard heartbreaking stories in the confessional, might have been suddenly struck by a passage of scripture during early morning prayer. These experiences constitute that unique voice when he steps up to the pulpit.

AI can analyze tens of thousands of homilies, find the best structures, most moving rhetoric, most suitable angles for the week's readings. It can even write a superb sermon that no one could fault. But what it cannot do is: bear witness.

To witness means: I have experienced it, so I can tell you. This is not information—this is existence. It is the pastor's physical suffering, spiritual struggle.

I work with AI every day now—writing articles, doing analysis, managing projects, even developing various service models and tools. But I'm very clear about one thing: AI helps me with information-level work, not meaning-level work. When I write an article about faith, what gives the article life is not something AI can give me. It's the path I've walked myself.

This is precisely the line the Pope is drawing.

## Don't Seek Likes on Social Media

In the same conversation, the Pope issued another warning: don't pursue likes and follower counts on platforms like TikTok. He called it a kind of "illusion of self-sacrifice on the internet."

These two things—using AI to write homilies and chasing likes on social media—seem unrelated on the surface, but share the same underlying logic: letting external metrics define the value of pastoral work.

AI helps you optimize quality indicators for your homily: complete structure, clear argumentation, precise wording. Social algorithms help you optimize influence indicators: reach, engagement rate, follower growth. But the Pope asks: "People want to see your faith, your experience of encounter with Jesus Christ." This cannot be measured by any indicator.

He asks priests to leave the church, to build real friendships with young people through sports, arts, and cultural activities. He emphasizes this requires "time and sacrifice," because many young people live in isolation, in the困境 of drugs, crime, and violence. True accompaniment is low-frequency, high-cost, and difficult to scale.

This completely violates Silicon Valley logic. But the Pope doesn't care.

## From Leo XIII to Leo XIV: Between Two Industrial Revolutions

There's a subtle context here.

In Leo XIV's first address to the College of Cardinals after his election, he said he chose this name to honor Leo XIII. Leo XIII issued the encyclical Rerum Novarum in 1891, the foundational document of Catholic social teaching, responding to the labor exploitation, wealth inequality, and social division brought by the First Industrial Revolution.

One hundred thirty-five years later, Leo XIV faces another revolution. Not steam engines, but large language models. Not workers replaced by machines for physical labor, but humans replaced by AI for judgment, creativity, even the capacity for faith expression.

In another interview, he refused a proposal to create an "AI Pope avatar"—someone suggested using AI to create a virtual Pope so believers worldwide could have "online audiences." He refused and warned: if technological development loses its relationship with faith and humanity, it becomes an "empty, cold shell" that damages human nature.

Notice, his subject isn't "AI," but "technology divorced from connection to humanity." This is a civilization-level judgment, not fear of a particular tool.

## The Boundaries of Efficiency

I'm someone who uses AI every day. I use AI to write code, do multilingual translation, gather information, run strategic analysis with debate engines. I believe AI is the most powerful productivity tool of this era.

But precisely because of this, I better understand what the Pope is saying.

Efficiency logic has its scope of application. In information processing, pattern recognition, and process optimization, AI isn't just useful—it's crushingly useful. But when you encounter concepts like "witness," "accompaniment," "faith," "friendship," "love," "loyalty," efficiency logic fails. Not because these things are too outdated to keep up with the times, but because their value was never in the dimension of efficiency.

A priest spending six hours preparing a homily versus AI generating one in six seconds—the difference isn't in quality. The difference is: in those six hours, he spent time with God. The trace of that time spent together will flow through his voice, his pauses, the imperfect but warm word he chooses.

The congregation can hear the difference.

This is the line the Pope draws. Not "AI is bad," but "some things derive their value from your doing them personally." Friction and suffering in life are sources of meaning.

In this age when everything can be optimized, knowing what shouldn't be optimized might be the most important wisdom.
