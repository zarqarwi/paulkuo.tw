Have you ever thought about this: when you're scrolling through Facebook or refreshing Instagram every day, your finger is actually doing one thing—voting.

Every pause, every like, every share is you telling the algorithm: "This has value for me." And the algorithm remembers your preferences, then feeds you more similar content. Day after day, your feed becomes more and more like a mirror, reflecting who you are.

But few people ever look back at that mirror.

## Four Forces That Make You Pause

I did some self-observation for a while and discovered that posts that genuinely make me stop and read carefully fall into roughly four categories.

**The first kind: high knowledge density.** After reading, you feel like your brain has been upgraded, as if someone has opened a new window for you. Maybe it's a perspective you've never considered, a set of data that lets you understand the world anew, or a clear explanation of a complex concept. This is humanity's yearning for "truth"—we are naturally driven to understand how things work.

**The second kind: sincere and moving.** The term "positive energy" has been overused, but real inspiration is not chicken soup. It's someone honestly writing down the process of getting knocked down and getting back up again. That kind of "breaking your bones but keeping your courage" story makes you feel a sudden surge to get up and do something by the time you scroll to the third line. This is humanity's resonance with "goodness."

**The third kind: pleasing to the eye.** Beautiful landscape photos, perfectly composed street photography, adorable animal videos that make the corners of your mouth curl up. No explanation needed, no analysis required—beauty is beauty. Your eyes automatically track it; this is a biological response written into our genes. This is humanity's pure appreciation of "beauty."

**The fourth kind: humorous and witty.** Letting you temporarily forget you're still struggling in spreadsheet hell during those few seconds of belly laughter. Good humor isn't just about being funny; it's a compression of wisdom—capturing the most common shared experiences in the shortest space.

Truth, goodness, beauty, plus humor. These four types of content are the hard currency of the social world.

## The Other End of the Spectrum

But social media isn't just these.

Open any social platform and you'll definitely see another extreme: every post, every photo, every piece of text screaming the same thing—"Look at me! I'm amazing!"

Boasting about check-ins, close-ups of designer brands, selfies from exclusive parties. The core of the content isn't sharing anything valuable; it's constructing a persona that "looks successful."

I'm not making a moral judgment. Everyone has a need to display themselves; this is normal human nature. But when someone's entire social output is self-aggrandizement—no knowledge, no emotional connection, no beauty, and no humor—they're not actually talking to the world. They're just talking to themselves in a mirror.

The problem isn't "is bragging bad?" The problem is: if your digital trace is nothing but self, then your social presence is just noise to others. And accounts that only produce noise long-term will eventually be abandoned by both algorithms and people alike.

## You're Not Just a Consumer

There's a role switch here that many people don't realize.

In the era of traditional media, you were a pure consumer. You turned on the TV or opened the newspaper and received content produced by others. You had no choice, and no production rights.

But social media has turned everyone into a producer. Every post you make, every story you share, every comment you leave is you "producing" content. And what you produce is your [digital footprint](/articles/digital-footprint-the-one)—it's more honest than your resume, more truthful than your self-introduction.

Because what you post on social media isn't the result of careful brand management (at least not for most people); it's a natural expression of your psychological state. An anxious person constantly shares anxious news. A fulfilled person shares what they're learning. Someone who only cares about appearances posts carefully edited vacation photos.

You think you're managing your social presence. Actually, social media is exposing you.

## Bees and Balloons

I later often used a metaphor to think about this.

Some people on social media are like bees. They fly around, collect pollen, then bring it back to the hive—sharing what they've learned, the beauty they've seen, and the emotions they've felt with the people who see their posts. Their posts are like drops of honey, nourishing everyone who scrolls past.

Some people are like balloons. They constantly inflate themselves, expanding and expanding and expanding again. They look big and eye-catching, but they're empty inside. And the more they expand, the further they get from the ground.

Do you want to be a bee or a balloon?

## A Small Observation at SDTI

During the years I ran SDTI, I observed something interesting.

The best-performing people in the team usually also had the highest quality social media content. Not because they deliberately managed their personal brand, but because their quality of thinking was high—someone who thinks deeply every day naturally has substance in what they casually share.

Conversely, if someone's social feed is all check-ins from meals and selfies, I wouldn't directly judge their abilities, but I'd be curious: where is their attention going? A day's time is fixed; the more time you spend displaying yourself, the less time you have to improve yourself. This is simple conservation of energy.

## What Trace Are You Leaving

I especially remember having a few friends whose Facebook profiles I would actively click into even if the algorithm didn't show them to me. Not because their posts were particularly flashy, but because every time I finished looking at them I felt recharged. They were like my "spiritual food supply station"—a glass of clean water in a pile of information garbage.

This made me start to reflect on myself. Is every post I send out honey or noise to the people who see it? Is it pollen or exhaust?

Social media won't disappear. Algorithms will only become more precise. In this age where everyone is a medium, you can't choose not to leave a trace—you can only choose what kind of trace to leave.

What kind of information do you like to consume? More importantly—what kind of information are you currently producing?

This is a question worth thinking carefully about tonight.
