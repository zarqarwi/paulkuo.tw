2027 is not far away.

Three years ago, ChatGPT didn't exist, and people were still debating when AI would become useful.

Three years later, those debates will become jokes. AI won't be future tense—"AI will change the world"—but present tense—"AI is changing the world, happening now."

At this inflection point, what I want to discuss is not AI's capabilities, but how we should treat it.

## Four Key Reflections

### 1. The Paradox of Transparency

When AI becomes everyday, it will make more decisions.

For example, banks use AI to decide your loan amount. Hospitals use AI to determine your treatment plan. Police use AI to decide who's likely to commit a crime. Companies use AI to decide who should be hired.

In all these cases, people want to know: Why? Why was my loan rejected? Why is this treatment recommended? Why was I flagged as high-risk?

AI cannot fully answer this question.

Deep learning models are inherently black boxes. They can tell you "this is my answer," but they cannot fully explain "why."

But human decision-makers can. A banker can say, "Your credit score is too low, your income is unstable." A doctor can say, "Based on your medical history and these test results, I recommend this treatment."

So we have a paradox: AI is trusted because it can handle big data, but it cannot explain its decisions. And human decisions can be questioned more easily, but at least they're transparent.

By 2027, we need to answer: In this paradox, what are we willing to sacrifice?

### 2. The Illusion of Fairness

AI advocates often say: AI is fairer than humans because it has no bias.

This is wrong.

AI doesn't have human biases, but it has data biases. If you train AI on historical data, and that data itself contains human biases, then AI will amplify that bias.

For example, if past hiring data shows that men were hired at higher rates, AI will learn this pattern and repeat it in future hiring.

Even more subtle: AI might discover biases you didn't even know existed. For instance, AI finds that "people who look like they often work in coffee shops are smarter"—this might be a statistical association in the training data, but if AI makes decisions based on it, it reinforces a bias we didn't even know existed.

By 2027, when AI has penetrated every decision, we'll start seeing the cumulative effects of these biases.

### 3. Concentration of Power

AI is not democratic.

AI development, training, and deployment all require vast resources. Only large companies and governments can afford to do these.

So when AI becomes everyday, power will concentrate in the hands of those who control AI.

Google, Amazon, Apple, Microsoft will know everything about you. Not because they want to control you, but because AI needs big data.

And if AI makes decisions, then these companies that control AI hold the power to decide.

By 2027, the consequences of this power concentration will become obvious. There might be new laws to limit AI, new social movements to oppose AI monopoly, or people might get used to this power structure.

But either way, this is a problem 2027 must face.

### 4. The Meaning of Being Human

Finally, the deepest question: If AI makes decisions, what use is humanity?

If AI can write code, design, diagnose, even judge, what value can humans offer?

This is not a technical question but a philosophical one.

One possible answer is: The value of humans lies in value judgment. AI can optimize, but it cannot decide what to optimize for. AI can solve problems, but it cannot decide what a problem is.

In the world of 2027, people need to redefine their role—not as a labor force, but as decision-makers, value setters.

But this requires changes in education, culture, even social structure. And these changes are harder than any technological progress.

## Conclusion: 2027 Is Not the End

2027 is not the end of AI adoption but the beginning.

The real challenge is not what AI can do, but what we allow AI to do, and how we ensure that AI's decisions are fair, transparent, and aligned with human values.

In the next three years, these four reflections—transparency, fairness, power, and human meaning—will become increasingly important.

If we start reflecting now, 2027 might be a better world.

If we wait until 2027 to reflect, it might be too late.

The question is not whether AI will change the world. It will.

The question is: What kind of change do we want?

The game is not over. We don't need to wait until 2027 to start thinking about this.
