## 40 Minutes to Complete 15 Man-Days of Work: What Can Man-Days Still Measure?

*If 40 minutes of deep thinking can create output equivalent to 15 traditional man-days—then are our ways of measuring work already obsolete?*

---

Three AIs running simultaneously. One handles strategic analysis, one writes code and deploys, one manages documentation and operational tasks. The operator spends about 40 minutes—issuing commands, reviewing output, making judgments, adjusting direction. When it's over, there's a market analysis report, a refactored database, and a literature review on the desk.

Traditional approach? Five people, three to four working days. 15 to 20 man-days.

But those 40 minutes weren't idle time—they were high-density cognitive labor: decomposing vague objectives, orchestrating AI division of labor, real-time error correction, integrating scattered outputs, until results took shape.

So what exactly do "man-days" measure here? They measure time, not value. When measurement methods lag behind reality, we're measuring not productivity, but merely presence (attendance).

[Anthropic's Economic Index released in January 2026](https://www.anthropic.com/research) provides empirical foundation. They analyzed over a million Claude conversations and found that prompt quality correlates 0.92 with output quality. Tasks requiring university-level comprehension see 12x AI acceleration; high school level only gets 9x.

AI doesn't uniformly strengthen everyone. It exponentially amplifies capability gaps.

Two people's resumes both say "familiar with AI tools," but behind that phrase lie vastly different capabilities.
One person can ship a full-stack application over the weekend with AI, while another spends eight hours using ChatGPT to revise an email.
The problem isn't just the capability gap itself, but that until now, we still lack a way to identify, measure, or even name this gap.

## From The Mythical Man-Month to AI Collaboration: Why Attention Duration Isn't Enough Either?

In 1975, computer scientist [Fred Brooks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fred_Brooks) published [*The Mythical Man-Month*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Mythical_Man-Month), with the most classic insight being the debunking of the "man-month" myth: headcount and time were never interchangeable variables. Adding each person not only increases capacity but also simultaneously increases costs of communication, coordination, and integration, and these costs can devour the efficiency gains originally imagined. So when a project is already behind, adding more people usually isn't a remedy—it makes it slower.

Brooks was talking about human teams. But the AI era validates his point from the opposite direction: AI has reduced communication costs to zero. No onboarding needed, no alignment meetings, 24/7 availability, zero context switch costs. When coordination costs approach zero, parallel execution becomes truly efficient for the first time—but measurement systems built on the assumption that "coordination is expensive" simultaneously lose their meaning.

Some have tried to replace man-days with "attention duration." This concept divides human cognitive investment in AI collaboration into four categories:
• Initiation attention: translating vague requirements into precise instructions;
• Supervision attention: checking AI output and real-time error correction;
• Integration attention: coordinating scattered outputs from multiple AIs;
• Incubation attention: letting unconscious background thinking settle and catalyze insights.

This categorization is helpful, but "attention duration" is essentially still an input-side metric. It tells us how much cognitive resources were invested, but doesn't tell us what those resources actually produced, what the quality was, or how much value was created compared to traditional approaches.

The current problem is: the input side has "attention duration," the macro side has the [Anthropic Economic Index](https://www.anthropic.com/research), estimating AI's contribution to US labor productivity annual growth at about 1.0 percentage point; but at the individual level output side—the level where hiring decisions happen, where freelancers are evaluated—it's still almost a complete void.

The measurement vacuum isn't at the periphery, but right at the center.

## Global AI Capability Framework Survey: Why Is No One Testing Individual Output?

This vacuum isn't because no one cares. Over the past two years, governments, international organizations, and academia worldwide have produced numerous AI capability frameworks. But they're all solving the same problem: can you use AI? None are asking: what have you created with AI?

The [AI Fluency Framework](https://www.anthropic.com/research) developed by Anthropic in collaboration with Rick Dakan and Joseph Feller defines four core capabilities (4D): Delegation, Description, Discernment, and Diligence. This is probably the most mature capability model currently available. But it describes "what good AI collaboration looks like," not quantifying "what it produced."

Anthropic's own Economic Index approaches from another angle, analyzing millions of real conversations to estimate AI's impact on labor productivity. The data is extremely valuable—but it operates at the statistical aggregate level, not the individual portfolio level.

The [US Department of Labor](https://www.dol.gov/) released an AI Literacy Framework, the [UK Turing Institute](https://www.turing.ac.uk/) launched the third version of "AI Skills for Business Framework," [UNESCO](https://www.unesco.org/) has AI capability frameworks for students and teachers, and academia has proposed scales like Collaborative AI Literacy and Collaborative AI Metacognition.

There seem to be many frameworks, but they mostly answer the same type of questions: do you understand AI, can you use it reasonably, do you know its limitations? In other words, they test literacy.

What's truly missing is performance-level measurement: what have you actually produced with AI, what's the quality of the results, how much efficiency and value have you gained compared to traditional methods?

Put existing frameworks on a 2×2 matrix—horizontal axis "literacy vs performance," vertical axis "individual vs macro"—and you'll see the left side crowded with people (UNESCO, DOL, Turing, various academic scales), the upper right corner has the [Anthropic Economic Index](https://www.anthropic.com/research). The lower right corner—individual level, performance-based, verifiable—is empty.

Everyone is testing AI literacy. No one is testing individual AI productivity. This article proposes a framework to fill this gap. It's on the same thread as my previous article on [AI Era Capability Gaps](/articles/ai-capability-gap-2026)—that one discussed the existence of the gap, this one discusses how to quantify it.

## AI Collaboration Portfolio Five-Dimensional Model: What to Measure, How to Measure

AI Collaboration Portfolio is a five-dimensional model that measures what individuals actually produce through AI collaboration. Design principles: each dimension must have theoretical foundation, quantifiable indicators, and verifiable data sources from third parties.

### Command (25%)

Can you get AI to do the right things?

Corresponds to Delegation and Description in the 4D Framework, and "initiation attention" in attention theory. Anthropic found 0.92 correlation between prompt quality and output quality—this may be the single most critical variable in AI collaboration effectiveness.

Quantifiable indicators: number of reusable skills/workflows/system prompts, number of automation pipelines (CI/CD, cron, GitHub Actions), breadth of AI tool integration, complexity of multi-step task decomposition.

### Delivery (25%)

After AI collaboration, what did you actually ship?

Not what you tried, but what went live. Corresponds to task completion rate in the Economic Index.

Quantifiable indicators: commit frequency and volume, number of deployed services/tools, published content count, complete zero-to-live projects, code quality metrics (PR merge rate, issue resolution speed).

### Leverage (20%)

With the same cognitive investment, how much amplification did you achieve?

This is the core of "40 minutes vs. 15 man-days." Directly answers the question enterprises care about most: when you join, what's the team's capacity multiplier?

Quantifiable indicators: actual cognitive investment vs traditional estimation ratio, number of parallel projects, number of AI tools orchestrated, automation coverage rate.

### Quality (15%)

Can your output withstand scrutiny?

In an era when AI can mass-generate content, quality gatekeepers are unprecedentedly important. Corresponds to Discernment in the 4D Framework and reliability adjustments in the Economic Index—they found that factoring in task success rates reduces productivity improvement estimates by about one-third.

Quantifiable indicators: user/traffic numbers, system uptime, number of quality control mechanisms (automated testing, verification SOPs, review processes), external citations/shares.

### Influence (15%)

Are your methods being learned or adopted by others?

The transition from individual contributor to organizational enabler. The highest leverage form of value creation.

Quantifiable indicators: open source project interactions (stars, forks, contributors), skill/template adoption count, teaching content reach, external citations of methodologies.

### Relationships Between Dimensions

To be honest: these five dimensions aren't completely independent. High Quality often precedes Influence. A powerful automation pipeline (Leverage) is itself a form of Delivery. Causal relationships exist between dimensions, and precise attribution can sometimes be blurry.

This is a design feature, not a flaw. Real value creation is inherently multi-dimensionally intertwined. Revenue, gross profit, and net profit in financial statements also have causal relationships, but we don't just look at one number because of this—each number reveals different aspects. Five dimensions are five observation lenses, not five orthogonal axes. The goal is richer observation, not perfect decomposition.

## Anti-Fraud Mechanism: Automatic Capture, Structured Self-Assessment, AI Verification

The most common challenge to any capability framework is: can it be gamed?

This concern is completely valid. Goodhart's Law—when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure—applies to all quantification systems. The question isn't whether it can be gamed, but whether the cost of gaming is high enough.

AI Collaboration Portfolio responds with a three-layer evidence architecture:

**Layer 1: Automatic capture.** After user authorization, the system directly pulls data from third-party platforms—GitHub commit records, repo structure, CI/CD workflow files, website analytics, package download counts, community metrics. This data is recorded by independent platforms and cannot be tampered with by users. However many commits you have on GitHub is however many you have. This layer is the "hard evidence" baseline of the Portfolio.

**Layer 2: Structured self-assessment.** Parts that cannot be automatically captured (number of parallel projects, traditional man-day estimates, AI tool integration methods) use structured forms to guide input. Each self-assessment field has an adjacent "evidence link" field. Items with links are marked as *Evidenced*, those without are marked as *Self-reported*. Anyone viewing your Portfolio can see this marking and judge credibility themselves.

**Layer 3: AI verification.** After users authorize GitHub, AI analyzes repo structure and code patterns to independently suggest scores for each dimension. AI suggested scores are displayed alongside user self-assessments. If you self-assess 90 points but AI suggests 50 points, this gap is visually flagged.

All three layers work together: automatic data cannot be faked, self-assessments have transparent evidence marking, AI provides independent reference. This doesn't eliminate gaming—it raises the cost of gaming to unprofitable levels.

More critically, the framework's ultimate defense isn't internal validation but external reality. Quality and Influence dimensions require evidence from outside users' control—user adoption rates, community interactions, customer feedback, market results. Teams can collude to game Jira ticket counts internally, but can't force the market to buy, nor fake genuine open source community adoption.

This framework isn't an automatic judge. It's a forensic accounting system—an auditable ledger that shifts evaluation from "who tells the best story" to "whose evidence chain withstands questioning."

## Six Strongest Challenges and Responses: From Evidence Forgery to Survivorship Bias

We put this framework through adversarial debate engines with multiple models, running three rounds of stress testing. Here are the six most powerful attacks and our responses.

**Q1: AI can help people forge evidence chains.**

AI can forge maps but cannot forge memories of walking that path. In framework-guided deep interviews, interviewers probe not "what did you do" but "how did you make decisions"—why abandon that approach? How did you consider token consumption trade-offs? How did you handle model hallucinations? People who actually did the work can answer three layers of follow-up questions. Those following scripts collapse at the third layer.

**Q2: Leverage's baseline (man-days) is something you yourself said is obsolete—using it as denominator is circular reasoning.**

Leverage shouldn't be interpreted as a static multiplier against a fixed baseline. Its core value is the rate of change over time. An engineer achieving 10x leverage with GPT-4 in 2024 and still 10x with Claude Opus 4.6 in 2026—this itself reveals adaptation stagnation. The framework measures growth slope, not absolute values. AI tools are evolving, baselines are shifting (Red Queen effect), what truly predicts is your speed of adapting to each paradigm shift.

**Q3: Why not add "Adaptability" and "Ethics" dimensions?**

Adaptability isn't an independent dimension—it's the derivative of five dimensions over time. A person whose Command, Delivery, Leverage scores continuously improve across AI paradigm shifts demonstrates high adaptability. The same logic applies to "reflection loops"—the meta-skill of using AI to analyze and optimize one's own work patterns. It manifests as the upward trajectory of five dimensions over time, not a static score in a sixth column. Ethics is a baseline constraint, not a performance indicator—ethical violations should result in direct disqualification, not a 15-point deduction.

**Q4: Dimensions have causal relationships and cannot be precisely attributed.**

Acknowledged. See "Relationships Between Dimensions" above. The framework pursues richer observation, not mathematical orthogonality. Where attribution is ambiguous, the three-layer evidence architecture provides raw data for evaluators to judge themselves—this is precisely the framework's design intent: it doesn't replace human judgment, it gives human judgment better materials.

**Q5: The framework focuses on individuals, but the highest-value contributions in the AI era are often shared cognitive assets.**

This is a real limitation of v1. Someone who builds a prompt library for the entire department to use, designs a workflow that doubles everyone's efficiency—this network effect indeed cannot be fully captured by individual dimensions. The Influence dimension partially touches on this, but not enough. Team-version framework—measuring how one person amplifies system capacity not just their own—is the most important evolution direction for v2.

**Q6: The author using himself as a case study is survivorship bias.**

Methodologically completely valid. A framework designer naturally cherry-picks evidence favorable to themselves. The response isn't to deny bias but to make bias structurally unimportant: every claim in the case study below can be independently verified through public URLs, GitHub repos, or third-party analytics APIs. This case study's value isn't statistical—it's engineering. It provides a complete, inspectable, replicable blueprint that anyone can run with their own data. Think of it as open source release, not clinical trial. Initial versions inevitably reflect creator context, but their value depends on whether the community finds them worth forking, attacking, improving.

## The Cost of Not Measuring AI Productivity: Capability Gaps Are Invisibly Widening

Why does this matter? Because the cost of not measuring is greater than measuring wrong.

The [Anthropic Economic Index](https://www.anthropic.com/research) documents a deskilling effect: AI preferentially takes over high-skill components of work, leaving low-skill parts behind. If we cannot identify who truly excels at AI collaboration, high-capability individuals will be systematically undervalued, while those who interview well but produce poorly will be overvalued.

Productivity gaps are real and widening. University-level tasks are accelerated 12x by AI, high school level only 9x. This isn't a small gap that accumulates slowly over time—this is structural divergence. And currently completely invisible to labor markets because no measurement system can reveal it.

Regarding this topic "How should productivity be calculated in the AI era?" using multi-model cross-validation, several honest limitations became clearer:

**Individual vs System Value.** The framework measures individual output, but the most valuable contributions in the AI era are often shared cognitive assets—prompt libraries used by entire departments, automation workflows that accelerate everyone. Measuring how one person amplifies system capacity—not just their own—is the most important extension direction for v2.

**Reflection Loops.** The highest-order skill in AI collaboration may be using AI to analyze and optimize one's own work patterns—a metacognitive ability that drives growth in all dimensions. Current framework captures this indirectly through score trajectories over time, but doesn't explicitly measure it. Future versions could treat self-iteration speed as a first-class signal.

**Cross-organizational Portability.** AI collaboration occurs in vastly different cultural, legal, economic contexts. Collectivist cultures might systematically suppress self-assessment scores. GDPR restricts automatic data capture. Small organizations cannot afford AI verification infrastructure. A framework only usable in well-resourced English-speaking tech companies isn't truly universal.

**Fairness and Accessibility.** People with access to latest AI tools naturally have advantages in this framework. If the measurement system itself amplifies digital divides rather than revealing capabilities, it fails.

These aren't flaws to hide. They're research directions for the next iteration.

But the alternative to an imperfect framework isn't a perfect framework—it's no framework at all. It's continuing to evaluate AI collaboration capabilities through resume keywords and interview impressions, in a world where "familiar with AI tools" and "can use AI to do an entire team's work alone" differ by an order of magnitude.

What gets measured gets valued. What doesn't gets invisible.

AI Collaboration Portfolio is a first attempt to make the invisible visible. It's an imperfect map. But the alternative isn't a better map—it's running blindfolded in a new continent.
