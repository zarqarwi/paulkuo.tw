## 40 Minutes to Complete 15 Person-Days of Work: What Can Person-Days Still Measure?

*If 40 minutes of deep thinking can create output equivalent to traditional 15 person-days of work—then are our ways of measuring work already obsolete?*

---

Three AIs running simultaneously. One handles strategic analysis, another writes code and deploys, the third manages documentation and operational tasks. The operator spends about 40 minutes—issuing commands, reviewing outputs, making decisions, adjusting direction. When it's over, there's a market analysis report on the desk, a refactored database, and a literature review.

The traditional approach? Five people, three to four working days. 15 to 20 person-days.

But those 40 minutes weren't idle time—they were high-density cognitive labor: decomposing vague objectives, orchestrating AI division of labor, correcting errors in real-time, integrating scattered outputs until results took shape.

So what exactly do "person-days" measure here? They measure time, not value. When measurement methods lag behind reality, we're not measuring productivity—just whether people showed up (attendance).

The [Anthropic Economic Index released in January 2026](https://www.anthropic.com/research) provides empirical foundation. They analyzed over one million Claude conversations and found that prompt quality correlates with output quality at 0.92. Tasks requiring college-level comprehension show 12x AI acceleration; high school level tasks only 9x.

AI doesn't uniformly strengthen everyone. It exponentially amplifies capability gaps.

Two résumés both list "familiar with AI tools," but behind that phrase could be vastly different capability levels.
One person can use AI to ship a full-stack application over a weekend, while another spends eight hours using ChatGPT to revise an email.
The problem isn't just the capability gap itself, but that until now, we still lack a framework to recognize, measure, or even name this gap.

## From The Mythical Man-Month to AI Collaboration: Why Attention Duration Isn't Enough Either

In 1975, computer scientist [Fred Brooks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fred_Brooks) published [*The Mythical Man-Month*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Mythical_Man-Month). His core insight: man-months are fictional. People and time aren't interchangeable because communication costs eat up efficiency gains. Adding people to a late project only makes it later.

Brooks was talking about human teams. But the AI era validates his thesis from the opposite direction: AI has zeroed out communication costs. No onboarding needed, no alignment meetings, 24/7 operation, zero context switching costs. When coordination costs approach zero, parallel execution becomes truly efficient for the first time—but measurement systems built on the assumption that "coordination is expensive" simultaneously lose their meaning.

Some have tried using "attention duration" to replace person-days. This concept divides human cognitive investment in AI collaboration into four categories: initiation attention (converting vague requirements into precise instructions), monitoring attention (checking AI output and course-correcting), integration attention (coordinating across multiple AI outputs), and incubation attention (unconscious background processing that generates insights).

The categorization is useful, but attention duration remains an input-side metric. It tells us how much cognitive resources you spent, not what those resources produced, the quality level, or how much value was created relative to traditional methods.

The current situation is this: we have attention duration on the input side, Anthropic Economic Index on the macro side (estimating AI's annual contribution to US labor productivity at approximately 1.0 percentage points), but the individual-level output side—the level where hiring decisions are made, where freelancers are evaluated—is completely blank.

The measurement vacuum isn't at the periphery, it's at the center.

## Global AI Capability Framework Survey: Why Is No One Testing Individual Output?

This vacuum isn't because no one cares. Over the past two years, governments worldwide, international organizations, and academia have produced numerous AI capability frameworks. But they're all solving the same problem: Do you know how to use AI? None are asking: What did you create with AI?

Anthropic's [AI Fluency Framework](https://www.anthropic.com/research), developed with Rick Dakan and Joseph Feller, defines four core capabilities (4D): Delegation, Description, Discernment, and Diligence. This is probably the most mature capability model currently available. But it describes "what good AI collaboration looks like," not quantifying "what it produced."

Anthropic's own Economic Index approaches from another angle, analyzing millions of real conversations to estimate AI's impact on labor productivity. The data is extremely valuable—but it operates at the statistical aggregate level, not the individual portfolio level.

The [US Department of Labor released an AI Literacy Framework in February 2026](https://www.dol.gov/). The [UK's Turing Institute](https://www.turing.ac.uk/) published the third version of its AI Skills for Business Framework in late 2025. [UNESCO](https://www.unesco.org/) produced AI capability frameworks for students and teachers. Academia has scales for Collaborative AI Literacy and Collaborative AI Metacognition.

Their common characteristic: they test literacy (whether you understand AI, can use it appropriately, know its limitations), not performance (what you actually produced with AI, its quality, how much more efficient it is than traditional methods).

If you place existing frameworks on a 2×2 matrix—horizontal axis "Literacy vs Performance," vertical axis "Individual vs Macro"—you'll see the left side crowded with people (UNESCO, DOL, Turing, various academic scales), the upper right corner has Anthropic Economic Index. The lower right corner—individual level, performance-based, verifiable—is empty.

Everyone is testing AI literacy. No one is testing individual AI productivity. This article proposes a framework to fill that gap. It's on the same thread as my previous piece on [AI-era capability gaps](/articles/ai-capability-gap-2026)—that piece discussed the existence of gaps, this one discusses how to quantify them.

## AI Collaboration Portfolio Five-Dimensional Model: What to Measure and How

AI Collaboration Portfolio is a five-dimensional model measuring what individuals actually produce through AI collaboration. Design principle: each dimension must have theoretical foundation, quantifiable metrics, and verifiable data sources from third parties.

### Command (25%)

Can you make AI do the right things?

Corresponds to Delegation and Description in the 4D Framework, and "initiation attention" in attention theory. Anthropic found 0.92 correlation between prompt quality and output quality—this might be the most critical single variable in AI collaboration effectiveness.

Quantifiable metrics: number of reusable skills/workflows/system prompts, automation pipeline count (CI/CD, cron, GitHub Actions), breadth of AI tool integration, complexity of multi-step task decomposition.

### Delivery (25%)

After AI collaboration, what did you actually ship?

Not what you tried, but what went live. Corresponds to task completion rates in the Economic Index.

Quantifiable metrics: commit frequency and volume, number of deployed services/tools, published content count, complete zero-to-production projects, code quality indicators (PR merge rates, issue resolution speed).

### Leverage (20%)

How many times did you amplify the same cognitive investment?

This is the core of "40 minutes vs. 15 person-days." Directly answers the question enterprises care most about: What's the team's capacity multiplier after you join?

Quantifiable metrics: actual cognitive investment vs traditional estimation ratio, number of concurrent projects, AI tool orchestration count, automation coverage rate.

### Quality (15%)

Can your output withstand scrutiny?

In an era when AI can generate content at scale, quality gatekeepers are unprecedentedly important. Corresponds to Discernment in the 4D Framework and reliability adjustments in the Economic Index—they found that factoring in task success rates reduces productivity improvement estimates by about one-third.

Quantifiable metrics: user/traffic numbers, system uptime, quality control mechanism count (automated testing, verification SOPs, review processes), external citations/shares.

### Influence (15%)

Are your methods being learned or adopted by others?

The transition from individual contributor to organizational enabler. The highest-leverage form of value creation.

Quantifiable metrics: open source project interactions (stars, forks, contributors), skill/template adoption rates, educational content reach, external methodology citations.

### Relationships Between Dimensions

Honestly: these five dimensions aren't completely independent. High Quality is often a precursor to Influence. A powerful automation pipeline (Leverage) is itself a form of Delivery. Causal relationships exist between dimensions, and precise attribution can sometimes be ambiguous.

This is a design feature, not a flaw. Real value creation is inherently multi-dimensionally interwoven. Revenue, gross profit, and net profit in financial statements also have causal relationships, but we don't just look at one number because each reveals different facets. The five dimensions are five observation lenses, not five orthogonal axes. The goal is richer observation, not perfect decomposition.

## Anti-Fraud Mechanisms: Automatic Extraction, Structured Self-Assessment, AI Verification

The most common challenge to any capability framework is: Can it be gamed?

This concern is completely valid. Goodhart's Law—when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure—applies to all quantification systems. The question isn't whether it can be gamed, but whether the cost of gaming is high enough.

AI Collaboration Portfolio responds with a three-layer evidence architecture:

**Layer 1: Automatic extraction.** After user authorization, the system directly pulls data from third-party platforms—GitHub commit records, repo structure, CI/CD workflow files, website analytics, package download counts, community metrics. This data is recorded by independent platforms and cannot be tampered with by users. The number of commits on your GitHub is what it is. This layer is the "hard evidence" baseline of the Portfolio.

**Layer 2: Structured self-assessment.** For parts that cannot be automatically extracted (concurrent project count, traditional person-day estimates, AI tool integration methods), structured forms guide completion. Each self-assessment field has an accompanying "evidence link" field. Those with attached links are marked as *Evidenced*, those without as *Self-reported*. Anyone viewing your Portfolio can see this marking and judge credibility independently.

**Layer 3: AI verification.** After users authorize GitHub access, AI analyzes repo structure and code patterns to independently suggest scores for each dimension. AI suggested scores are displayed alongside user self-assessments. If you self-assess 90 points but AI suggests 50, this gap is visually flagged.

The three layers work together: automatic data can't be faked, self-assessments have transparent evidence marking, AI provides independent reference. This doesn't eliminate gaming, but raises the cost of gaming to unprofitable levels.

More critically, the framework's ultimate defense isn't internal validation, but external reality. Quality and Influence dimensions require evidence from beyond user control—adoption rates, community interaction, customer feedback, market results. Teams can collude to inflate Jira ticket counts, but can't force markets to buy, nor fake genuine adoption by open source communities.

This framework isn't an automatic judge. It's a forensic accounting system—an auditable ledger that shifts evaluation from "who tells the best story" to "whose evidence chain withstands questioning."

## Six Strongest Challenges and Responses: From Evidence Forgery to Survivor Bias

We put this framework through a multi-model adversarial debate engine for three rounds of stress testing. Here are the six most powerful attacks and our responses.

**Q1: AI can help people forge evidence chains.**

AI can forge maps, but can't forge the memory of walking that path. In framework-guided deep interviews, interviewers ask not "what did you do" but "how did you make decisions"—why abandon that approach? How did you consider token consumption tradeoffs? How did you handle model hallucinations? People who actually did the work can answer three layers of follow-up questions. Those following scripts collapse at the third layer.

**Q2: Leverage's baseline (person-days) is something you said was obsolete, using it as a denominator is circular reasoning.**

Leverage shouldn't be interpreted as a static multiplier against a fixed baseline. Its core value is the rate of change over time. If an engineer achieved 10x leverage with GPT-4 in 2024 and still only 10x with Claude Opus 4.6 in 2026—that itself exposes stagnation in adaptability. The framework tests growth slope, not absolute values. AI tools are evolving, baselines are shifting (Red Queen Effect), what truly has predictive power is your speed of adaptation to each paradigm shift.

**Q3: Why not add "Adaptability" and "Ethics" dimensions?**

Adaptability isn't an independent dimension, it's the derivative of the five dimensions with respect to time. Someone whose Command, Delivery, and Leverage scores consistently improve across AI paradigm shifts demonstrates high adaptability. The same logic applies to "reflection loops"—the meta-skill of using AI to analyze and optimize one's own work patterns. It manifests as the upward trajectory of the five dimensions over time, not a static score in a sixth column. Ethics is a baseline constraint, not a performance indicator—ethical violations should result in immediate disqualification, not a 15-point deduction.

**Q4: Causal relationships exist between dimensions, precise attribution is impossible.**

Acknowledged. See "Relationships Between Dimensions" above. The framework pursues richer observation, not mathematical orthogonality. Where attribution is ambiguous, the three-layer evidence architecture provides raw data for evaluators to judge independently—this is precisely the framework's design intent: it doesn't replace human judgment, it gives human judgment better materials.

**Q5: The framework focuses on individuals, but the highest-value contributions in the AI era are often shared cognitive assets.**

This is a real limitation of v1. When someone builds a prompt library that an entire department can use, or designs a workflow that doubles everyone's efficiency—this network effect truly cannot be fully captured by individual dimensions. The Influence dimension partially touches on this, but not enough. A team version framework—measuring how someone amplifies system capacity, not just their own—is the most important evolutionary direction for v2.

**Q6: Using himself as a case study is survivor bias.**

Methodologically completely valid. A framework's designer will naturally select favorable evidence. The response isn't to deny bias, but to make bias structurally irrelevant: every claim in the case study below can be independently verified through public URLs, GitHub repos, or third-party analytics APIs. This case study's value isn't statistical—it's engineering. It provides a complete, inspectable, replicable blueprint that anyone can run with their own data. Think of it as an open source release, not a clinical trial. Initial versions inevitably reflect the creator's context, but their value depends on whether the community finds them worth forking, attacking, and improving.

## The Cost of Not Measuring AI Productivity: Capability Gaps Are Expanding Invisibly

Why does this matter? Because the cost of not measuring is greater than measuring incorrectly.

The Anthropic Economic Index recorded a deskilling effect: AI preferentially takes over high-skill components of work, leaving behind low-skill parts. If we can't identify who truly excels at AI collaboration, high-capability individuals will be systematically undervalued, while those who interview well but produce poorly will be overvalued.

Productivity gaps are real and expanding. University-level tasks are accelerated 12x by AI, high school level only 9x. This isn't a small gap that accumulates slowly over time—this is structural divergence. And it's currently completely invisible to labor markets because no measurement system can reveal it.

Regarding this topic "In the AI era, how should productivity be calculated?", using multi-model cross-validation, several honest limitations became clearer:

**Individual vs System Value.** The framework measures individual output, but the most valuable contributions in the AI era are often shared cognitive assets—prompt libraries used by entire departments, automation workflows that accelerate everyone. Measuring how someone amplifies system capacity—not just their own—is the most important extension direction for v2.

**Reflection Loops.** The highest-level skill in AI collaboration might be using AI to analyze and optimize one's own work patterns—a meta-cognitive capability that drives growth in all dimensions. The current framework captures this indirectly through score trajectories over time, but doesn't explicitly measure it. Future versions could treat self-iteration speed as a first-class signal.

**Cross-organizational Portability.** AI collaboration occurs in extremely different cultural, legal, and economic contexts. Collectivist cultures might systematically suppress self-assessment scores. GDPR restricts automatic data extraction. Small organizations can't afford AI verification infrastructure. A framework that only works in resource-rich, English-speaking tech companies isn't truly universal.

**Fairness and Accessibility.** People with access to the latest AI tools naturally have advantages in this framework. If the measurement system itself amplifies digital divides rather than revealing capabilities, it fails.

These aren't flaws to hide. They're research directions for the next iteration.

But the alternative to an imperfect framework isn't a perfect framework—it's having no framework at all. It's continuing to evaluate AI collaboration capability through résumé keywords and interview impressions, in a world where "familiar with AI tools" and "can use AI to do an entire team's work alone" differ by an order of magnitude.

What can be quantified can be valued. What cannot becomes invisible.

AI Collaboration Portfolio is a first attempt to make the invisible visible. It's an imperfect map. But the alternative isn't a better map—it's running blindfolded through a new continent.
