## 40 Minutes Completing 15 Person-Days of Work: What Can Person-Days Still Measure?

*If 40 minutes of deep thinking can create output equivalent to traditional 15 person-days—then are our ways of measuring work already obsolete?*

---

Three AIs running simultaneously. One handling strategic analysis, one writing code and deploying, one processing documents and operational tasks. The operator spent about 40 minutes—giving commands, reviewing output, making judgments, adjusting directions. When finished, there was a market analysis report, a refactored database, and a literature review on the desk.

Using traditional methods? Five people, three to four working days. 15 to 20 person-days.

But those 40 minutes weren't idle time—they were high-density cognitive labor: decomposing vague goals, orchestrating AI division of labor, correcting errors in real-time, integrating scattered outputs until results took shape.

So what exactly did "person-days" measure here? It measured time, not value. When measurement methods lag behind reality, what we measure isn't productivity—just whether people showed up (attendance).

[Anthropic's Economic Index released in January 2026](https://www.anthropic.com/research) provides empirical foundation. They analyzed over one million Claude conversations and found prompt quality correlates with output quality at 0.92. Tasks requiring college-level comprehension see 12x AI acceleration; high school level only 9x.

AI doesn't uniformly make everyone stronger. It exponentially amplifies capability gaps.

Two people's resumes both say "familiar with AI tools," but behind that phrase may lie vastly different capability gaps.
One person can ship a full-stack application using AI over a weekend, while another spends eight hours using ChatGPT to revise an email.
The problem isn't just the capability gap itself, but that until now, we still lack a way to recognize, measure, or even name this gap.

## From The Mythical Man-Month to AI Collaboration: Why Attention Duration Isn't Enough Either?

In 1975, computer scientist [Fred Brooks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fred_Brooks) published [*The Mythical Man-Month*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Mythical_Man-Month), with its most classic insight debunking the "man-month" myth: people and time were never interchangeable variables. Each additional person not only increases capacity but simultaneously increases communication, coordination, and integration costs—costs sufficient to consume the originally imagined efficiency gains. So when a project is already behind, adding more people usually isn't a remedy—it makes things slower.

Brooks was talking about human teams. But the AI era validates his argument from the opposite direction: AI has reduced communication costs to zero. No onboarding needed, no alignment meetings, 24/7 work, zero context switch costs. When coordination costs approach zero, parallel execution becomes truly efficient for the first time—but measurement systems built on the assumption that "coordination is expensive" simultaneously lose meaning.

Some try using "attention duration" to replace person-days. This concept divides human cognitive input in AI collaboration into four categories: startup attention (converting vague requirements into precise instructions), supervisory attention (checking AI output and correcting course), integration attention (coordinating across multiple AI outputs), and incubation attention (unconscious background thinking generating insights).

The categorization is useful, but attention duration remains an input-side metric. It tells us how much cognitive resources you spent, not what those resources produced, the quality level, or how much value was created relative to traditional methods.

The current situation is this: input-side has attention duration, macro-side has the Anthropic Economic Index (estimating AI contributes approximately 1.0 percentage point annual increase to US labor productivity), but the individual-level output side—the level where hiring decisions are made, where freelancers are evaluated—is completely blank.

The measurement vacuum isn't at the margins—it's at the center.

## Global AI Competency Framework Survey: Why Is No One Testing Individual Output?

This vacuum isn't because no one cares. Over the past two years, governments, international organizations, and academia have produced numerous AI competency frameworks. But they're all solving the same problem: Do you know how to use AI? None are asking: What have you accomplished using AI?

Anthropic's [AI Fluency Framework](https://www.anthropic.com/research), developed with Rick Dakan and Joseph Feller, defines four core competencies (4D): Delegation, Description, Discernment, and Diligence. This is probably the most mature capability model currently available. But it describes "what good AI collaboration looks like," not quantifying "what it produced."

Anthropic's own Economic Index approaches from another angle, analyzing millions of real conversations to estimate AI's impact on labor productivity. The data is extremely valuable—but it operates at the statistical aggregate level, not the individual portfolio level.

The [US Department of Labor released an AI Literacy Framework in February 2026](https://www.dol.gov/). The [UK Turing Institute](https://www.turing.ac.uk/) released the third version of AI Skills for Business Framework in late 2025. [UNESCO](https://www.unesco.org/) published AI competency frameworks for students and teachers. Academia has Collaborative AI Literacy and Collaborative AI Metacognition scales.

Their common characteristic: testing literacy (do you understand AI, can you use it appropriately, do you know its limitations), not performance (what have you actually produced using AI, what quality, how much more efficient compared to traditional methods).

Put existing frameworks on a 2×2 matrix—horizontal axis "Literacy vs Performance," vertical axis "Individual vs Macro"—you'll see the left side crowded with people (UNESCO, DOL, Turing, various academic scales), upper right has Anthropic Economic Index. Lower right—individual level, performance-based, verifiable—is empty.

Everyone is testing AI literacy. No one is testing individual AI productivity. This article proposes a framework to fill that gap. It's on the same thread as my previous article on [AI Era Capability Gaps](/articles/ai-capability-gap-2026)—that piece discussed the existence of gaps, this one discusses how to quantify them.

## AI Collaboration Portfolio Five-Dimensional Model: What to Measure, How to Measure

AI Collaboration Portfolio is a five-dimensional model measuring what individuals actually produce through AI collaboration. Design principles: each dimension must have theoretical foundation, quantifiable metrics, and verifiable data sources from third parties.

### Command (25%)

Can you make AI do the right things?

Corresponds to Delegation and Description in the 4D Framework, and "startup attention" in attention theory. Anthropic found prompt quality correlates with output quality at 0.92—this may be the single most critical variable in AI collaboration effectiveness.

Quantifiable metrics: number of reusable skills/workflows/system prompts, number of automation pipelines (CI/CD, cron, GitHub Actions), AI tool integration breadth, complexity of multi-step task decomposition.

### Delivery (25%)

After AI collaboration, what did you actually ship?

Not what you tried, but what went live. Corresponds to task completion rates in the Economic Index.

Quantifiable metrics: commit frequency and volume, number of deployed services/tools, published content count, complete zero-to-production projects, code quality indicators (PR merge rate, issue resolution speed).

### Leverage (20%)

With the same cognitive input, how much amplification was achieved?

This is the core of "40 minutes vs 15 person-days." Directly answers the question enterprises care about most: after you join, what's the team's capacity multiplier?

Quantifiable metrics: actual cognitive input vs traditional estimate ratio, number of concurrent projects, AI tool orchestration count, automation coverage rate.

### Quality (15%)

Does your output withstand scrutiny?

In an era when AI can generate content at scale, quality gatekeepers are unprecedentedly important. Corresponds to Discernment in the 4D Framework and reliability adjustments in the Economic Index—they found that factoring in task success rates reduces productivity improvement estimates by about one-third.

Quantifiable metrics: user/traffic numbers, system uptime, number of quality control mechanisms (automated testing, verification SOPs, review processes), external citations/shares.

### Influence (15%)

Are your methods being learned or adopted by others?

The transformation from individual contributor to organizational enabler. The highest-leverage form of value creation.

Quantifiable metrics: open source project interactions (stars, forks, contributors), skill/template adoption frequency, teaching content reach, external citations of methodology.

### Relationships Between Dimensions

To be honest: these five dimensions aren't completely independent. High Quality is often a precursor to Influence. A powerful automation pipeline (Leverage) is itself a form of delivery (Delivery). Causal relationships exist between dimensions, and precise attribution can sometimes be unclear.

This is a design feature, not a flaw. Real value creation is inherently multi-dimensional and interwoven. Revenue, gross profit, and net profit in financial statements also have causal relationships, but we don't just look at one number because each reveals different aspects. The five dimensions are five observation lenses, not five orthogonal axes. The goal is richer observation, not perfect decomposition.

## Anti-Fraud Mechanisms: Automatic Extraction, Structured Self-Assessment, AI Verification

The most common challenge to any competency framework is: can it be gamed?

This concern is completely valid. Goodhart's Law—when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure—applies to all quantification systems. The question isn't whether it can be gamed, but whether the cost of gaming is high enough.

AI Collaboration Portfolio responds with a three-layer evidence architecture:

**Layer 1: Automatic extraction.** After user authorization, the system directly pulls data from third-party platforms—GitHub commit records, repo structure, CI/CD workflow files, website analytics, package download counts, community metrics. This data is recorded by independent platforms and cannot be tampered with by users. Your GitHub shows however many commits you have. This layer is the "hard evidence" baseline of the Portfolio.

**Layer 2: Structured self-assessment.** For parts that can't be automatically extracted (concurrent project count, traditional person-day estimates, AI tool integration methods), structured forms guide completion. Each self-assessment field has an adjacent "evidence link" field. Those with links are marked as *Evidenced*, those without as *Self-reported*. Anyone viewing your Portfolio can see these markers and judge credibility themselves.

**Layer 3: AI verification.** After users authorize GitHub access, AI analyzes repo structure and code patterns to independently suggest scores for each dimension. AI suggested scores are displayed alongside user self-assessments. If you self-assess 90 but AI suggests 50, this gap is visually flagged.

The three layers work together: automatic data can't be faked, self-assessments have transparent evidence markers, AI provides independent reference. This doesn't eliminate gaming—it raises gaming costs to unprofitable levels.

More critically, the framework's ultimate defense isn't internal validation but external reality. Quality and Influence dimensions require evidence from outside user control—user adoption rates, community interaction, customer feedback, market results. Internal teams can collude to inflate Jira ticket counts, but can't force market adoption or fake genuine open source community uptake.

This framework isn't an automatic judge. It's a forensic accounting system—an auditable ledger that shifts evaluation from "who tells the best story" to "whose evidence chain withstands questioning."

## Six Strongest Challenges and Responses: From Forged Evidence to Survivorship Bias

We put this framework through a multi-model adversarial debate engine for three rounds of stress testing. Below are the six most powerful attacks and our responses.

**Q1: AI can help people forge evidence chains.**

AI can forge maps but can't forge the memory of walking that path. In framework-guided deep interviews, interviewers probe not "what did you do" but "how did you make decisions"—why abandon that approach? How did you consider token consumption tradeoffs? How did you handle model hallucinations? People who actually did the work can answer three layers of follow-up. Those following scripts collapse at the third layer.

**Q2: Leverage's baseline (person-days) is something you said is obsolete—using it as a denominator is circular reasoning.**

Leverage shouldn't be interpreted as a static multiplier against a fixed baseline. Its core value is the rate of change over time. An engineer achieving 10x leverage with GPT-4 in 2024 and still 10x with Claude Opus 4.6 in 2026—this itself reveals stagnated adaptability. The framework tests growth slope, not absolute values. AI tools evolve, baselines shift (Red Queen effect), and what has true predictive power is your speed adapting to each paradigm shift.

**Q3: Why not add "Adaptability" and "Ethics" dimensions?**

Adaptability isn't an independent dimension—it's the time derivative of the five dimensions. When someone's Command, Delivery, Leverage scores continuously improve across AI paradigm shifts, they demonstrate high adaptability. The same logic applies to "reflection loops"—meta-skills of using AI to analyze and optimize one's own work patterns. This manifests as upward trajectories of the five dimensions over time, not static scores in a sixth column. Ethics is a baseline constraint, not a performance indicator—ethical violations should result in immediate disqualification, not a 15-point deduction.

**Q4: Dimensions have causal relationships and can't be precisely attributed.**

Acknowledged. See "Relationships Between Dimensions" above. The framework pursues richer observation, not mathematical orthogonality. Where attribution is ambiguous, the three-layer evidence architecture provides raw data for evaluators to judge themselves—this is precisely the framework's design intent: it doesn't replace human judgment, it gives human judgment better materials.

**Q5: The framework focuses on individuals, but the highest-value contributions in the AI era are often shared cognitive assets.**

This is a real limitation of v1. When someone builds a prompt library that the whole department uses, designs a workflow that doubles everyone's efficiency—this network effect indeed can't be fully captured by individual dimensions. The Influence dimension partially addresses this, but not enough. Team-version framework—measuring how someone amplifies system capacity rather than just their own—is v2's most important evolution direction.

**Q6: The author using himself as a case study is survivorship bias.**

Methodologically completely valid. A framework's designer will naturally select evidence favorable to themselves. The response isn't denying bias but making bias structurally unimportant: every claim in the case below can be independently verified through public URLs, GitHub repos, or third-party analytics APIs. This case's value isn't statistical—it's engineering. It provides a complete, inspectable, replicable blueprint that anyone can run with their own data. Think of it as open source release, not clinical trial. Initial versions inevitably reflect creator context, but value depends on whether the community finds it worth forking, attacking, improving.

## The Cost of Not Measuring AI Productivity: Capability Gaps Are Invisibly Expanding

Why does this matter? Because the cost of not measuring is greater than measuring wrong.

Anthropic Economic Index documents a deskilling effect: AI preferentially takes over high-skill components of work, leaving the low-skill parts. If we can't identify who truly excels at AI collaboration, high-capability individuals will be systematically undervalued, while those who interview well but produce poorly will be overvalued.

Productivity gaps are real and expanding. University-level tasks accelerated 12x by AI, high school level only 9x. This isn't small gaps slowly accumulating over time—this is structural divergence. And it's currently completely invisible to labor markets because no measurement system can reveal it.

On this topic "How should productivity be calculated in the AI era?", using multi-model cross-validation, several honest limitations became clearer:

**Individual vs System Value.** The framework measures individual output, but the most valuable contributions in the AI era are often shared cognitive assets—prompt libraries used by entire departments, automation workflows that accelerate everyone. Measuring how someone amplifies system capacity—not just their own—is v2's most important extension direction.

**Reflection Loops.** The highest-level skill in AI collaboration may be using AI to analyze and optimize one's own work patterns—a meta-cognitive ability driving growth across all dimensions. Current framework indirectly captures this through score trajectories over time but doesn't explicitly measure it. Future versions could treat self-iteration speed as a first-class signal.

**Cross-Organizational Portability.** AI collaboration occurs in vastly different cultural, legal, economic contexts. Collectivist cultures may systematically suppress self-assessment scores. GDPR limits automatic data extraction. Small organizations can't afford AI verification infrastructure. A framework only usable in well-resourced English-speaking tech companies isn't truly universal.

**Fairness and Accessibility.** Those with access to latest AI tools naturally have advantages in this framework. If the measurement system itself amplifies digital divides rather than revealing capabilities, it fails.

These aren't flaws to hide. They're research directions for the next iteration round.

But the alternative to an imperfect framework isn't a perfect framework—it's having no framework at all. It's continuing to evaluate AI collaboration capabilities based on resume keywords and interview impressions, in a world where "familiar with AI tools" and "can use AI to do an entire team's work alone" differ by an order of magnitude.

What can be quantified can be valued. What can't becomes invisible.

AI Collaboration Portfolio is the first attempt to make the invisible visible. It's an imperfect map. But the alternative isn't a better map—it's running blindfolded across new continents.
