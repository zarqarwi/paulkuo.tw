## 40 Minutes to Complete 15 Person-Days of Work: What Can Person-Days Still Measure?

*If 40 minutes of deep thinking can create output equivalent to traditional 15 person-days of work—then are the ways we measure work already obsolete?*

---

Three AIs running simultaneously. One handles strategic analysis, one writes code and deploys, one processes documents and operational tasks. The operator spends about 40 minutes—giving commands, reviewing output, making judgments, adjusting direction. When finished, on the desk sits a market analysis report, a refactored database, and a literature review.

The traditional approach? Five people, three to four working days. 15 to 20 person-days.

But those 40 minutes weren't idle time—they were high-density cognitive labor: decomposing vague objectives, orchestrating AI division of labor, correcting errors in real-time, integrating fragmented outputs, until results took shape.

So what exactly did "person-days" measure here? It measured time, not value. When measurement methods lag behind reality, we're not measuring productivity—just attendance (whether people showed up).

[Anthropic's Economic Index released in January 2026](https://www.anthropic.com/research) provides empirical foundation. They analyzed over one million Claude conversations and found that prompt quality correlates 0.92 with output quality. Tasks requiring college-level comprehension see 12x AI acceleration; high school level only 9x.

AI doesn't uniformly make everyone stronger. It exponentially amplifies capability gaps.

Two people's resumes both say "familiar with AI tools," but behind that phrase might lie vastly different capabilities.
One person can use AI to ship a full-stack application over a weekend, while another spends eight hours using ChatGPT to revise an email.
The problem isn't just the capability gap itself, but that until now, we still lack a system to recognize, measure, or even name this gap.

## From The Mythical Man-Month to AI Collaboration: Why Attention Duration Isn't Enough Either?

In 1975, computer scientist [Fred Brooks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fred_Brooks) published [*The Mythical Man-Month*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Mythical_Man-Month), with its most classic insight debunking the "man-month" myth: people and time were never interchangeable variables. Adding each person doesn't just increase capacity—it simultaneously increases communication, coordination, and integration costs, and these costs are enough to devour the originally imagined efficiency gains. So when a project is already behind, adding more people usually isn't a remedy—it makes it slower.

Brooks was talking about human teams. But the AI era validates his argument from the opposite direction: AI has reduced communication costs to zero. No onboarding needed, no alignment meetings, 24/7 operation, zero context switch costs. When coordination costs approach zero, parallel execution truly becomes efficient for the first time—but measurement systems built on the assumption that "coordination is expensive" simultaneously lose their meaning.

Some have tried to replace person-days with "attention duration." This concept divides human cognitive input in AI collaboration into four categories:
• Initiation attention: translating vague requirements into precise instructions;
• Supervision attention: checking AI output and correcting course in real-time;
• Integration attention: coordinating scattered outputs from multiple AIs;
• Incubation attention: letting unconscious background thinking settle and catalyze inspiration.

This categorization is helpful, but "attention duration" is essentially still an input-side metric. It tells us how much cognitive resources were invested, but doesn't tell us what these resources actually produced, their quality, or how much value they created compared to traditional methods.

The current problem is: the input side has "attention duration," the macro side has the [Anthropic Economic Index](https://www.anthropic.com/research), estimating AI's contribution to US labor productivity annual growth at approximately 1.0 percentage point; but at the individual level output side—the level where hiring decisions are made, where freelancers are evaluated—it's still almost a complete void.

The vacuum in measurement isn't at the margins—it's right at the center.

## Global AI Capability Framework Survey: Why Is No One Testing Individual Output?

This vacuum isn't because no one cares. Over the past two years, governments worldwide, international organizations, and academia have produced a wealth of AI capability frameworks. But they're all solving the same problem: do you know how to use AI? Not one is asking: what have you accomplished using AI?

The [AI Fluency Framework](https://www.anthropic.com/research) developed by Anthropic in collaboration with Rick Dakan and Joseph Feller defines four core capabilities (4D): Delegation, Description, Discernment, and Diligence. This is probably the most mature capability model currently available. But it describes "what good AI collaboration looks like," not quantifying "what it produced."

Anthropic's own Economic Index approaches from another angle, analyzing millions of real conversations to estimate AI's impact on labor productivity. The data is extremely valuable—but it operates at the statistical aggregate level, not the individual portfolio level.

The [US Department of Labor](https://www.dol.gov/) released an AI Literacy Framework, the [UK Turing Institute](https://www.turing.ac.uk/) launched the third edition of "AI Skills for Business Framework," [UNESCO](https://www.unesco.org/) has AI capability frameworks for students and teachers, and academia has proposed scales like Collaborative AI Literacy and Collaborative AI Metacognition.

It seems like there are many frameworks, but most are answering the same type of questions: do you understand AI, can you use it reasonably, do you know its limitations? In other words, they test literacy.

What's truly missing is performance-level measurement: what have you actually produced using AI, what's the quality of results, and how much efficiency and value have you gained compared to traditional methods?

![Global AI Capability Framework Positioning Chart: The lower-left quadrant is crowded with literacy-based frameworks, while the lower-right quadrant (individual performance) remains blank](/images/ai-framework-matrix.svg)

Put existing frameworks on a 2×2 matrix—horizontal axis is "literacy vs performance," vertical axis is "individual vs macro"—you'll see the left side crowded with people (UNESCO, DOL, Turing, various academic scales), the upper right has the [Anthropic Economic Index](https://www.anthropic.com/research). The lower right—individual level, performance-based, verifiable—is empty.

Everyone is testing AI literacy. No one is testing individual AI productivity. This article proposes a framework to fill this gap. It's on the same thread as my previous article on [AI Era Capability Gaps](/articles/ai-capability-gap-2026)—that piece discussed the existence of the gap, this one discusses how to quantify it.

## AI Collaboration Portfolio Five-Dimensional Model: What to Measure, How to Measure

AI Collaboration Portfolio is a five-dimensional model measuring what individuals actually produce through AI collaboration. Design principles: each dimension must have theoretical basis, quantifiable indicators, and verifiable data sources from third parties.

### Command (25%)

Can you make AI do the right things?

Corresponds to Delegation and Description in the 4D Framework, and "initiation attention" in attention theory. Anthropic found that prompt quality correlates 0.92 with output quality—this might be the single most critical variable in AI collaboration effectiveness.

Quantitative indicators: number of reusable skills/workflows/system prompts, number of automation pipelines (CI/CD, cron, GitHub Actions), breadth of AI tool integration, complexity of multi-step task decomposition.

### Delivery (25%)

After AI collaboration, what did you actually ship?

Not what you tried, but what went live. Corresponds to task completion rates in the Economic Index.

Quantitative indicators: commit frequency and volume, number of deployed services/tools, published content count, complete zero-to-production projects, code quality metrics (PR merge rates, issue resolution speed).

### Leverage (20%)

How many times did you amplify the same cognitive input?

This is the core of "40 minutes vs. 15 person-days." Directly answers the question enterprises care most about: what's the team's capacity multiplier after you join?

Quantitative indicators: actual cognitive input vs traditional estimate ratios, number of parallel projects, number of AI tools orchestrated, automation coverage rate.

### Quality (15%)

Can your output withstand scrutiny?

In an era when AI can mass-generate content, quality gatekeepers are unprecedentedly important. Corresponds to Discernment in the 4D Framework and reliability adjustments in the Economic Index—they found that factoring in task success rates reduces productivity improvement estimates by about one-third.

Quantitative indicators: user/traffic numbers, system uptime, number of quality control mechanisms (automated testing, verification SOPs, review processes), external citations/shares.

### Influence (15%)

Have your methods been learned or adopted by others?

The transition from individual contributor to organizational enabler. The highest leverage form of value creation.

Quantitative indicators: open source project interactions (stars, forks, contributors), skill/template adoption rates, teaching content reach, external methodology citations.

### Relationships Between Dimensions

To be honest: these five dimensions are not completely independent. High Quality is often a precursor to Influence. A powerful automation pipeline (Leverage) is itself a form of Delivery. Causal relationships exist between dimensions, and precise attribution can sometimes be blurred.

This is a design feature, not a flaw. Real value creation is inherently multi-dimensional and intertwined. Revenue, gross profit, and net profit in financial statements also have causal relationships, but we don't look at just one number because of this—each number reveals different aspects. The five dimensions are five observation lenses, not five orthogonal axes. The goal is richer observation, not perfect decomposition.

## Anti-Fraud Mechanisms: Automatic Capture, Structured Self-Assessment, AI Verification

The most common question about any capability framework is whether it will devolve into a score-gaming system.

This concern is completely valid. Goodhart's Law long ago pointed out: when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure. No quantification system is immune to this rule. What really needs to be asked isn't whether it can be gamed, but whether the cost of gaming is high enough to make manipulation unprofitable.

AI Collaboration Portfolio responds with a three-layer evidence architecture:

**First Layer: Automatic Capture.** After user authorization, the system directly pulls data from third-party platforms—GitHub commit records, repo structure, CI/CD workflow files, website analytics, package downloads, community metrics. These data are recorded by independent platforms and cannot be tampered with by users. However many commits you have on GitHub is how many you have. This layer is the "hard evidence" baseline of the Portfolio.

**Second Layer: Structured Self-Assessment.** For parts that cannot be automatically captured (number of parallel projects, traditional person-day estimates, AI tool integration methods), structured forms guide completion. Each self-assessment field has an adjacent "evidence link" field. Those with links are marked as *Evidenced*, those without as *Self-reported*. Anyone viewing your Portfolio can see this marking and judge credibility independently.

**Third Layer: AI Verification.** After users authorize GitHub, AI analyzes repo structure and code patterns to independently suggest scores for each dimension. AI suggested scores are displayed alongside user self-assessments. If you self-rate 90 points but AI suggests 50, this gap is visually flagged.

All three layers work together: automatic data can't be faked, self-assessments have transparent evidence marking, AI provides independent reference. This doesn't eliminate gaming—it raises gaming costs to unprofitable levels.

More critically, this framework's ultimate defense line isn't internal validation, but external reality.
The evidence required by Quality and Influence dimensions comes from outside user control: user adoption rates, community interactions, customer feedback, market results. Internal teams might collude to inflate Jira ticket counts, but they cannot force market acceptance or fake real adoption by open source communities.

This framework isn't an automatic judgment machine, but more like a forensic accounting system: an auditable ledger that shifts evaluation from "whose story sounds better" to "whose evidence chain withstands more scrutiny."

## Six Strongest Challenges and Responses: From Forged Evidence to Survivor Bias

We ran this framework through a multi-model adversarial debate engine for three rounds of stress testing. Here are the six most powerful attacks and our responses.

**Q1: AI can help people forge evidence chains.**

AI can forge maps, but cannot forge memories of walking that path. In framework-guided deep interviews, interviewers pursue not "what did you do" but "how did you make decisions"—why abandon that method? How did you weigh token consumption trade-offs? How did you handle model hallucinations? People who actually did the work can answer three layers of follow-up questions. Those acting from scripts collapse at the third layer.

**Q2: Leverage's baseline (person-days) is what you yourself said is obsolete—using it as denominator is circular reasoning.**

Leverage shouldn't be interpreted as a static multiplier against a fixed baseline. Its core value is the rate of change over time. An engineer achieving 10x leverage with GPT-4 in 2024 and still 10x with Claude Opus 4.6 in 2026—this itself exposes stagnation in adaptability. The framework measures growth slope, not absolute values. AI tools evolve, baselines shift (Red Queen effect), what truly has predictive power is your speed of adapting to each paradigm shift.

**Q3: Why not add "Adaptability" and "Ethics" dimensions?**

Adaptability isn't an independent dimension—it's the time derivative of the five dimensions. Someone whose Command, Delivery, and Leverage scores continuously improve across AI paradigm shifts demonstrates high adaptability. The same logic applies to "reflection loops"—the meta-skill of using AI to analyze and optimize one's own work patterns. It manifests as the upward trajectory of the five dimensions over time, not as a static score in a sixth column. Ethics is a baseline constraint, not a performance indicator—ethical violations should result in direct disqualification, not a 15-point deduction.

**Q4: Dimensions have causal relationships and cannot be precisely attributed.**

Acknowledged. See "Relationships Between Dimensions" above. The framework pursues richer observation, not mathematical orthogonality. Where attribution is blurred, the three-layer evidence architecture provides raw data for evaluators to judge independently—this is precisely the framework's design intent: it doesn't replace human judgment, it gives human judgment better materials.

**Q5: The framework focuses on individuals, but the highest-value contributions in the AI era are often shared cognitive assets.**

This is a real limitation of the current version. When someone builds a prompt library that the entire department can use, designs a workflow that doubles everyone's efficiency—this network effect truly cannot be fully captured by individual dimensions. The Influence dimension partially touches on this, but not enough. A team version framework—measuring how one person amplifies system capacity rather than just their own—is the most important evolutionary direction for the next phase.

**Q6: Using yourself as a case study is survivor bias.**

Methodologically completely valid. A framework's designer will naturally select evidence favorable to themselves. The response isn't to deny bias, but to make bias structurally unimportant: every claim in the case below can be independently verified through public URLs, GitHub repos, or third-party analytics APIs. This case's value isn't statistical—it's engineering. It provides a complete, inspectable, replicable blueprint that anyone can run with their own data. Think of it as open source release, not clinical trial. Initial versions inevitably reflect the creator's context, but their value depends on whether the community finds them worth forking, attacking, and improving.

## The Cost of Not Measuring AI Productivity: Capability Gaps Are Invisibly Expanding

Why does this matter? Because the cost of not measuring is greater than measuring incorrectly.

The [Anthropic Economic Index](https://www.anthropic.com/research) documents a deskilling effect: AI preferentially takes over high-skill components of work, leaving the low-skill parts. If we cannot identify who truly excels at AI collaboration, high-capability individuals will be systematically undervalued, while those who interview well but produce poorly will be overvalued.

Productivity gaps are real and expanding. College-level tasks are accelerated 12x by AI, high school level only 9x. This isn't a small gap that slowly accumulates over time—this is structural divergence. And it's currently completely invisible to the labor market because no measurement system can make it apparent.

On this topic "How should productivity be calculated in the AI era?" using multi-model cross-validation, several limitations became clearer:

**Individual vs System Value.** This framework measures individual output, but truly high-value contributions in the AI era are often shared cognitive assets: a prompt library used by the entire department, an automated workflow that accelerates everyone. How to measure one person's amplification effect on system capacity, rather than just their own output, is the most important extension direction for the next phase.

**Reflection Loops.** The highest-level skill in AI collaboration might not be operating AI, but using AI to analyze and optimize one's own work patterns: a meta-cognitive ability that drives growth across all dimensions. The current framework can only indirectly capture this through changes in scores over time, not explicitly measure it. Future versions could incorporate self-iteration speed as a first-class signal.

**Cross-organizational Portability.** AI collaboration occurs in vastly different cultural, legal, and economic contexts. Collectivist cultures might systematically suppress self-assessment scores; GDPR limits the scope of automatic data capture; small organizations may not afford AI verification infrastructure. A framework that only works in well-resourced English-speaking tech companies cannot be called truly universal.

**Fairness and Accessibility.** Those with access to the latest AI tools naturally have advantages in this framework. If the measurement system itself amplifies digital divides rather than revealing true capabilities, then it has failed.

These aren't flaws to be covered up, but research directions for the next iteration.

But don't forget: the choice we face isn't "this framework" vs "a more perfect framework," but "having a framework" vs "having nothing at all." What does a world without frameworks look like? It's what we have now: companies screening people by resume keywords, making decisions based on interview impressions, yet completely unable to distinguish between "knows how to use AI" and "can use AI to single-handedly handle an entire team's workload."

What can be quantified gets valued. What cannot becomes invisible.

AI Collaboration Portfolio is an attempt to make the invisible visible. It's an imperfect map: but the alternative isn't a better map—it's running blindfolded through new territory.
