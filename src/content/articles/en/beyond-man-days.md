## 40 Minutes of Work Equals 15 Person-Days: What Can Man-Days Still Measure?

*If 40 minutes of deep thinking can create output equivalent to traditional 15 person-days of work—then are our methods of measuring work already obsolete?*

---

Three AIs running simultaneously. One handles strategic analysis, one writes code and deploys, one processes documents and operational tasks. The operator spends about 40 minutes—giving commands, reviewing outputs, making judgments, adjusting directions. At the end, there's a market analysis report on the desk, a refactored database, and a literature review.

Using traditional methods? Five people, three to four working days. 15 to 20 person-days.

But those 40 minutes weren't idle time—they were high-density cognitive labor: decomposing vague objectives, orchestrating AI division of labor, real-time error correction, integrating scattered outputs until results took shape.

So what exactly do "person-days" measure here? They measure time, not value. When measurement methods lag behind reality, what we measure isn't productivity—it's just whether people are present (attendance).

[Anthropic's Economic Index released in January 2026](https://www.anthropic.com/research) provides empirical foundation. They analyzed over one million Claude conversations and found that prompt quality correlates 0.92 with output quality. Tasks requiring college-level understanding are accelerated 12x by AI; high school level only 9x.

AI doesn't uniformly strengthen everyone. It exponentially amplifies capability gaps.

Two people's resumes both say "familiar with AI tools," but behind this phrase might lie vastly different capabilities.
One person can ship a full-stack application using AI over a weekend, while another spends eight hours using ChatGPT to revise an email.
The problem isn't just the capability gap itself—it's that we still have no way to identify, measure, or even name this gap.

## From The Mythical Man-Month to AI Collaboration: Why Attention Duration Isn't Enough Either?

In 1975, computer scientist [Fred Brooks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fred_Brooks) published [*The Mythical Man-Month*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Mythical_Man-Month), with the most classic insight being the debunking of the "person-month" myth: people and time were never interchangeable variables. Adding each person not only increases capacity but simultaneously increases communication, coordination, and integration costs, and these costs are sufficient to consume the originally imagined efficiency gains. So when a project is already behind schedule, adding more people is usually not a remedy—it makes it slower.

Brooks was talking about human teams. But the AI era validates his argument from the opposite direction: AI has reduced communication costs to zero. No onboarding needed, no alignment meetings, 24-hour continuous work, zero context switch costs. When coordination costs approach zero, parallel execution becomes truly efficient for the first time—but measurement systems built on the assumption that "coordination is expensive" simultaneously lose their meaning.

Some have tried to replace person-days with "attention duration." This concept divides human cognitive investment in AI collaboration into four categories:
• Initiation attention: translating vague requirements into precise instructions;
• Supervision attention: checking AI output and correcting course in real-time;
• Integration attention: coordinating scattered outputs from multiple AIs;
• Incubation attention: letting unconscious background thinking settle and catalyze insights.

Such categorization is helpful, but "attention duration" is essentially still an input-side indicator. It tells us how much cognitive resources were invested, but doesn't tell us what these resources actually produced, the quality, or how much value was created compared to traditional approaches.

The current problem is: the input side has "attention duration," the macro side has the [Anthropic Economic Index](https://www.anthropic.com/research), estimating AI's contribution to US labor productivity annual growth at about 1.0 percentage points; but at the individual level output side—the level where hiring decisions happen, where freelancers are evaluated—it's still almost a complete blank.

The vacuum in measurement isn't at the margins—it's at the very center.

## Global AI Capability Framework Inventory: Why Is No One Testing Individual Output?

This vacuum isn't because no one cares. Over the past two years, governments, international organizations, and academia have produced numerous AI capability frameworks. But they're all solving the same problem: do you know how to use AI? Not one asks: what have you created using AI?

The [AI Fluency Framework](https://www.anthropic.com/research) developed by Anthropic in collaboration with Rick Dakan and Joseph Feller defines four core capabilities (4D): Delegation, Description, Discernment, and Diligence. This is probably the most mature capability model currently available. But it describes "what good AI collaboration looks like," not quantifying "what it produces."

Anthropic's own Economic Index approaches from another angle, analyzing millions of real conversations to estimate AI's impact on labor productivity. The data is extremely valuable—but it operates at the statistical aggregate level, not at the individual Portfolio level.

The [US Department of Labor](https://www.dol.gov/) released an AI Literacy Framework, the [UK Turing Institute](https://www.turing.ac.uk/) launched the third version of "AI Skills for Business Framework," [UNESCO](https://www.unesco.org/) has AI competency frameworks for students and teachers, and academia has also proposed scales like Collaborative AI Literacy and Collaborative AI Metacognition.

There seem to be many frameworks, but they mostly answer the same type of questions: do you understand AI, can you use it reasonably, do you know its limitations? In other words, they test literacy.

What's truly missing is performance-level measurement: what you've actually produced using AI, the quality of results, and how much efficiency and value you've improved compared to traditional approaches.

Everyone is testing AI literacy. No one is testing individual AI productivity. This article proposes a framework to fill this gap. ## AI Collaboration Portfolio Five-Dimensional Model: What to Measure and How

AI Collaboration Portfolio is a five-dimensional model that measures what individuals actually produce through AI collaboration. Design principles: each dimension must have theoretical foundation, quantifiable indicators, and verifiable data sources from third parties.

### Command (25%)

Can you make AI do the right things?

Corresponds to Delegation and Description in the 4D Framework, and "initiation attention" in attention theory. Anthropic found prompt quality correlates 0.92 with output quality—this might be the single most critical variable for AI collaboration effectiveness.

Quantitative indicators: number of reusable skills/workflows/system prompts, number of automated pipelines (CI/CD, cron, GitHub Actions), breadth of AI tool integration, complexity of multi-step task decomposition.

### Delivery (25%)

What did you actually ship after AI collaboration?

Not what you tried, but what went live. Corresponds to task completion rates in the Economic Index.

Quantitative indicators: commit frequency and volume, number of deployed services/tools, published content count, complete projects from zero to production, code quality metrics (PR merge rate, issue resolution speed).

### Leverage (20%)

How many times did you amplify the same cognitive investment?

This is the core of "40 minutes vs. 15 person-days." Directly answers the question enterprises care most about: what's the team's capacity multiplier after you join?

Quantitative indicators: ratio of actual cognitive investment vs traditional estimates, number of parallel projects, number of AI tools orchestrated, automation coverage rate.

### Quality (15%)

Can your output withstand scrutiny?

In an era when AI can mass-generate content, quality gatekeepers are unprecedentedly important. Corresponds to Discernment in the 4D Framework and reliability adjustments in the Economic Index—they found that factoring in task success rates reduces productivity improvement estimates by about one-third.

Quantitative indicators: user/traffic numbers, system uptime, number of quality control mechanisms (automated testing, verification SOPs, review processes), external citations/shares.

### Influence (15%)

Are your methods being learned or adopted by others?

The transition from individual contributor to organizational enabler. The highest leverage form of value creation.

Quantitative indicators: open-source project interactions (stars, forks, contributors), skill/template adoption counts, teaching content reach, external citations of methodologies.

### Relationships Between Dimensions

To be honest: these five dimensions are not completely independent. High Quality is often a precursor to Influence. A powerful automation pipeline (Leverage) is itself a form of Delivery. Causal relationships exist between dimensions, and precise attribution can sometimes be ambiguous.

This is a design feature, not a defect. Real value creation is inherently multi-dimensionally intertwined. Revenue, gross profit, and net profit in financial statements also have causal relationships, but we don't look at just one number because of this—each number reveals different aspects. The five dimensions are five observation lenses, not five orthogonal axes. The goal is richer observation, not perfect decomposition.

## Anti-Fraud Mechanisms: Automatic Collection, Structured Self-Assessment, AI Verification

The most common challenge to any capability framework is whether it will devolve into a score-gaming system.

This concern is completely valid. Goodhart's Law pointed out long ago: when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure. No quantification system can be exempt from this rule. What really needs questioning isn't whether it can be gamed, but whether the cost of gaming is high enough to make manipulation uneconomical.

AI Collaboration Portfolio responds with a three-layer evidence architecture:

**Layer 1: Automatic collection.** After user authorization, the system directly pulls data from third-party platforms—GitHub commit records, repo structure, CI/CD workflow files, website analytics, package download counts, community metrics. This data is recorded by independent platforms and cannot be tampered with by users. The number of commits on your GitHub is what it is. This layer is the "hard evidence" baseline of the Portfolio.

**Layer 2: Structured self-assessment.** Parts that cannot be automatically collected (number of parallel projects, traditional person-day estimates, AI tool integration methods) use structured forms to guide completion. Each self-assessment field has an "evidence link" field next to it. Those with attached links are marked as *Evidenced*, those without are marked as *Self-reported*. Anyone viewing your Portfolio can see this marking and judge credibility themselves.

**Layer 3: AI verification.** After users authorize GitHub, AI analyzes repo structure and code patterns to independently suggest scores for each dimension. AI suggested scores are displayed alongside user self-assessments. If you self-assess 90 points but AI suggests 50 points, this gap is visually flagged.

The three layers work together: automatic data can't be faked, self-assessments have transparent evidence marking, AI provides independent reference. This doesn't eliminate gaming—it raises the cost of gaming to an uneconomical level.

More critically, this framework's ultimate defense isn't internal validation, but external reality.
The evidence required by Quality and Influence dimensions comes from outside the user's control: user adoption rates, community interactions, customer feedback, market results. Teams might collude internally to inflate Jira ticket counts, but they can't force the market to buy, nor can they fake genuine adoption by the open-source community.

This framework isn't an automatic judgment machine, but more like a forensic accounting system: an auditable ledger that shifts evaluation from "whose story sounds better" to "whose evidence chain withstands more questioning."

## Six Strongest Challenges and Responses: From Evidence Forgery to Survivor Bias

We put this framework through a multi-model adversarial debate engine for three rounds of stress testing. Here are the six most powerful attacks and our responses.

**Q1: AI can help people forge evidence chains.**

AI can forge maps, but it can't forge the memory of walking that path. In framework-guided deep interviews, interviewers don't ask "what did you do" but "how did you make decisions"—why abandon that approach? How did you weigh token consumption trade-offs? How did you handle model hallucinations? People who actually did it can answer three layers of follow-up questions. Those acting from scripts collapse at the third layer.

**Q2: Leverage's baseline (person-days) is something you yourself said was obsolete—using it as denominator is circular reasoning.**

Leverage shouldn't be interpreted as a static multiplier against a fixed baseline. Its core value is the rate of change over time. If an engineer achieved 10x leverage with GPT-4 in 2024 and still 10x with Claude Opus 4.6 in 2026—this itself exposes stagnation in adaptability. The framework tests growth slope, not absolute values. AI tools are evolving, baselines are shifting (Red Queen Effect)—what's truly predictive is your speed of adapting to each paradigm shift.

**Q3: Why not add "adaptability" and "ethics" dimensions?**

Adaptability isn't an independent dimension—it's the derivative of the five dimensions over time. When someone's Command, Delivery, and Leverage scores continue improving across AI paradigm shifts, they demonstrate high adaptability. The same logic applies to "reflective loops"—the meta-skill of using AI to analyze and optimize one's own work patterns. This manifests as the upward trajectory of the five dimensions over time, not as a static score in a sixth column. Ethics is a baseline constraint, not a performance indicator—ethical violations should result in direct disqualification, not a 15-point deduction.

**Q4: Dimensions have causal relationships and cannot be precisely attributed.**

Acknowledged. See "Relationships Between Dimensions" above. The framework pursues richer observation, not mathematical orthogonality. Where attribution is ambiguous, the three-layer evidence architecture provides raw data for evaluators to judge themselves—this is precisely the framework's design intent: it doesn't replace human judgment, it gives human judgment better material.

**Q5: The framework focuses on individuals, but the highest-value contributions in the AI era are often shared cognitive assets.**

This is a real limitation of the current version. When someone builds a prompt instruction library for the entire department to use or designs a workflow that doubles everyone's efficiency—this network effect indeed cannot be fully captured by individual dimensions. The Influence dimension partially addresses this, but not enough. A team version framework—measuring how a person amplifies system capacity, not just their own—is the most important evolutionary direction for the next phase.

**Q6: The author using themselves as a case study is survivor bias.**

Methodologically completely valid. A framework designer naturally selects evidence favorable to themselves. The response isn't to deny bias, but to make bias structurally unimportant: every claim in the case below can be independently verified through public URLs, GitHub repos, or third-party analytics APIs. This case's value isn't statistical—it's engineering. It provides a complete, inspectable, replicable blueprint that anyone can run with their own data. Think of it as an open-source release, not a clinical trial. Initial versions inevitably reflect the creator's context, but their value depends on whether the community finds them worth forking, attacking, and improving.

## The Cost of Not Measuring AI Productivity: Capability Gaps Are Invisibly Widening

Why does this matter? Because the cost of not measuring is greater than measuring incorrectly.

The [Anthropic Economic Index](https://www.anthropic.com/research) documents a deskilling effect: AI preferentially takes over high-skill components of work, leaving the low-skill parts. If we cannot identify who truly excels at AI collaboration, high-capability individuals will be systematically undervalued, while those who interview well but produce poorly will be overvalued.

Productivity gaps are real and widening. College-level tasks are accelerated 12x by AI, high school level only 9x. This isn't a small gap that slowly accumulates over time—it's structural divergence. And it's currently completely invisible to the labor market because no measurement system can reveal it.

Regarding this theme "How should productivity be calculated in the AI era?", using multi-model cross-verification, several honest limitations became clearer:

**Individual vs System Value.** The framework measures individual output, but the most valuable contributions in the AI era are often shared cognitive assets—prompt libraries used by entire departments, automated workflows that accelerate everyone. Measuring how someone amplifies system capacity—not just their own—is the most important extension direction for the next phase.

**Reflective Loops.** The highest-level skill in AI collaboration might be using AI to analyze and optimize one's own work patterns—a meta-cognitive ability that drives growth across all dimensions. The current framework indirectly captures this through score trajectories over time but doesn't explicitly measure it. Future versions could make self-iteration speed a first-class signal.

**Cross-Organizational Portability.** AI collaboration happens in vastly different cultural, legal, and economic contexts. Collectivist cultures might systematically suppress self-assessment scores. GDPR restricts automatic data collection. Small organizations can't afford AI verification infrastructure. A framework that only works in well-resourced English-speaking tech companies isn't truly universal.

**Fairness and Accessibility.** People with access to the latest AI tools naturally have advantages in this framework. If the measurement system itself amplifies rather than reveals the digital divide, it fails.

These aren't flaws to hide. They're research directions for the next iteration round.

But the alternative to an imperfect framework isn't a perfect framework—it's having no framework at all. It's continuing to evaluate AI collaboration capabilities based on resume keywords and interview impressions, in a world where "familiar with AI tools" and "can use AI to do an entire team's work alone" differ by an order of magnitude.

What can be quantified can be valued. What cannot becomes invisible.

AI Collaboration Portfolio is the first attempt to make the invisible visible. It's an imperfect map. But the alternative isn't a better map—it's running blindfolded through a new continent.
