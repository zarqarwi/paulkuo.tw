## 40 Minutes Completing 15 Man-Days of Work: What Can Man-Days Still Measure?

*When one person's 40 minutes of cognitive input produces what traditionally required 15 man-days of work—what exactly are we measuring?*

---

Three AIs running simultaneously. One handling strategic analysis, one writing code and deploying, one managing documents and operational tasks. The operator spent about 40 minutes—issuing commands, reviewing outputs, making judgments, adjusting directions. When finished, on the desk lay a market analysis report, a refactored database, and a literature review.

Using traditional methods? Five people, three to four working days. 15 to 20 man-days.

But those 40 minutes weren't spent idling. They were high-density cognitive labor: breaking down vague objectives into executable AI instructions, deciding which AI does what, intercepting errors in real-time, integrating scattered outputs into a coherent whole.

So what exactly do "man-days" measure in this scenario? They measure the passage of time, not the creation of value. When our measurement system can't keep up with reality, what we're measuring isn't productivity—it's attendance.

[Anthropic's Economic Index, released in January 2026](https://www.anthropic.com/research), provides empirical foundation. They analyzed over one million Claude conversations and found that prompt quality correlates 0.92 with output quality. Tasks requiring university-level comprehension see 12x AI acceleration; high school level only 9x.

AI doesn't uniformly strengthen everyone. It exponentially amplifies capability gaps.

Two people's resumes both say "familiar with AI tools." One used AI to ship an entire full-stack application over a weekend, the other spent eight hours using ChatGPT to revise an email. Currently, we have no standardized way to distinguish between these two people.

## From The Mythical Man-Month to AI Collaboration: Why Attention Duration Isn't Enough Either?

In 1975, computer scientist [Fred Brooks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fred_Brooks) published [*The Mythical Man-Month*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Mythical_Man-Month). His core insight: man-months are a myth. People and time aren't interchangeable because communication costs eat up efficiency gains. Adding people to a late project only makes it later.

Brooks was talking about human teams. But the AI era validates his thesis from the opposite direction: AI has reduced communication costs to zero. No onboarding needed, no alignment meetings, 24/7 operation, zero context switching cost. When coordination costs approach zero, parallel execution becomes truly efficient for the first time—but measurement systems built on the assumption that "coordination is expensive" simultaneously lose their meaning.

Some have tried to replace man-days with "attention duration." This concept divides human cognitive input in AI collaboration into four categories: initiation attention (converting vague requirements into precise instructions), supervision attention (checking AI output and correcting course), integration attention (coordinating across multiple AI outputs), and incubation attention (unconscious background thinking that generates insights).

The categorization is useful, but attention duration remains an input-side metric. It tells us how much cognitive resources you spent, not what those resources produced, the quality level, or how much value was created compared to traditional methods.

The current situation is this: we have attention duration on the input side, Anthropic Economic Index on the macro side (estimating AI's contribution to US labor productivity at about 1.0 percentage points annually), but the individual level output side—the level where hiring decisions are made, where freelancers are evaluated—is completely blank.

The measurement vacuum isn't at the edges; it's dead center.

## Global AI Capability Framework Survey: Why Is No One Testing Individual Output?

This vacuum isn't because no one cares. Over the past two years, governments, international organizations, and academia have produced numerous AI capability frameworks. But they're all solving the same problem: can you use AI? None are asking: what have you accomplished with AI?

Anthropic's [AI Fluency Framework](https://www.anthropic.com/research), developed with Rick Dakan and Joseph Feller, defines four core capabilities (4D): Delegation, Description, Discernment, and Diligence. This is probably the most mature capability model available. But it describes "what good AI collaboration looks like," not quantifying "what it produced."

Anthropic's own Economic Index approaches from another angle, analyzing millions of real conversations to estimate AI's impact on labor productivity. The data is extremely valuable—but it operates at the statistical aggregate level, not the individual portfolio level.

[The US Department of Labor released its AI Literacy Framework in February 2026](https://www.dol.gov/). The [UK Turing Institute](https://www.turing.ac.uk/) published the third version of its AI Skills for Business Framework in late 2025. [UNESCO](https://www.unesco.org/) released AI capability frameworks for students and teachers. Academia has scales for Collaborative AI Literacy and Collaborative AI Metacognition.

Their common characteristic: they test literacy (do you understand AI, can you use it appropriately, do you know the limitations), not performance (what did you actually produce with AI, what quality, how much more efficient than traditional methods).

Place existing frameworks on a 2×2 matrix—horizontal axis "literacy vs performance," vertical axis "individual vs macro"—and you'll see the left side crowded with people (UNESCO, DOL, Turing, various academic scales), the upper right has Anthropic Economic Index. The lower right—individual level, performance-based, verifiable—is empty.

Everyone is testing AI literacy. No one is testing individual AI productivity. This article proposes a framework to fill this gap. It follows the same line as my previous article on [AI-era capability gaps](/articles/ai-capability-gap-2026)—that one discussed the existence of the gap, this one discusses how to quantify it.

## AI Collaboration Portfolio Five-Dimensional Model: What to Measure, How to Measure

AI Collaboration Portfolio is a five-dimensional model that measures what individuals actually produce through AI collaboration. Design principle: each dimension must have theoretical foundation, quantifiable indicators, and verifiable data sources from third parties.

### Command (25%)

Can you get AI to do the right things?

Corresponds to Delegation and Description in the 4D Framework, and "initiation attention" in attention theory. Anthropic found that prompt quality correlates 0.92 with output quality—this may be the single most critical variable for AI collaboration effectiveness.

Quantified indicators: number of reusable skills/workflows/system prompts, number of automation pipelines (CI/CD, cron, GitHub Actions), breadth of AI tool integration, complexity of multi-step task decomposition.

### Delivery (25%)

After AI collaboration, what did you actually ship?

Not what you tried, but what went live. Corresponds to task completion rates in the Economic Index.

Quantified indicators: commit frequency and volume, number of deployed services/tools, published content count, complete zero-to-production projects, code quality metrics (PR merge rate, issue resolution speed).

### Leverage (20%)

How many times did you amplify the same cognitive input?

This is the core of "40 minutes vs. 15 man-days." Directly answers the question enterprises care most about: after you join, what's the team's capacity multiplier?

Quantified indicators: ratio of actual cognitive input vs traditional estimates, number of concurrent projects, number of AI tools orchestrated, automation coverage rate.

### Quality (15%)

Can your output withstand scrutiny?

In an era when AI can mass-generate content, quality gatekeepers are more important than ever. Corresponds to Discernment in the 4D Framework and reliability adjustments in the Economic Index—they found that factoring in task success rates reduces productivity improvement estimates by about one-third.

Quantified indicators: user/traffic numbers, system uptime, number of quality control mechanisms (automated testing, verification SOPs, review processes), external citations/shares.

### Influence (15%)

Are your methods being learned or adopted by others?

The transition from individual contributor to organizational enabler. The highest-leverage form of value creation.

Quantified indicators: open source project interactions (stars, forks, contributors), skill/template adoption counts, teaching content reach, external methodology citations.

### Relationships Between Dimensions

Honestly: these five dimensions aren't completely independent. High Quality is often a precursor to Influence. A powerful automation pipeline (Leverage) is itself a form of Delivery. Causal relationships exist between dimensions, and precise attribution can sometimes be ambiguous.

This is a design feature, not a flaw. Real value creation is inherently multi-dimensional and interconnected. Revenue, gross profit, and net profit in financial statements also have causal relationships, but we don't only look at one number because each reveals different facets. The five dimensions are five observation lenses, not five orthogonal axes. The goal is richer observation, not perfect decomposition.

## Anti-Fraud Mechanisms: Automated Collection, Structured Self-Assessment, AI Verification

The most common criticism of any capability framework is: can it be gamed?

This concern is completely valid. Goodhart's Law—when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure—applies to all quantification systems. The question isn't whether it can be gamed, but whether the cost of gaming is high enough.

AI Collaboration Portfolio responds with a three-layer evidence architecture:

**Layer 1: Automated collection.** After user authorization, the system directly pulls data from third-party platforms—GitHub commit records, repo structure, CI/CD workflow files, website analytics, package download counts, community metrics. This data is recorded by independent platforms and cannot be tampered with by users. If you have X commits on GitHub, that's what it is. This layer is the "hard evidence" baseline for the Portfolio.

**Layer 2: Structured self-assessment.** For parts that cannot be automatically collected (concurrent project counts, traditional man-day estimates, AI tool integration methods), structured forms guide completion. Each self-assessment field has an "evidence link" field beside it. Those with links are marked *Evidenced*, those without are marked *Self-reported*. Anyone viewing your Portfolio can see these markers and judge credibility themselves.

**Layer 3: AI verification.** After GitHub authorization, AI analyzes repo structure and code patterns to independently suggest scores for each dimension. AI suggested scores are displayed alongside user self-assessments. If you self-assess 90 but AI suggests 50, this gap is visually flagged.

The three layers work together: automated data cannot be faked, self-assessments have transparent evidence markers, AI provides independent reference. This doesn't eliminate gaming; it raises the cost of gaming to an uneconomical level.

More critically, the framework's ultimate defense isn't internal validation but external reality. Quality and Influence dimensions require evidence from outside the user's control—user adoption rates, community interaction, customer feedback, market results. Teams can conspire to inflate Jira ticket counts, but they can't force the market to buy, nor fake genuine adoption by open source communities.

This framework isn't an automatic judgment system. It's a forensic accounting system—an auditable ledger that shifts evaluation from "who tells the better story" to "whose evidence chain withstands scrutiny."

## Six Strongest Challenges and Responses: From Forged Evidence to Survivor Bias

We put this framework through a multi-model adversarial debate engine for three rounds of stress testing. Here are the six most powerful attacks and our responses.

**Q1: AI can help people forge evidence chains.**

AI can forge maps, but it cannot forge the memory of walking that path. In framework-guided depth interviews, interviewers don't ask "what did you do" but "how did you make decisions"—why did you abandon that approach? How did you weigh token consumption trade-offs? How did you handle model hallucinations? People who actually did the work can answer three layers of follow-up questions. Those following scripts collapse at the third layer.

**Q2: Leverage's baseline (man-days) is something you yourself said was obsolete. Using it as a denominator is circular reasoning.**

Leverage shouldn't be interpreted as a static multiplier against a fixed baseline. Its core value is the rate of change over time. An engineer achieving 10x leverage with GPT-4 in 2024 and still only 10x with Claude Opus 4.6 in 2026—this itself exposes stagnation in adaptability. The framework tests growth slope, not absolute values. AI tools are evolving, baselines are shifting (Red Queen effect), and what truly has predictive power is your speed of adaptation to each paradigm shift.

**Q3: Why not add "Adaptability" and "Ethics" dimensions?**

Adaptability isn't an independent dimension; it's the derivative of the five dimensions over time. When someone's Command, Delivery, and Leverage scores continuously improve between AI paradigm shifts, they demonstrate high adaptability. The same logic applies to "reflection loops"—the meta-skill of using AI to analyze and optimize one's own work patterns. It manifests as the upward trajectory of the five dimensions over time, not as a static score in a sixth column. Ethics is a baseline constraint, not a performance indicator—ethical violations should result in immediate disqualification, not a 15-point deduction.

**Q4: Dimensions have causal relationships making precise attribution impossible.**

Acknowledged. See "Relationships Between Dimensions" above. The framework pursues richer observation, not mathematical orthogonality. Where attribution is ambiguous, the three-layer evidence architecture provides raw data for evaluators to judge themselves—this is precisely the framework's design intent: it doesn't replace human judgment, it gives human judgment better materials.

**Q5: The framework focuses on individuals, but the highest-value contributions in the AI era are often shared cognitive assets.**

This is a real limitation of v1. When someone builds a Prompt library that the entire department uses, or designs a workflow that doubles everyone's efficiency—this network effect indeed cannot be fully captured by individual dimensions. The Influence dimension partially touches on this, but not enough. A team version framework—measuring how someone amplifies system capacity, not just their own—is the most important evolution direction for v2.

**Q6: The author using himself as a case study is survivor bias.**

Methodologically completely valid. A framework designer will naturally cherry-pick evidence favorable to themselves. The response isn't to deny bias, but to make bias structurally irrelevant: every claim in the case study below can be independently verified through public URLs, GitHub repos, or third-party analytics APIs. This case study's value isn't statistical—it's engineering. It provides a complete, inspectable, replicable blueprint that anyone can run with their own data. Think of it as an open source release, not a clinical trial. Initial versions inevitably reflect the creator's context, but their value depends on whether the community finds them worth forking, attacking, and improving.

## The Cost of Not Measuring AI Productivity: Capability Gaps Are Invisibly Widening

Why does this matter? Because the cost of not measuring is greater than measuring incorrectly.

Anthropic Economic Index recorded a deskilling effect: AI preferentially takes over high-skill components of work, leaving low-skill parts behind. If we cannot identify who truly excels at AI collaboration, high-capability individuals will be systematically undervalued, while those who interview well but produce poorly will be overvalued.

Productivity gaps are real and widening. University-level tasks are accelerated 12x by AI, high school level only 9x. This isn't a small gap that slowly accumulates over time—it's a structural divergence. And it's currently completely invisible to the labor market because no measurement system can reveal it.

Three rounds of debate pressure testing made several honest limitations clearer:

**Individual vs System Value.** The framework measures individual output, but the most valuable contributions in the AI era are often shared cognitive assets—Prompt libraries used by entire departments, automation workflows that accelerate everyone. Measuring how someone amplifies system capacity—not just their own—is the most important extension direction for v2.

**Reflection Loops.** The highest-order skill in AI collaboration may be using AI to analyze and optimize one's own work patterns—a metacognitive ability that drives growth in all dimensions. The current framework indirectly captures this through score trajectories over time, but doesn't explicitly measure it. Future versions could treat self-iteration speed as a first-class signal.

**Cross-Organizational Portability.** AI collaboration occurs in vastly different cultural, legal, and economic contexts. Collectivist cultures may systematically suppress self-assessment scores. GDPR restricts automated data collection. Small organizations cannot afford AI verification infrastructure. A framework that only works in well-resourced English-speaking tech companies isn't truly universal.

**Fairness and Accessibility.** People with access to the latest AI tools have natural advantages in this framework. If the measurement system itself amplifies digital divides rather than revealing capabilities, it fails.

These aren't flaws to be concealed. They're research directions for the next iteration.

But the alternative to an imperfect framework isn't a perfect framework—it's having no framework at all. It's continuing to evaluate AI collaboration capabilities based on resume keywords and interview impressions, in a world where "familiar with AI tools" and "can use AI to do an entire team's work alone" differ by an order of magnitude.

What can be quantified can be valued. What cannot becomes invisible.

AI Collaboration Portfolio is a first attempt to make the invisible visible. It's an imperfect map. But the alternative isn't a better map—it's running blindfolded through a new continent.
