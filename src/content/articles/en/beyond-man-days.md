## 40 Minutes to Complete 15 Person-Days of Work: What Can Person-Days Still Measure?

*When one person's 40 minutes of cognitive input produces what traditionally required 15 person-days of work—what exactly are we measuring?*

---

Three AIs running simultaneously. One handling strategy analysis, one writing code and deploying, one processing documents and operational tasks. The operator spent about 40 minutes—giving instructions, reviewing output, making judgments, adjusting direction. When finished, on the desk lay a market analysis report, a refactored database, and a literature review.

The traditional approach? Five people, three to four working days. 15 to 20 person-days.

But those 40 minutes weren't spent idling. They were high-density cognitive labor: breaking down vague objectives into executable AI instructions, deciding which AI does what, intercepting errors in real-time, integrating scattered outputs into a coherent whole.

So what exactly do "person-days" measure in this scenario? They measure the passage of time, not the creation of value. When metrics can't keep up with reality, what we're measuring isn't productivity—it's attendance.

[Anthropic's Economic Index released in January 2026](https://www.anthropic.com/research) provides empirical foundation. They analyzed over one million Claude conversations and found that prompt quality correlates with output quality at 0.92. Tasks requiring university-level comprehension see AI acceleration of 12x; high school level only 9x.

AI doesn't uniformly strengthen everyone. It exponentially amplifies capability gaps.

Two people's resumes both state "familiar with AI tools." One used AI to ship a complete full-stack application over a weekend, another spent eight hours using ChatGPT to revise an email. Currently, we have no standardized way to distinguish between these two people.

## From The Mythical Man-Month to AI Collaboration: Why Attention Duration Isn't Enough Either?

In 1975, computer scientist [Fred Brooks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fred_Brooks) published [*The Mythical Man-Month*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Mythical_Man-Month). His core insight: man-months are fictional. People and time aren't interchangeable because communication costs consume efficiency gains. Adding people to a late project only makes it later.

Brooks was talking about human teams. But the AI era validates his thesis from the opposite direction: AI has eliminated communication costs. No onboarding needed, no alignment meetings, 24/7 operation, zero context switch costs. When coordination costs approach zero, parallel execution becomes truly efficient for the first time—but metrics built on the assumption that "coordination is expensive" simultaneously lose meaning.

Some have tried replacing person-days with "attention duration." This concept divides human cognitive input in AI collaboration into four categories: initiation attention (converting vague requirements into precise instructions), supervision attention (checking AI output and correcting course), integration attention (coordinating across multiple AI outputs), and incubation attention (unconscious background thinking that generates insights).

The categorization is useful, but attention duration remains an input-side metric. It tells us how much cognitive resources you spent, not what those resources produced, at what quality, or how much value they created relative to traditional methods.

The current situation is this: input side has attention duration, macro side has the Anthropic Economic Index (estimating AI contributes approximately 1.0 percentage point annual growth to US labor productivity), but the personal level output side—the level where hiring decisions are made, where freelancers are evaluated—is completely blank.

The measurement vacuum isn't at the periphery; it's dead center.

## Global AI Capability Framework Survey: Why Is No One Testing Personal Output?

This vacuum isn't because no one cares. Over the past two years, governments, international organizations, and academia have produced numerous AI capability frameworks. But they're all solving the same problem: can you use AI? None are asking: what did you create with AI?

Anthropic's [AI Fluency Framework](https://www.anthropic.com/research), developed with Rick Dakan and Joseph Feller, defines four core capabilities (4D): Delegation, Description, Discernment, and Diligence. This is probably the most mature capability model currently. But it describes "what good AI collaboration looks like," not quantifying "what it produces."

Anthropic's own Economic Index approaches from another angle, analyzing millions of real conversations to estimate AI's impact on labor productivity. The data is extremely valuable—but it operates at the statistical aggregate level, not the personal portfolio level.

[The US Department of Labor released its AI Literacy Framework in February 2026](https://www.dol.gov/). The [UK Turing Institute](https://www.turing.ac.uk/) released version 3 of its AI Skills for Business Framework in late 2025. [UNESCO](https://www.unesco.org/) produced AI capability frameworks for students and teachers. Academia has scales for Collaborative AI Literacy and Collaborative AI Metacognition.

Their common characteristic: they test literacy (do you understand AI, can you use it appropriately, do you know its limitations), not performance (what did you actually produce with AI, at what quality, how much more efficient than traditional methods).

Plotting existing frameworks on a 2×2 matrix—horizontal axis "literacy vs performance," vertical axis "personal vs macro"—you'll see the left side crowded with people (UNESCO, DOL, Turing, various academic scales), the upper right corner has the Anthropic Economic Index. The lower right corner—personal level, performance-based, verifiable—is empty.

Everyone is testing AI literacy. No one is testing personal AI productivity. This article proposes a framework to fill this gap. It's on the same thread as my previous piece on [capability gaps in the AI era](/articles/ai-capability-gap-2026)—that piece discussed the existence of the gap, this one discusses how to quantify it.

## AI Collaboration Portfolio Five-Dimensional Model: What to Measure, How to Measure

AI Collaboration Portfolio is a five-dimensional model measuring what individuals actually produce through AI collaboration. Design principle: each dimension must have theoretical foundation, quantifiable metrics, and verifiable data sources from third parties.

### Command (25%)

Can you get AI to do the right things?

Corresponds to Delegation and Description in the 4D Framework, and "initiation attention" in attention theory. Anthropic found prompt quality correlates with output quality at 0.92—this may be the most critical single variable in AI collaboration effectiveness.

Quantitative indicators: number of reusable skills/workflows/system prompts, number of automation pipelines (CI/CD, cron, GitHub Actions), breadth of AI tool integration, complexity of multi-step task decomposition.

### Delivery (25%)

After AI collaboration, what did you actually ship?

Not what you tried, but what went live. Corresponds to task completion rates in the Economic Index.

Quantitative indicators: commit frequency and volume, number of deployed services/tools, published content count, complete zero-to-production projects, code quality metrics (PR merge rates, issue resolution speed).

### Leverage (20%)

For the same cognitive input, by how much was it amplified?

This is the core of "40 minutes vs. 15 person-days." Directly answers the question enterprises care most about: when you join, what's the team's productivity multiplier?

Quantitative indicators: ratio of actual cognitive input vs traditional estimates, number of parallel projects, number of AI tools orchestrated, automation coverage rate.

### Quality (15%)

Can your output withstand scrutiny?

In an era when AI can mass-generate content, quality gatekeepers are unprecedentedly important. Corresponds to Discernment in the 4D Framework and reliability adjustments in the Economic Index—they found that factoring in task success rates reduces productivity improvement estimates by about one-third.

Quantitative indicators: user/traffic numbers, system uptime, number of quality control mechanisms (automated testing, verification SOPs, review processes), external citations/shares.

### Influence (15%)

Are your methods being learned or adopted by others?

The transition from individual contributor to organizational enabler. The highest leverage form of value creation.

Quantitative indicators: open source project interactions (stars, forks, contributors), skill/template adoption rates, teaching content reach, external methodology citations.

### Relationships Between Dimensions

To be honest: these five dimensions aren't completely independent. High Quality is often a precursor to Influence. A powerful automation pipeline (Leverage) is itself a form of Delivery. Causal relationships exist between dimensions, and precise attribution can sometimes be blurry.

This is a design feature, not a flaw. Real value creation is inherently multi-dimensionally intertwined. Revenue, gross profit, and net profit in financial statements also have causal relationships, but we don't therefore only look at one number, because each number reveals different aspects. Five dimensions are five observation lenses, not five orthogonal axes. The goal is richer observation, not perfect decomposition.

## Anti-Fraud Mechanisms: Automatic Capture, Structured Self-Assessment, AI Verification

The most common criticism of any capability framework is: can it be gamed?

This concern is completely valid. Goodhart's Law—when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure—applies to all quantification systems. The question isn't whether it can be gamed, but whether the cost of gaming is high enough.

AI Collaboration Portfolio responds with a three-layer evidence architecture:

**Layer 1: Automatic capture.** After user authorization, the system directly pulls data from third-party platforms—GitHub commit records, repo structure, CI/CD workflow files, website analytics, package downloads, community metrics. This data is recorded by independent platforms and cannot be tampered with by users. However many commits you have on GitHub, that's how many there are. This layer is the Portfolio's "hard evidence" baseline.

**Layer 2: Structured self-assessment.** Parts that can't be automatically captured (number of parallel projects, traditional person-day estimates, AI tool integration methods) use structured forms for guided input. Each self-assessment field has an adjacent "evidence link" field. Those with attached links are marked *Evidenced*, those without are marked *Self-reported*. Anyone viewing your Portfolio can see this marking and judge credibility independently.

**Layer 3: AI verification.** After GitHub authorization, AI analyzes repo structure and code patterns to independently suggest scores for each dimension. AI suggested scores are displayed alongside user self-assessments. If you self-assess 90 but AI suggests 50, this gap is visually flagged.

The three layers work together: automatic data can't be faked, self-assessments have transparent evidence marking, AI provides independent reference. This doesn't eliminate gaming; it raises gaming costs to unprofitable levels.

More crucially, the framework's ultimate defense isn't internal validation, but external reality. Quality and Influence dimensions require evidence from outside user control—user adoption rates, community interactions, customer feedback, market results. Team members can collude to inflate Jira ticket counts, but they can't force market adoption or fake genuine open source community usage.

This framework isn't an automatic judgment system. It's a forensic accounting system—an auditable ledger that shifts evaluation from "who tells the best story" to "whose evidence chain withstands questioning."

## Six Strongest Challenges and Responses: From Evidence Forgery to Survivorship Bias

We put this framework through multi-model adversarial debate engines for three rounds of stress testing. Here are the six most powerful attacks and our responses.

**Q1: AI can help people forge evidence chains.**

AI can forge maps but cannot forge memories of walking that path. In framework-guided deep interviews, interviewers don't ask "what did you do" but "how did you make decisions"—why abandon that approach? How did you weigh token consumption trade-offs? How did you handle model hallucinations? People who actually did it can answer three layers of follow-ups. Those performing from scripts collapse at the third layer.

**Q2: Leverage's baseline (person-days) is something you yourself said is obsolete—using it as denominator is circular reasoning.**

Leverage shouldn't be interpreted as a static multiplier against a fixed baseline. Its core value is the rate of change over time. An engineer achieving 10x leverage with GPT-4 in 2024, still achieving 10x with Claude Opus 4.6 in 2026—this itself exposes stagnation in adaptability. The framework measures growth slope, not absolute values. AI tools are evolving, baselines are shifting (Red Queen effect), what truly has predictive power is your speed of adapting to each paradigm shift.

**Q3: Why not add "Adaptability" and "Ethics" dimensions?**

Adaptability isn't an independent dimension; it's the time derivative of the five dimensions. A person whose Command, Delivery, and Leverage scores continuously improve across AI paradigm shifts demonstrates high adaptability. The same logic applies to "reflection loops"—the meta-skill of using AI to analyze and optimize one's own work patterns. It manifests as the upward trajectory of the five dimensions over time, not as a static score in a sixth column. Ethics is a baseline constraint, not a performance metric—ethical violations should result in direct disqualification, not a 15-point deduction.

**Q4: Dimensions have causal relationships and cannot be precisely attributed.**

Acknowledged. See "Relationships Between Dimensions" above. The framework pursues richer observation, not mathematical orthogonality. Where attribution is fuzzy, the three-layer evidence architecture provides raw data for evaluators to judge independently—this is precisely the framework's design intent: it doesn't replace human judgment, it gives human judgment better materials.

**Q5: The framework focuses on individuals, but the highest value contributions in the AI era are often shared cognitive assets.**

This is a real limitation of v1. When someone builds a prompt instruction library that the entire department can use, designs a workflow that doubles everyone's efficiency—these network effects indeed cannot be fully captured by individual dimensions. The Influence dimension partially touches this, but not enough. Team version framework—measuring how a person amplifies system productivity, not just their own—is the most important evolutionary direction for v2.

**Q6: The author using himself as a case study is survivorship bias.**

Methodologically completely valid. A framework's designer will naturally select evidence favorable to themselves. The response isn't to deny bias, but to make bias structurally unimportant: every claim in the case study below can be independently verified through public URLs, GitHub repos, or third-party analysis APIs. This case's value isn't statistical—it's engineering. It provides a complete, inspectable, replicable blueprint that anyone can run with their own data. Think of it as open source release, not clinical trial. Initial versions inevitably reflect creator context, but its value depends on whether the community finds it worth forking, attacking, improving.

## Case Validation: Paul Kuo

To verify the framework's practical operability, I ran it with my own data. I'm Paul Kuo, an independently operating developer and BD consultant in Taiwan, using AI collaboration as my primary work method. All data below is publicly verifiable.

**Command: L4 (Architect).** Maintaining 7+ custom AI workflow specifications (including v2.3 writing standards, multi-session handover protocols, community publishing pipelines), extracting reusable decision rules from 12 production incidents, simultaneously orchestrating 4+ AI models with routing logic (e.g., Chinese audio uses Qwen, English uses Groq, other languages use Deepgram). CI/CD pipelines include daily automated evaluation, scheduled data updates, single-commit triggered four-language content generation. All recorded at [github.com/zarqarwi](https://github.com/zarqarwi).

**Delivery: L4 (Architect).** Output includes: 80+ four-language articles on [paulkuo.tw](https://paulkuo.tw), three deployed web tools (AI-Ready Dashboard, Builder's Scorecard, Community Activity Wall), a 55-language Chrome extension (Claude Usage Nyan), a real-time meeting transcription system with three-way voice recognition (Agora Translator), an e-commerce operation with completed pricing strategy and momo listing (Daily Table). Each item can be viewed online directly.

**Leverage: L3-4.** All the above built and maintained by one person. Timing App's time tracking data is integrated into the website's real-time dashboard, providing actual cognitive input hours. Simultaneously maintaining 8+ active projects—spanning software development, content publishing, e-commerce, consulting—traditionally requiring a 5-8 person team.

**Quality: L3.** GitHub Actions automated CI/CD deployment with pre-commit hooks, two-stage fact-checking protocol (L1 material verification + L2 final draft verification) applied to all 82 articles, AI-Ready assessment system rates own infrastructure 90/100. Cloudflare website analytics provides independent traffic data through password-protected dashboard.

**Influence: L2.** This is my weakest dimension—I know. Open source projects exist (claude-usage-nyan, multi-agent-debate-engine) but community interaction is limited. Social media active on X, Threads, Bluesky but not yet scaled. LinkedIn and Medium completely unused. Significant speaking engagements and external methodology citations nearly zero.

The framework's diagnostic value is visible here: it doesn't just validate strengths, it exposes weaknesses with uncomfortably precise accuracy. My Command and Delivery are strong, Influence directly tells me what to do next.

But the more crucial observation isn't the scores themselves, but the nature of evidence. Every claim above can be independently verified through public URLs, GitHub repos, or website analytics APIs. Not a single item relies solely on self-declaration. This is the fundamental difference between this framework and all survey-based assessments: evidence exists outside the system.

## The Cost of Not Measuring AI Productivity: Capability Gaps Are Invisibly Widening

Why does this matter? Because the cost of not measuring is greater than measuring wrong.

The Anthropic Economic Index recorded a deskilling effect: AI preferentially takes over high-skill components of work, leaving low-skill parts. If we can't identify who truly excels at AI collaboration, high-capability individuals will be systematically undervalued, while those who interview well but produce poorly will be overvalued.

Productivity gaps are real and widening. University-level tasks are accelerated 12x by AI, high school level only 9x. This isn't a small gap that slowly accumulates over time—it's structural divergence. And it's currently completely invisible to labor markets because no measurement system exists to reveal it.

Three rounds of debate pressure testing made several honest limitations clearer:

**Individual vs System Value.** The framework measures individual output, but the most valuable contributions in the AI era are often shared cognitive assets—prompt libraries used by entire departments, automation workflows that accelerate everyone. Measuring how a person amplifies system productivity—not just their own—is v2's most important extension direction.

**Reflection Loops.** The highest-order skill in AI collaboration might be using AI to analyze and optimize one's own work patterns—a meta-cognitive ability that drives growth in all dimensions. Current framework captures this indirectly through score trajectories over time, but doesn't explicitly measure it. Future versions could treat self-iteration speed as a first-class signal.

**Cross-Organizational Transferability.** AI collaboration occurs in vastly different cultural, legal, and economic contexts. Collectivist cultures might systematically suppress self-assessment scores. GDPR restricts automatic data capture. Small organizations can't afford AI verification infrastructure. A framework that only works in well-resourced English-speaking tech companies isn't truly universal.

**Fairness and Accessibility.** Those with access to the latest AI tools have natural advantages in this framework. If the measurement system itself amplifies digital divides rather than revealing capabilities, it fails.

These aren't flaws to hide. They're research directions for the next iteration round.

But the alternative to an imperfect framework isn't a perfect framework—it's no framework at all. It's continuing to evaluate AI collaboration capabilities through resume keywords and interview impressions, in a world where "familiar with AI tools" and "can use AI to single-handedly do an entire team's work" differ by an order of magnitude.

What can be quantified can be valued. What cannot becomes invisible.

AI Collaboration Portfolio is a first attempt to make the invisible visible. It's an imperfect map. But the alternative isn't a better map—it's running blindfolded through a new continent.
