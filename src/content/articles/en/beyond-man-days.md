## 40 Minutes of Work Equivalent to 15 Man-Days: What Can Man-Days Still Measure?

*If 40 minutes of deep thinking can create output equivalent to traditional 15 man-days—then are our methods of measuring work already obsolete?*

---

Three AIs running simultaneously. One handling strategic analysis, one writing code and deploying, one managing documents and operational tasks. The operator spent about 40 minutes—issuing commands, reviewing output, making judgments, adjusting direction. When finished, there was a market analysis report, a refactored database, and a literature review on the desk.

Traditional approach? Five people, three to four working days. 15 to 20 man-days.

But those 40 minutes weren't idle time—they were high-density cognitive labor: decomposing vague goals, orchestrating AI division of labor, real-time error correction, integrating scattered outputs until results took shape.

So what exactly did "man-days" measure here? It measured time, not value. When measurement methods lag behind reality, we're not measuring productivity—just whether people showed up (attendance).

The [Anthropic Economic Index released in January 2026](https://www.anthropic.com/research) provides empirical foundation. They analyzed over a million Claude conversations and found that prompt quality correlates 0.92 with output quality. Tasks requiring college-level comprehension are accelerated 12x by AI; high school level only 9x.

AI doesn't uniformly strengthen everyone. It exponentially amplifies capability gaps.

Two people's resumes both say "familiar with AI tools," but behind that phrase could be vastly different capabilities.
One person can ship a full-stack application using AI over a weekend; another spends eight hours using ChatGPT to revise an email.
The problem isn't just the capability gap itself, but that until now, we still lack a way to recognize, measure, or even name this gap.

## From The Mythical Man-Month to AI Collaboration: Why Attention Duration Isn't Enough

In 1975, computer scientist [Fred Brooks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fred_Brooks) published [*The Mythical Man-Month*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Mythical_Man-Month), with his most classic insight debunking the "man-month" myth: people and time were never interchangeable variables. Adding each person not only increases capacity but simultaneously increases communication, coordination, and integration costs—costs sufficient to consume the originally imagined efficiency gains. So when a project is already behind schedule, adding more people usually isn't a remedy—it makes it slower.

Brooks was talking about human teams. But the AI era validates his argument from the opposite direction: AI has reduced communication costs to zero. No onboarding needed, no alignment meetings, 24/7 continuous work, zero context switch costs. When coordination costs approach zero, parallel execution becomes truly efficient for the first time—but measurement systems built on the assumption that "coordination is expensive" simultaneously lose meaning.

Some have tried to replace man-days with "attention duration." This concept categorizes human cognitive investment in AI collaboration into four types:
• Startup attention: translating vague requirements into precise instructions;
• Supervisory attention: checking AI output and real-time course correction;
• Integration attention: coordinating scattered outputs from multiple AIs;
• Incubation attention: allowing unconscious background thinking to settle and inspire insights.

Such categorization is helpful, but "attention duration" is essentially still an input-side metric. It tells us how much cognitive resources were invested, but doesn't tell us what these resources actually produced, the quality, or how much value was created compared to traditional methods.

The current problem: input-side has "attention duration," macro-side has the [Anthropic Economic Index](https://www.anthropic.com/research), estimating AI's contribution to US labor productivity annual growth at approximately 1.0 percentage point; but at the individual level output-side—the level where hiring decisions happen, where freelancers are evaluated—it's still almost a complete void.

The measurement vacuum isn't at the margins, but at the very center.

## Global AI Competency Framework Survey: Why Is Nobody Testing Individual Output?

This vacuum isn't because nobody cares. Over the past two years, governments, international organizations, and academia worldwide have produced numerous AI competency frameworks. But they're all solving the same problem: can you use AI? Not one asks: what did you create with AI?

The [AI Fluency Framework](https://www.anthropic.com/research) developed by Anthropic in collaboration with Rick Dakan and Joseph Feller defines four core competencies (4D): Delegation, Description, Discernment, and Diligence. This is probably the most mature competency model currently available. But it describes "what good AI collaboration looks like," not quantifying "what it produced."

Anthropic's own Economic Index approaches from another angle, analyzing millions of real conversations to estimate AI's impact on labor productivity. The data is extremely valuable—but it operates at the statistical aggregate level, not the individual portfolio level.

The [US Department of Labor released an AI Literacy Framework in February 2026](https://www.dol.gov/). The [UK Turing Institute](https://www.turing.ac.uk/) published the third version of the AI Skills for Business Framework in late 2025. [UNESCO](https://www.unesco.org/) issued AI competency frameworks for students and teachers. Academia has scales for Collaborative AI Literacy and Collaborative AI Metacognition.

Their common characteristic: they test literacy (do you understand AI, can you use it appropriately, do you know its limitations), not performance (what did you actually produce with AI, what quality, how much more efficient than traditional methods).

If you plot existing frameworks on a 2×2 matrix—horizontal axis "Literacy vs Performance," vertical axis "Individual vs Macro"—you'll see the left side crowded with people (UNESCO, DOL, Turing, various academic scales), the upper right has the [Anthropic Economic Index](https://www.anthropic.com/research). The lower right—individual level, performance-based, verifiable—is empty.

Everyone is testing AI literacy. Nobody is testing individual AI productivity. This article proposes a framework to fill this gap. It's on the same thread as my previous article on [AI-era capability gaps](/articles/ai-capability-gap-2026)—that one discussed the existence of the gap, this one discusses how to quantify it.

## AI Collaboration Portfolio Five-Dimensional Model: What to Measure, How to Measure

AI Collaboration Portfolio is a five-dimensional model measuring what individuals actually produce through AI collaboration. Design principle: each dimension must have theoretical foundation, quantifiable metrics, and verifiable data sources from third parties.

### Command (25%)

Can you make AI do the right things?

Corresponds to Delegation and Description in the 4D Framework, and "startup attention" in attention theory. Anthropic found prompt quality correlates 0.92 with output quality—this might be the single most critical variable in AI collaboration effectiveness.

Quantitative metrics: number of reusable skills/workflows/system prompts, automated pipeline count (CI/CD, cron, GitHub Actions), AI tool integration breadth, complexity of multi-step task decomposition.

### Delivery (25%)

After AI collaboration, what did you actually ship?

Not what you tried, but what went live. Corresponds to task completion rate in the Economic Index.

Quantitative metrics: commit frequency and volume, number of deployed services/tools, published content count, complete zero-to-live projects, code quality indicators (PR merge rate, issue resolution speed).

### Leverage (20%)

How many times did you amplify the same cognitive input?

This is the core of "40 minutes vs. 15 man-days." Directly answers the question enterprises care most about: what's the team capacity multiplier when you join?

Quantitative metrics: actual cognitive input vs traditional estimation ratio, number of concurrent projects, AI tool orchestration count, automation coverage rate.

### Quality (15%)

Can your output withstand scrutiny?

In an era when AI can generate massive content, quality gatekeepers are unprecedentedly important. Corresponds to Discernment in the 4D Framework and reliability adjustment in the Economic Index—they found that factoring in task success rate reduces productivity improvement estimates by about one-third.

Quantitative metrics: user/traffic numbers, system uptime, number of quality control mechanisms (automated testing, verification SOPs, review processes), external citations/sharing count.

### Influence (15%)

Are your methods being learned or adopted by others?

The transition from individual contributor to organizational enabler. The highest leverage form of value creation.

Quantitative metrics: open source project interactions (stars, forks, contributors), skill/template adoption count, educational content reach, external methodology citations.

### Relationships Between Dimensions

To be honest: these five dimensions aren't completely independent. High Quality is often a precursor to Influence. A powerful automation pipeline (Leverage) is itself a form of Delivery. Causal relationships exist between dimensions, and precise attribution can sometimes be blurry.

This is a design feature, not a flaw. Real value creation is inherently multi-dimensional and interwoven. Revenue, gross profit, and net profit in financial statements also have causal relationships, but we don't look at just one number because of this—each number reveals different aspects. Five dimensions are five observation lenses, not five orthogonal axes. The goal is richer observation, not perfect decomposition.

## Anti-Fraud Mechanisms: Automated Capture, Structured Self-Assessment, AI Verification

Any competency framework's most common challenge is: can it be gamed?

This concern is completely valid. Goodhart's Law—when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure—applies to all quantitative systems. The question isn't whether it can be gamed, but whether the gaming cost is high enough.

AI Collaboration Portfolio responds with a three-tier evidence architecture:

**Tier 1: Automated Capture.** After user authorization, the system directly pulls data from third-party platforms—GitHub commit records, repo structure, CI/CD workflow files, website analytics, package download counts, community metrics. This data is recorded by independent platforms and cannot be tampered with by users. Your GitHub shows however many commits you have. This tier is the "hard evidence" baseline of the Portfolio.

**Tier 2: Structured Self-Assessment.** Parts that cannot be automatically captured (concurrent project count, traditional man-day estimates, AI tool integration methods) use structured forms to guide input. Each self-assessment field has an adjacent "evidence link" field. Those with attached links are marked *Evidenced*, those without are marked *Self-reported*. Anyone viewing your Portfolio can see these markings and judge credibility themselves.

**Tier 3: AI Verification.** After user GitHub authorization, AI analyzes repo structure and code patterns, independently suggesting scores for each dimension. AI suggested scores are displayed alongside user self-assessments. If you self-assess 90 but AI suggests 50, this gap gets visually flagged.

All three tiers work together: automated data can't be faked, self-assessments have transparent evidence marking, AI provides independent reference. This doesn't eliminate gaming—it raises gaming costs to unprofitable levels.

More critically, the framework's ultimate defense isn't internal verification, but external reality. Quality and Influence dimensions require evidence from outside user control—adoption rates, community interaction, customer feedback, market results. Teams can collude to game Jira ticket counts internally, but can't force markets to buy in or fake genuine open source community adoption.

This framework isn't an automatic judge. It's a forensic accounting system—an auditable ledger shifting evaluation from "whose story sounds better" to "whose evidence chain withstands questioning."

## Six Strongest Challenges and Responses: From Evidence Forgery to Survivorship Bias

We threw this framework into a multi-model adversarial debate engine for three rounds of stress testing. Here are the six most powerful attacks and our responses.

**Q1: AI can help people forge evidence chains.**

AI can forge maps, but can't forge memories of walking that path. In framework-guided deep interviews, interviewers ask not "what did you do" but "how did you make decisions"—why abandon that approach? How did you weigh token consumption trade-offs? How did you handle model hallucinations? People who actually did the work can answer three layers of follow-up. Those performing from scripts collapse at the third layer.

**Q2: Leverage's baseline (man-days) is something you said was obsolete—using it as denominator is circular reasoning.**

Leverage shouldn't be interpreted as a static multiplier against a fixed baseline. Its core value is the rate of change over time. An engineer achieving 10x leverage with GPT-4 in 2024 and still 10x with Claude Opus 4.6 in 2026—this itself exposes stagnation in adaptability. The framework measures growth slope, not absolute value. AI tools are evolving, baselines are moving (Red Queen Effect), what truly has predictive power is your speed of adapting to each paradigm shift.

**Q3: Why not add "Adaptability" and "Ethics" dimensions?**

Adaptability isn't an independent dimension—it's the derivative of five dimensions over time. When someone's Command, Delivery, and Leverage scores continuously improve across AI paradigm shifts, they demonstrate high adaptability. Same logic applies to "reflection loops"—the meta-skill of using AI to analyze and optimize one's own work patterns. It manifests as the upward trajectory of five dimensions over time, not a static score in a sixth column. Ethics is a baseline constraint, not a performance metric—ethics violations should result in immediate disqualification, not -15 points.

**Q4: Dimensions have causal relationships, preventing precise attribution.**

Acknowledged. See "Relationships Between Dimensions" above. The framework pursues richer observation, not mathematical orthogonality. Where attribution is ambiguous, the three-tier evidence architecture provides raw data for evaluators to judge themselves—this is precisely the framework's design intent: it doesn't replace human judgment, it gives human judgment better material.

**Q5: The framework focuses on individuals, but the highest value contributions in the AI era are often shared cognitive assets.**

This is a real limitation of v1. When someone builds a prompt library for the entire department to use, designs a workflow that doubles everyone's efficiency—this network effect indeed cannot be fully captured by individual dimensions. The Influence dimension partially touches this, but insufficiently. Team-version framework—measuring how a person amplifies system capacity, not just their own—is v2's most important evolution direction.

**Q6: Using yourself as a case study is survivorship bias.**

Methodologically completely valid. A framework's designer would naturally pick evidence favorable to themselves. The response isn't denying bias, but making bias structurally unimportant: every claim in the case below can be independently verified through public URLs, GitHub repos, or third-party analytics APIs. This case's value isn't statistical—it's engineering. It provides a complete, inspectable, replicable blueprint anyone can run with their own data. Think of it as open source release, not clinical trial. Initial versions inevitably reflect creator context, but their value depends on whether the community finds them worth forking, attacking, and improving.

## The Cost of Not Measuring AI Productivity: Capability Gaps Are Invisibly Widening

Why does this matter? Because the cost of not measuring is greater than measuring wrongly.

The [Anthropic Economic Index](https://www.anthropic.com/research) documents a deskilling effect: AI preferentially takes over high-skill components of work, leaving low-skill parts behind. If we can't identify who truly excels at AI collaboration, high-capability individuals will be systematically undervalued, while those who interview well but produce poorly will be overvalued.

Productivity gaps are real and widening. University-level tasks accelerated 12x by AI, high school level only 9x. This isn't a small gap gradually accumulating over time—this is structural divergence. And it's currently completely invisible to labor markets because no measurement system can reveal it.

On the topic "How should productivity be calculated in the AI era?", using multi-model cross-validation, several honest limitations became clearer:

**Individual vs System Value.** The framework measures individual output, but the most valuable contributions in the AI era are often shared cognitive assets—prompt libraries used by entire departments, automation workflows that accelerate everyone. Measuring how a person amplifies system capacity—not just their own—is v2's most important extension direction.

**Reflection Loops.** The highest-level skill in AI collaboration might be using AI to analyze and optimize one's own work patterns—a metacognitive ability that drives growth in all dimensions. Current framework captures this indirectly through score trajectories over time, but doesn't explicitly measure it. Future versions could treat self-iteration speed as a first-class signal.

**Cross-Organizational Portability.** AI collaboration occurs in extremely different cultural, legal, and economic contexts. Collectivist cultures might systematically suppress self-assessment scores. GDPR limits automated data capture. Small organizations can't afford AI verification infrastructure. A framework only usable in well-resourced English-language tech companies isn't truly universal.

**Fairness and Accessibility.** Those with access to latest AI tools naturally have advantages in this framework. If the measurement system itself amplifies digital divides rather than revealing capabilities, it fails.

These aren't flaws to hide. They're research directions for the next iteration round.

But the alternative to an imperfect framework isn't a perfect framework—it's having no framework at all. It's continuing to evaluate AI collaboration capabilities through resume keywords and interview impressions, in a world where "familiar with AI tools" and "can use AI to do an entire team's work alone" differ by an order of magnitude.

What can be quantified can be valued. What can't becomes invisible.

AI Collaboration Portfolio is a first attempt to make the invisible visible. It's an imperfect map. But the alternative isn't a better map—it's running blindfolded in a new continent.
