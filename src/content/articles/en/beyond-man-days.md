## 40 Minutes to Complete 15 Man-Days of Work: What Can Man-Days Still Measure?

*When one person's 40 minutes of cognitive input produces the traditional equivalent of 15 man-days of work—what exactly are we measuring?*

---

Three AIs running simultaneously. One handles strategic analysis, one writes code and deploys, one processes documents and operational tasks. The operator spends about 40 minutes—issuing commands, reviewing outputs, making decisions, adjusting direction. At the end, on the desk: a market analysis report, a refactored database, a literature review.

Traditional approach? Five people, three to four work days. 15 to 20 man-days.

But those 40 minutes weren't spent idle. They were high-density cognitive labor: decomposing vague objectives into executable AI instructions, deciding which AI does what, intercepting errors in real-time, integrating scattered outputs into a coherent whole.

So what exactly does "man-days" measure in this scenario? It measures the passage of time, not the creation of value. When our metrics can't keep up with reality, we're not measuring productivity—we're measuring attendance.

The [Anthropic Economic Index released in January 2026](https://www.anthropic.com/research) provides empirical foundation. They analyzed over a million Claude conversations and found prompt quality correlates with output quality at 0.92. Tasks requiring university-level comprehension show AI acceleration of 12x; high school level only 9x.

AI doesn't uniformly strengthen everyone. It exponentially amplifies capability gaps.

Two people's resumes both say "proficient with AI tools." One shipped an entire full-stack application over a weekend using AI, the other spent eight hours using ChatGPT to revise an email. Currently, we have no standardized way to distinguish between these two people.

## From The Mythical Man-Month to AI Collaboration: Why Attention Time Isn't Enough Either?

In 1975, computer scientist [Fred Brooks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fred_Brooks) published [*The Mythical Man-Month*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Mythical_Man-Month). His core insight: man-months are illusory. People and time aren't interchangeable because communication costs consume efficiency gains. Adding people to a late project only makes it later.

Brooks was talking about human teams. But the AI era validates his thesis from the opposite direction: AI reduces communication costs to zero. No onboarding needed, no alignment meetings, 24/7 continuous work, zero context switching costs. When coordination costs approach zero, parallel execution becomes truly efficient for the first time—but metrics based on the assumption that "coordination is expensive" simultaneously lose meaning.

Some have tried replacing man-days with "attention time." This concept divides human cognitive input in AI collaboration into four categories: activation attention (turning vague requirements into precise instructions), supervision attention (checking AI output and correcting course), integration attention (coordinating across multiple AI outputs), and incubation attention (unconscious background thinking that generates insights).

The categorization is useful, but attention time remains an input-side metric. It tells us how much cognitive resources you spent, not what those resources produced, what quality was achieved, or how much value was created relative to traditional methods.

The current situation: input side has attention time, macro side has the Anthropic Economic Index (estimating AI contributes approximately 1.0 percentage point annually to US labor productivity growth), but the personal level output side—the level where hiring decisions are made, where freelancers are evaluated—is completely blank.

The measurement vacuum isn't at the edges, it's at the center.

## Global AI Capability Framework Landscape: Why Is No One Testing Personal Output?

This vacuum isn't due to lack of interest. Over the past two years, governments worldwide, international organizations, and academia have produced extensive AI capability frameworks. But they're all solving the same problem: Can you use AI? None are asking: What have you produced with AI?

Anthropic's [AI Fluency Framework](https://www.anthropic.com/research), developed with Rick Dakan and Joseph Feller, defines four core capabilities (4D): Delegation, Description, Discernment, and Diligence. This is probably the most mature capability model currently available. But it describes "what good AI collaboration looks like," not quantifying "what it produces."

Anthropic's own Economic Index approaches from another angle, analyzing millions of real conversations to estimate AI's impact on labor productivity. The data is extremely valuable—but it operates at the statistical aggregate level, not the personal portfolio level.

The [US Department of Labor released an AI Literacy Framework in February 2026](https://www.dol.gov/). The [UK Turing Institute](https://www.turing.ac.uk/) published the third version of the AI Skills for Business Framework in late 2025. [UNESCO](https://www.unesco.org/) released AI competency frameworks for students and teachers. Academia has scales for Collaborative AI Literacy and Collaborative AI Metacognition.

Their common characteristic: they test literacy (do you understand AI, can you use it appropriately, do you know its limitations), not performance (what have you actually produced with AI, what quality, how much more efficient than traditional methods).

Place existing frameworks on a 2×2 matrix—horizontal axis is "literacy vs performance," vertical axis is "personal vs macro"—you'll see the left side crowded with people (UNESCO, DOL, Turing, various academic scales), the upper right has the Anthropic Economic Index. The lower right—personal level, performance-based, verifiable—is empty.

Everyone is testing AI literacy. No one is testing personal AI productivity. This article proposes a framework to fill this gap. It's on the same thread as my previous article on [AI-era capability gaps](/articles/ai-capability-gap-2026)—that one was about the existence of gaps, this one is about how to quantify them.

## AI Collaboration Portfolio Five-Dimensional Model: What to Measure, How to Measure

AI Collaboration Portfolio is a five-dimensional model measuring what individuals actually produce through AI collaboration. Design principle: each dimension must have theoretical foundation, quantifiable metrics, and verifiable data sources from third parties.

### Command (25%)

Can you make AI do the right things?

Corresponds to Delegation and Description in the 4D Framework, and "activation attention" in attention theory. Anthropic found prompt quality correlates with output quality at 0.92—this may be the single most critical variable in AI collaboration effectiveness.

Quantitative indicators: number of reusable skills/workflows/system prompts, automated pipeline count (CI/CD, cron, GitHub Actions), AI tool integration breadth, complexity of multi-step task decomposition.

### Delivery (25%)

After AI collaboration, what did you actually ship?

Not what you tried, but what went live. Corresponds to task completion rate in the Economic Index.

Quantitative indicators: commit frequency and volume, number of deployed services/tools, published content count, complete zero-to-production projects, code quality metrics (PR merge rate, issue resolution speed).

### Leverage (20%)

For the same cognitive input, how much amplification was achieved?

This is the core of "40 minutes vs. 15 man-days." It directly answers the question enterprises care about most: after you join, what's the team's productivity multiplier?

Quantitative indicators: actual cognitive input vs traditional estimation ratio, number of parallel projects, AI tool orchestration count, automation coverage rate.

### Quality (15%)

Does your output withstand scrutiny?

In an era when AI can mass-generate content, quality gatekeepers are unprecedentedly important. Corresponds to Discernment in the 4D Framework and reliability adjustment in the Economic Index—they found that factoring in task success rates reduces productivity improvement estimates by about one-third.

Quantitative indicators: user/traffic numbers, system uptime, quality control mechanisms (automated testing, verification SOPs, review processes), external citations/shares.

### Influence (15%)

Are your methods being learned or adopted by others?

The transition from individual contributor to organizational enabler. The highest-leverage form of value creation.

Quantitative indicators: open source project interactions (stars, forks, contributors), skill/template adoption count, educational content reach, external citations of methodologies.

### Relationships Between Dimensions

Honestly: these five dimensions are not completely independent. High Quality is often a precursor to Influence. A powerful automation pipeline (Leverage) is itself a form of Delivery. Causal relationships exist between dimensions, making precise attribution sometimes unclear.

This is a design feature, not a flaw. Real value creation is inherently multi-dimensional and intertwined. Revenue, gross profit, and net profit in financial statements also have causal relationships, but we don't therefore look at only one number, because each reveals different aspects. The five dimensions are five observation lenses, not five orthogonal axes. The goal is richer observation, not perfect decomposition.

## Anti-Fraud Mechanisms: Auto-Fetch, Structured Self-Assessment, AI Verification

The most common challenge to any capability framework: Can it be gamed?

This concern is completely valid. Goodhart's Law—when a metric becomes a target, it ceases to be a good metric—applies to all quantification systems. The question isn't whether it can be gamed, but whether the cost of gaming is high enough.

AI Collaboration Portfolio responds with a three-tier evidence architecture:

**Tier 1: Auto-fetch.** After user authorization, the system directly pulls data from third-party platforms—GitHub commit records, repo structure, CI/CD workflow files, website analytics, package download counts, community metrics. This data is recorded by independent platforms; users cannot tamper with it. If your GitHub has X commits, it has X commits. This tier is the Portfolio's "hard evidence" baseline.

**Tier 2: Structured self-assessment.** Parts that can't be auto-fetched (number of parallel projects, traditional man-day estimates, AI tool integration methods) use structured forms to guide completion. Each self-assessment field has an accompanying "evidence link" field. Items with links are marked as *Evidenced*, those without as *Self-reported*. Anyone viewing your Portfolio can see this marking and judge credibility themselves.

**Tier 3: AI verification.** After user authorizes GitHub, AI analyzes repo structure and code patterns, independently suggesting scores for each dimension. AI-suggested scores are displayed alongside user self-assessments. If you self-assess 90 points but AI suggests 50, this discrepancy is visually flagged.

Three tiers working together: auto data can't be faked, self-assessments have transparent evidence marking, AI provides independent reference. This doesn't eliminate gaming, it raises gaming costs to uneconomical levels.

More critically, the framework's ultimate defense isn't internal validation but external reality. Quality and Influence dimensions require evidence from outside user control—user adoption rates, community interaction, customer feedback, market results. Teams can collude to inflate Jira ticket counts, but can't force market adoption or fake genuine open source community usage.

This framework isn't an automatic verdict machine. It's a forensic accounting system—an auditable ledger that shifts evaluation from "who tells the best story" to "whose evidence chain withstands scrutiny."

## Six Strongest Challenges and Responses: From Evidence Forgery to Survivorship Bias

We put this framework through a multi-model adversarial debate engine for three rounds of stress testing. Here are the six most powerful attacks and our responses.

**Q1: AI can help people forge evidence chains.**

AI can forge maps, but can't forge the memory of walking that path. In framework-guided deep interviews, interviewers ask not "what did you do" but "how did you make decisions"—why abandon that approach? How did you consider token consumption tradeoffs? How did you handle model hallucinations? People who actually did it can answer three layers of follow-up. Those following scripts collapse at the third layer.

**Q2: Leverage's baseline (man-days) is something you said is obsolete; using it as a denominator is circular reasoning.**

Leverage shouldn't be interpreted as a static multiplier against a fixed baseline. Its core value is the rate of change over time. An engineer achieving 10x leverage with GPT-4 in 2024 and still 10x with Claude Opus 4.6 in 2026—this itself reveals adaptation stagnation. The framework tests growth slope, not absolute values. AI tools are evolving, baselines are moving (Red Queen effect), what truly predicts is your speed of adapting to each paradigm shift.

**Q3: Why not add "adaptability" and "ethics" dimensions?**

Adaptability isn't an independent dimension, it's the time derivative of the five dimensions. When someone's Command, Delivery, Leverage scores continuously improve across AI paradigm shifts, they demonstrate high adaptability. Same logic applies to "reflection loops"—the meta-skill of using AI to analyze and optimize one's own work patterns. It manifests as the upward trajectory of the five dimensions over time, not a static score in a sixth column. Ethics is a baseline constraint, not a performance metric—ethical violations should result in immediate disqualification, not a 15-point deduction.

**Q4: Dimensions have causal relationships, making precise attribution impossible.**

Acknowledged. See "Relationships Between Dimensions" above. The framework pursues richer observation, not mathematical orthogonality. Where attribution is unclear, the three-tier evidence architecture provides raw data for evaluators to judge themselves—this is the framework's design intent: it doesn't replace human judgment, it gives human judgment better materials.

**Q5: The framework focuses on individuals, but the highest value contributions in the AI era are often shared cognitive assets.**

This is a real limitation of v1. When someone builds a prompt library for the entire department to use, designs a workflow that doubles everyone's efficiency—these network effects indeed can't be fully captured by individual dimensions. The Influence dimension partially touches this, but not enough. A team version framework—measuring how someone amplifies system productivity, not just their own—is the most important evolutionary direction for v2.

**Q6: The author using himself as a case study is survivorship bias.**

Methodologically completely valid. A framework designer will naturally select evidence favorable to themselves. The response isn't to deny bias, but to make bias structurally irrelevant: every claim in the case study below can be independently verified through public URLs, GitHub repos, or third-party analysis APIs. This case study's value isn't statistical—it's engineering. It provides a complete, inspectable, replicable blueprint anyone can run with their own data. Think of it as open source release, not clinical trial. Initial versions inevitably reflect the creator's context, but their value depends on whether the community finds them worth forking, attacking, improving.

## The Cost of Not Measuring AI Productivity: Capability Gaps Are Invisibly Widening

Why does this matter? Because the cost of not measuring is greater than the cost of measuring wrong.

The Anthropic Economic Index documents a deskilling effect: AI preferentially takes over high-skill components of work, leaving low-skill parts. If we can't identify who truly excels at AI collaboration, high-capability individuals will be systematically undervalued while those who interview well but produce poorly will be overvalued.

Productivity gaps are real and widening. University-level tasks are accelerated 12x by AI, high school level only 9x. This isn't a small gap that accumulates slowly over time—it's structural divergence. And it's currently completely invisible to labor markets because no measurement system exists to reveal it.

Regarding this topic "How should productivity be calculated in the AI era?", using multi-model cross-validation, several honest limitations became clearer:

**Individual vs System Value.** The framework measures individual output, but the most valuable contributions in the AI era are often shared cognitive assets—prompt libraries used by entire departments, automated workflows that accelerate everyone. Measuring how someone amplifies system productivity—not just their own—is the most important extension direction for v2.

**Reflection Loops.** The highest-order skill in AI collaboration may be using AI to analyze and optimize one's own work patterns—a meta-cognitive ability that drives growth across all dimensions. The current framework captures this indirectly through score trajectories over time, but doesn't explicitly measure it. Future versions could treat self-iteration speed as a first-class signal.

**Cross-organizational Portability.** AI collaboration occurs in vastly different cultural, legal, and economic contexts. Collectivist cultures might systematically depress self-assessment scores. GDPR limits automated data fetching. Small organizations can't afford AI verification infrastructure. A framework that only works in resource-rich English-speaking tech companies isn't truly universal.

**Fairness and Accessibility.** People with access to the latest AI tools have natural advantages in this framework. If the measurement system itself amplifies digital divides rather than revealing capabilities, it fails.

These aren't flaws to hide. They're research directions for the next iteration.

But the alternative to an imperfect framework isn't a perfect framework—it's no framework at all. It's continuing to evaluate AI collaboration capabilities based on resume keywords and interview impressions, in a world where "familiar with AI tools" and "can use AI to single-handedly do an entire team's work" differ by an order of magnitude.

What can be quantified can be valued. What cannot becomes invisible.

AI Collaboration Portfolio is a first attempt to make the invisible visible. It's an imperfect map. But the alternative isn't a better map—it's running blindfolded in a new continent.
