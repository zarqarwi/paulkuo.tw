## 40 Minutes for 15 Man-Days of Work: What Can Man-Days Still Measure?

*If 40 minutes of deep thinking can create output equivalent to 15 traditional man-days—then are our methods of measuring work already obsolete?*

---

Three AIs running simultaneously. One handles strategic analysis, one writes code and deploys, one processes documents and operational tasks. The operator spent about 40 minutes—giving instructions, reviewing output, making judgments, adjusting direction. When finished, there was a market analysis report, a refactored database, and a literature review on the desk.

Traditional approach? Five people, three to four working days. 15 to 20 man-days.

But those 40 minutes weren't idle time—they were high-density cognitive labor: decomposing vague objectives, orchestrating AI division of labor, real-time error correction, integrating scattered outputs until results took shape.

So what exactly did "man-days" measure here? It measured time, not value. When measurement methods lag behind reality, what we measure isn't productivity—just whether people showed up (attendance).

[Anthropic's Economic Index released in January 2026](https://www.anthropic.com/research) provides empirical foundation. They analyzed over one million Claude conversations and found prompt quality correlates 0.92 with output quality. For tasks requiring college-level comprehension, AI acceleration is 12x; for high school level, only 9x.

AI doesn't uniformly strengthen everyone. It exponentially amplifies capability gaps.

Two people's resumes both say "familiar with AI tools," but behind that phrase may lie vastly different capabilities.
One person can use AI to ship a full-stack application over a weekend, while another spends eight hours using ChatGPT to revise one email.
The problem isn't just the capability gap itself, but that until now, we still lack a way to identify, measure, or even name this gap.

## From The Mythical Man-Month to AI Collaboration: Why Attention Duration Isn't Enough Either?

In 1975, computer scientist [Fred Brooks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fred_Brooks) published [*The Mythical Man-Month*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Mythical_Man-Month), with its most classic insight being the debunking of the "man-month" myth: people and time were never interchangeable variables. Adding each person doesn't just increase capacity—it simultaneously increases communication, coordination, and integration costs, and these costs can consume the efficiency gains originally imagined. So when a project is already behind schedule, adding more people usually isn't a remedy—it makes things slower.

Brooks was talking about human teams. But the AI era validates his point from the opposite direction: AI reduces communication costs to zero. No onboarding needed, no alignment meetings, 24/7 availability, zero context switch cost. When coordination costs approach zero, parallel execution becomes truly efficient for the first time—but metrics built on the assumption that "coordination is expensive" simultaneously lose meaning.

Some have tried using "attention duration" to replace man-days. This concept divides human cognitive input in AI collaboration into four categories:
• Initiation attention: translating vague requirements into precise instructions;
• Supervision attention: checking AI output and real-time course correction;
• Integration attention: coordinating scattered outputs from multiple AIs;
• Incubation attention: letting unconscious background thinking settle and catalyze insights.

Such categorization is helpful, but "attention duration" is essentially still an input-side indicator. It tells us how much cognitive resource was invested, but doesn't tell us what those resources actually produced, the quality, or how much value was created compared to traditional approaches.

The current problem is: the input side has "attention duration," the macro side has the [Anthropic Economic Index](https://www.anthropic.com/research), estimating AI's contribution to U.S. labor productivity annual growth at about 1.0 percentage point; but at the individual level on the output side—the level where hiring decisions happen, where freelancers are evaluated—it's almost still a blank space.

The vacuum in metrics isn't at the periphery, but right in the center.

## Global AI Capability Framework Survey: Why Is Nobody Testing Individual Output?

This vacuum isn't because nobody cares. Over the past two years, governments, international organizations, and academia have produced numerous AI capability frameworks. But they're all solving the same problem: Can you use AI? None are asking: What have you created with AI?

Anthropic's [AI Fluency Framework](https://www.anthropic.com/research), developed with Rick Dakan and Joseph Feller, defines four core capabilities (4D): Delegation, Description, Discernment, and Diligence. This is probably the most mature capability model currently. But it describes "what good AI collaboration looks like," not quantifying "what it produced."

Anthropic's own Economic Index approaches from another angle, analyzing millions of real conversations to estimate AI's impact on labor productivity. The data is extremely valuable—but it operates at the statistical aggregate level, not the individual Portfolio level.

The [U.S. Department of Labor](https://www.dol.gov/) released an AI Literacy Framework, the [UK Turing Institute](https://www.turing.ac.uk/) launched the third edition of "AI Skills for Business Framework," [UNESCO](https://www.unesco.org/) has AI capability frameworks for students and teachers, and academia has proposed scales like Collaborative AI Literacy and Collaborative AI Metacognition.

Many frameworks exist, but most answer the same type of question: Do you understand AI, can you use it reasonably, do you know its limitations? That is, they test literacy.

What's truly missing is performance-level measurement: What have you actually produced with AI, what's the quality of results, how much efficiency and value improvement compared to traditional approaches.

Place existing frameworks on a 2×2 matrix—horizontal axis "literacy vs performance," vertical axis "individual vs macro"—you'll see the left side crowded with people (UNESCO, DOL, Turing, various academic scales), upper right corner has the [Anthropic Economic Index](https://www.anthropic.com/research). The lower right corner—individual level, performance-based, verifiable—is empty.

Everyone is testing AI literacy. Nobody is testing individual AI productivity. This article proposes a framework to fill this gap. It's on the same line as my previous article on [AI-era capability gaps](/articles/ai-capability-gap-2026)—that piece talked about the existence of the gap, this one talks about how to quantify it.

## AI Collaboration Portfolio Five-Dimensional Model: What to Measure, How to Measure

AI Collaboration Portfolio is a five-dimensional model measuring what individuals actually produce through AI collaboration. Design principle: each dimension must have theoretical foundation, quantifiable indicators, and verifiable data sources from third parties.

### Command (25%)

Can you make AI do the right things?

Corresponds to Delegation and Description in the 4D Framework, and "initiation attention" in attention theory. Anthropic found prompt quality correlates 0.92 with output quality—this may be the single most critical variable for AI collaboration effectiveness.

Quantitative indicators: number of reusable skills/workflows/system prompts, number of automation pipelines (CI/CD, cron, GitHub Actions), breadth of AI tool integration, complexity of multi-step task decomposition.

### Delivery (25%)

After AI collaboration, what did you actually ship?

Not what you tried, but what went live. Corresponds to task completion rate in the Economic Index.

Quantitative indicators: commit frequency and volume, number of deployed services/tools, published content count, complete zero-to-production projects, code quality metrics (PR merge rate, issue resolution speed).

### Leverage (20%)

For the same cognitive input, how many times did you amplify it?

This is the core of "40 minutes vs. 15 man-days." Directly answers the question enterprises care most about: After you join, what's the team's capacity multiplier?

Quantitative indicators: ratio of actual cognitive input vs traditional estimates, number of parallel projects, number of AI tools orchestrated, automation coverage rate.

### Quality (15%)

Does your output withstand scrutiny?

In an era when AI can mass-generate content, quality gatekeepers are unprecedentedly important. Corresponds to Discernment in the 4D Framework and reliability adjustments in the Economic Index—they found that factoring in task success rates reduces productivity improvement estimates by about one-third.

Quantitative indicators: user/traffic numbers, system uptime, number of quality control mechanisms (automated testing, verification SOPs, review processes), external citations/shares.

### Influence (15%)

Have your methods been learned or adopted by others?

The transformation from individual contributor to organizational enabler. The highest-leverage form of value creation.

Quantitative indicators: open source project interactions (stars, forks, contributors), skill/template adoption count, teaching content reach, external methodology citations.

### Relationships Between Dimensions

Honestly: these five dimensions aren't completely independent. High Quality is often a precursor to Influence. A powerful automation pipeline (Leverage) is itself a form of Delivery. Causal relationships exist between dimensions, and precise attribution can sometimes be fuzzy.

This is a design feature, not a flaw. Real value creation is inherently multi-dimensional and interwoven. Revenue, gross profit, and net profit in financial statements also have causal relationships, but we don't look at just one number because each reveals different aspects. Five dimensions are five observation lenses, not five orthogonal axes. The goal is richer observation, not perfect decomposition.

## Anti-Fraud Mechanisms: Automated Collection, Structured Self-Assessment, AI Validation

The most common criticism of any capability framework is whether it will devolve into score manipulation games.

This concern is completely valid. Goodhart's Law long ago pointed out: when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure. No quantitative system can be exempt from this rule. What really needs questioning isn't whether it can be gamed, but whether the cost of gaming is high enough to make manipulation unprofitable.

AI Collaboration Portfolio responds with a three-layer evidence architecture:

**Layer 1: Automated collection.** After user authorization, the system directly pulls data from third-party platforms—GitHub commit records, repo structure, CI/CD workflow files, website analytics, package download counts, community metrics. This data is recorded by independent platforms and cannot be manipulated by users. The number of commits on your GitHub is what it is. This layer is the Portfolio's "hard evidence" baseline.

**Layer 2: Structured self-assessment.** For parts that cannot be automatically collected (number of parallel projects, traditional man-day estimates, AI tool integration methods), structured forms guide completion. Each self-assessment field has an "evidence link" field beside it. Those with attached links are marked *Evidenced*, those without are marked *Self-reported*. Anyone viewing your Portfolio can see this marking and judge credibility themselves.

**Layer 3: AI validation.** After users authorize GitHub, AI analyzes repo structure and code patterns, independently suggesting scores for each dimension. AI-suggested scores are displayed alongside user self-assessments. If you self-assess 90 points but AI suggests 50, this gap is visually highlighted.

Three layers working together: automated data can't be faked, self-assessments have transparent evidence marking, AI provides independent reference. This doesn't eliminate gaming—it raises gaming costs to unprofitable levels.

More critically, this framework's ultimate defense isn't internal validation, but external reality.
The Quality and Influence dimensions require evidence from beyond user control: user adoption rates, community interaction, customer feedback, market results. Internal teams might collude to inflate Jira ticket counts, but they can't force market adoption or fake genuine open source community uptake.

This framework isn't an automatic judge, but more like a forensic accounting system: an auditable ledger that shifts evaluation from "whose story sounds better" to "whose evidence chain withstands more scrutiny."

## Six Strongest Challenges and Responses: From Evidence Forgery to Survivorship Bias

We put this framework through a multi-model adversarial debate engine for three rounds of stress testing. Below are the six most powerful attacks and our responses.

**Q1: AI can help people forge evidence chains.**

AI can forge maps, but can't forge the memory of walking that path. In framework-guided deep interviews, interviewers don't ask "what did you do" but "how did you make decisions"—why abandon that method? How did you consider token consumption trade-offs? How did you handle model hallucinations? People who actually did it can answer three layers of follow-up. Those following scripts collapse at the third layer.

**Q2: Leverage's baseline (man-days) is something you said was ineffective, using it as denominator is circular reasoning.**

Leverage shouldn't be interpreted as a static multiplier against a fixed baseline. Its core value is the rate of change over time. An engineer who achieved 10x leverage with GPT-4 in 2024 and still 10x with Claude Opus 4.6 in 2026—this itself exposes stagnant adaptability. The framework tests growth slope, not absolute values. AI tools evolve, baselines shift (Red Queen effect), what truly has predictive power is your speed of adapting to each paradigm shift.

**Q3: Why not add "Adaptability" and "Ethics" dimensions?**

Adaptability isn't an independent dimension—it's the derivative of five dimensions over time. Someone whose Command, Delivery, Leverage scores continuously improve between AI paradigm shifts is highly adaptable. Same logic applies to "reflection loops"—the meta-skill of using AI to analyze and optimize one's own work patterns. It manifests as upward trajectories of five dimensions over time, not a static score in column six. Ethics is a baseline constraint, not a performance indicator—ethical violations should result in immediate disqualification, not a 15-point deduction.

**Q4: Causal relationships exist between dimensions, precise attribution impossible.**

Acknowledged. See "Relationships Between Dimensions" above. The framework pursues richer observation, not mathematical orthogonality. Where attribution is fuzzy, the three-layer evidence architecture provides raw data for evaluators to judge themselves—this is precisely the framework's design intent: it doesn't replace human judgment, it gives human judgment better materials.

**Q5: The framework focuses on individuals, but highest-value contributions in the AI era are often shared cognitive assets.**

This is a real limitation of the current version. Someone building a prompt library for the entire department, designing a workflow that doubles everyone's efficiency—this network effect indeed cannot be fully captured by individual dimensions. The Influence dimension partially addresses this, but inadequately. Team-version framework—measuring how someone amplifies system capacity, not just their own—is the most important evolutionary direction for the next phase.

**Q6: Author using himself as case study is survivorship bias.**

Methodologically completely valid. A framework designer would naturally select evidence favorable to themselves. The response isn't denying bias, but making bias structurally unimportant: every claim in the case below can be independently verified through public URLs, GitHub repos, or third-party analytics APIs. This case's value isn't statistical—it's engineering. It provides a complete, reviewable, replicable blueprint anyone can run with their own data. Think of it as open source release, not clinical trial. Initial versions inevitably reflect the creator's context, but value depends on whether the community finds it worth forking, attacking, improving.

## The Cost of Not Measuring AI Productivity: Capability Gaps Are Invisibly Expanding

Why does this matter? Because the cost of not measuring is greater than measuring wrong.

The [Anthropic Economic Index](https://www.anthropic.com/research) documents a deskilling effect: AI preferentially takes over high-skill components of work, leaving low-skill parts. If we cannot identify who truly excels at AI collaboration, high-capability individuals will be systematically undervalued, while those who interview well but produce poorly will be overvalued.

Productivity gaps are real and expanding. College-level tasks accelerated 12x by AI, high school level only 9x. This isn't a small gap slowly accumulating over time—this is structural divergence. And currently completely invisible to labor markets because no measurement system can reveal it.

Regarding this topic "In the AI era, how should productivity be calculated?", I used [cross-model comparative validation](/articles/beyond-man-days/#six-strongest-challenges-and-responses-from-evidence-forgery-to-survivorship-bias), several limitations became clearer:

**Individual vs System Value.** This framework measures individual output, but truly high-value contributions in the AI era are often shared cognitive assets: a prompt library used by the entire department, an automation workflow that accelerates everyone. How to measure someone's amplification effect on system capacity, not just their own output, is the most important extension direction for the next phase.

**Reflection Loops.** The highest-level skill in AI collaboration might not be operating AI, but using AI to analyze and optimize one's own work patterns: a meta-cognitive ability that drives growth across all dimensions. Current framework can only indirectly capture this through score changes over time, not yet explicitly measuring. Future versions could incorporate self-iteration speed as first-class signal.

**B2B Pricing Commercial Reality.** The framework can change how enterprises evaluate talent internally, but cannot change how markets buy services. Most client procurement processes remain rigidly tied to "man-day pricing sheets"—you can use Portfolio to prove you completed 15 man-days of work in 40 minutes, but client systems have no field for this non-linear output. Internal talent assessment can lead, but for this framework to truly replace man-day pricing requires the entire business ecosystem's pricing conventions to transform together. This isn't a problem one tool can solve—it's an industry consensus shift requiring time.

**Cross-Organizational Standardization Difficulty.** "Supervisory" attention means completely different things in a 10-person startup versus a 5000-person enterprise. Command capability has verifiable GitHub commits for software engineers, but how do you measure it for marketing directors or designers? Different industries, functions, organizational sizes may have vastly different definitions for dimensions. The framework's five dimensions provide observational structure, but specific quantitative indicators and weighting must necessarily be locally calibrated by industry and function, rather than forcing one globally universal standard.

**Privacy Boundaries of Digital Traces.** The first layer of the three-layer evidence architecture is automated collection—but the line between "automatic" and "surveillance" depends on who controls the data. If employees voluntarily authorize and Portfolio belongs to individuals, it's an empowerment tool; if enterprises mandate deployment and managers unilaterally access, it becomes digital monitoring. This framework's design premise is "individual sovereignty"—you choose what to reveal—but during enterprise implementation, this line easily gets crossed. Technical architecture must lock data ownership to individuals from the start, otherwise Portfolio becomes a new-era time clock.

**Fairness and Accessibility.** Those with access to latest AI tools naturally have advantages in this framework. If the measurement system itself amplifies digital divides rather than revealing genuine capability, it fails.

These aren't flaws to hide, but research directions for the next iteration.

But remember: the choice we face isn't "this framework" vs "a more perfect framework," but "having a framework" vs "having nothing." What does a world without frameworks look like? Like now: enterprises screen people by resume keywords, make decisions based on interview impressions, completely unable to distinguish between "can use AI" and "can use AI to single-handedly carry an entire team"—that enormous gap.

What can be quantified gets valued. What can't becomes invisible.

AI Collaboration Portfolio is an attempt to make the invisible visible. It's an imperfect map: but the alternative isn't a better map—it's running blindfolded across a new continent.
