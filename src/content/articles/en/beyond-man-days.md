## 40 Minutes for 15 Person-Days of Work: What Can Man-Days Still Measure?

*If 40 minutes of deep thinking can create output equivalent to traditional 15 person-days—then are our ways of measuring work already obsolete?*

---

Three AIs running simultaneously. One handles strategy analysis, one writes and deploys code, one manages documentation and operational tasks. The operator spends about 40 minutes—giving commands, reviewing output, making judgments, adjusting direction. At the end, on the desk: a market analysis report, a refactored database, a literature review.

The traditional approach? Five people, three to four working days. 15 to 20 person-days.

But those 40 minutes weren't idle time—they were high-density cognitive labor: decomposing vague objectives, orchestrating AI division of labor, correcting errors in real-time, integrating scattered outputs until results took shape.

So what exactly do "person-days" measure here? They measure time, not value. When measurement methods lag behind reality, we're not measuring productivity—we're only measuring whether people show up (attendance).

[Anthropic's Economic Index, released in January 2026](https://www.anthropic.com/research), provides empirical foundations. They analyzed over one million Claude conversations and found the correlation between prompt quality and output quality reaches 0.92. For tasks requiring college-level comprehension, AI acceleration is 12x; for high school level, only 9x.

AI doesn't uniformly make everyone stronger. It exponentially amplifies capability gaps.

Two people's resumes both state "familiar with AI tools," but behind that phrase could be vastly different capabilities.
One person can ship a full-stack application over a weekend using AI, while another spends eight hours using ChatGPT to revise an email.
The problem isn't just the capability gap itself, but that until now, we still lack a system to recognize, measure, or even name this gap.

## From The Mythical Man-Month to AI Collaboration: Why Attention Span Isn't Enough

In 1975, computer scientist [Fred Brooks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fred_Brooks) published [*The Mythical Man-Month*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Mythical_Man-Month), with its most classic insight dismantling the "man-month" myth: people and time were never interchangeable variables. Adding each person doesn't just increase capacity—it simultaneously increases communication, coordination, and integration costs, and these costs can consume the originally imagined efficiency gains. So when a project is already behind, adding more people usually isn't a remedy—it makes it slower.

Brooks was talking about human teams. But the AI era validates his argument from the opposite direction: AI has made communication costs approach zero. No onboarding needed, no alignment meetings, 24/7 uninterrupted work, zero context switch costs. When coordination costs approach zero, parallel execution becomes truly efficient for the first time—but metrics built on the assumption that "coordination is expensive" simultaneously lose their meaning.

Some have tried to replace person-days with "attention span." This concept divides human cognitive investment in AI collaboration into four categories:
• Initiation attention: translating vague requirements into precise instructions;
• Supervision attention: checking AI output and correcting course in real-time;
• Integration attention: coordinating scattered outputs from multiple AIs;
• Incubation attention: letting unconscious background thinking settle and catalyze insights.

Such categorization is helpful, but "attention span" is essentially still an input-side metric. It tells us how much cognitive resources were invested, but doesn't tell us what these resources actually produced, their quality, or how much value they created compared to traditional approaches.

The current problem is: the input side has "attention span," the macro side has the Anthropic Economic Index estimating AI's contribution to US labor productivity annual growth at about 1.0 percentage point; but at the personal level output side—the level where hiring decisions happen, where freelancers are evaluated—it's almost still a complete void.

The vacuum in measurement isn't at the periphery, but at the very center.

## Global AI Capability Framework Survey: Why Is No One Testing Personal Output?

This vacuum isn't because no one cares. Over the past two years, governments worldwide, international organizations, and academia have produced numerous AI capability frameworks. But they're all solving the same problem: do you know how to use AI? None are asking: what did you accomplish using AI?

Anthropic's [AI Fluency Framework](https://www.anthropic.com/research), developed in collaboration with Rick Dakan and Joseph Feller, defines four core capabilities (4D): Delegation, Description, Discernment, and Diligence. This is probably the most mature capability model currently available. But it describes "what good AI collaboration looks like," not quantifying "what it produced."

Anthropic's own Economic Index approaches from another angle, analyzing millions of real conversations to estimate AI's impact on labor productivity. The data is extremely valuable—but it operates at the statistical aggregate level, not the personal portfolio level.

[The US Department of Labor released the AI Literacy Framework in February 2026](https://www.dol.gov/). [The UK's Turing Institute](https://www.turing.ac.uk/) released the third version of the AI Skills for Business Framework in late 2025. [UNESCO](https://www.unesco.org/) produced AI capability frameworks for students and teachers. Academia has scales for Collaborative AI Literacy and Collaborative AI Metacognition.

Their common characteristic: they test literacy (do you understand AI, can you use it appropriately, do you know its limitations), not performance (what did you actually produce using AI, how high was the quality, how much more efficient compared to traditional methods).

Put existing frameworks on a 2×2 matrix—horizontal axis is "literacy vs. performance," vertical axis is "individual vs. macro"—you'll see the left side crowded with people (UNESCO, DOL, Turing, various academic scales), the upper right corner has the Anthropic Economic Index. The lower right corner—individual level, performance-based, verifiable—is empty.

Everyone is testing AI literacy. No one is testing personal AI productivity. This article proposes a framework to fill this gap. It's on the same thread as my previous piece [AI Era Capability Gap](/articles/ai-capability-gap-2026)—that one discussed the existence of the gap, this one discusses how to quantify it.

## AI Collaboration Portfolio Five-Dimensional Model: What to Measure and How

AI Collaboration Portfolio is a five-dimensional model that measures what individuals actually produce through AI collaboration. Design principle: each dimension must have theoretical foundation, quantifiable metrics, and verifiable data sources from third parties.

### Command (25%)

Can you make AI do the right things?

Corresponds to Delegation and Description in the 4D Framework, and "initiation attention" in attention theory. Anthropic found prompt quality correlates with output quality at 0.92—this might be the single most critical variable in AI collaboration effectiveness.

Quantifiable metrics: number of reusable skills/workflows/system prompts, number of automated pipelines (CI/CD, cron, GitHub Actions), breadth of AI tool integration, complexity of multi-step task decomposition.

### Delivery (25%)

After AI collaboration, what did you actually ship?

Not what you tried, but what went live. Corresponds to task completion rate in the Economic Index.

Quantifiable metrics: commit frequency and volume, number of deployed services/tools, published content count, complete zero-to-production projects, code quality indicators (PR merge rate, issue resolution speed).

### Leverage (20%)

For the same cognitive investment, how much amplification did you achieve?

This is the core of "40 minutes vs. 15 person-days." Directly answers the question enterprises care most about: after you join, what's the team's productivity multiplier?

Quantifiable metrics: ratio of actual cognitive investment vs. traditional estimates, number of parallel projects, number of AI tools orchestrated, automation coverage rate.

### Quality (15%)

Can your output withstand scrutiny?

In an era where AI can generate content at scale, quality gatekeepers are unprecedentedly important. Corresponds to Discernment in the 4D Framework and reliability adjustments in the Economic Index—they found that accounting for task success rates reduces productivity improvement estimates by about one-third.

Quantifiable metrics: user/traffic numbers, system uptime, number of quality control mechanisms (automated testing, verification SOPs, review processes), external citations/shares.

### Influence (15%)

Have your methods been learned or adopted by others?

The transition from individual contributor to organizational enabler. The highest leverage form of value creation.

Quantifiable metrics: open source project interactions (stars, forks, contributors), skill/template adoption frequency, educational content reach, external methodology citations.

### Relationships Between Dimensions

Honestly: these five dimensions aren't completely independent. High Quality is often a precursor to Influence. A powerful automation pipeline (Leverage) is itself a form of Delivery. Causal relationships exist between dimensions, and precise attribution can sometimes be ambiguous.

This is a design feature, not a flaw. Real value creation is inherently multi-dimensional and interwoven. Revenue, gross profit, and net profit in financial statements also have causal relationships, but we don't therefore look at only one number, because each number reveals different aspects. The five dimensions are five observation lenses, not five orthogonal axes. The goal is richer observation, not perfect decomposition.

## Anti-Fraud Mechanisms: Automated Collection, Structured Self-Assessment, AI Verification

The most common challenge to any capability framework is: can it be gamed?

This concern is completely valid. Goodhart's Law—when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure—applies to all quantification systems. The question isn't whether it can be gamed, but whether the cost of gaming is high enough.

AI Collaboration Portfolio responds with a three-layer evidence architecture:

**Layer 1: Automated Collection.** After user authorization, the system directly pulls data from third-party platforms—GitHub commit records, repo structure, CI/CD workflow files, website analytics, package downloads, social metrics. This data is recorded by independent platforms and cannot be tampered with by users. The number of commits on your GitHub is what it is. This layer is the "hard evidence" baseline of the Portfolio.

**Layer 2: Structured Self-Assessment.** For parts that cannot be automatically collected (number of parallel projects, traditional person-day estimates, AI tool integration methods), structured forms guide completion. Each self-assessment field has an "evidence link" field beside it. Those with links are marked *Evidenced*, those without are marked *Self-reported*. Anyone viewing your Portfolio can see this marking and judge credibility themselves.

**Layer 3: AI Verification.** After GitHub authorization, AI analyzes repo structure and code patterns, independently suggesting scores for each dimension. AI suggested scores are displayed alongside user self-assessments. If you self-assess 90 points but AI suggests 50, this gap is visually highlighted.

The three layers work together: automated data cannot be faked, self-assessments have transparent evidence marking, AI provides independent reference. This doesn't eliminate gaming—it raises the cost of gaming to unprofitable levels.

More critically, the framework's ultimate defense isn't in internal verification, but external reality. Quality and Influence dimensions require evidence from beyond user control—user adoption rates, community interactions, customer feedback, market results. Internal teams can collude to inflate Jira ticket numbers, but they can't force the market to buy, nor can they fake genuine adoption by open source communities.

This framework isn't an automatic judge. It's a forensic accounting system—an auditable ledger that shifts evaluation from "who tells the best story" to "whose evidence chain withstands scrutiny."

## Six Strongest Challenges and Responses: From Evidence Fabrication to Survivor Bias

We put this framework through a multi-model adversarial debate engine for three rounds of stress testing. Below are the six most powerful attacks and our responses.

**Q1: AI can help people fabricate evidence chains.**

AI can forge maps, but it cannot forge the memory of having walked that path. In framework-guided in-depth interviews, interviewers pursue not "what did you do" but "how did you make decisions"—why did you abandon that approach? How did you consider token consumption trade-offs? How did you handle model hallucinations? People who actually did the work can answer three layers of follow-up questions. Those following scripts collapse at the third layer.

**Q2: Leverage's baseline (person-days) is something you yourself said is ineffective; using it as denominator is circular reasoning.**

Leverage shouldn't be interpreted as a static multiplier against a fixed baseline. Its core value is the rate of change over time. An engineer achieving 10x leverage with GPT-4 in 2024 and still 10x with Claude Opus 4.6 in 2026—this itself exposes stagnation in adaptability. The framework measures growth slope, not absolute values. AI tools evolve, baselines shift (Red Queen effect), what truly has predictive power is your speed of adapting to each paradigm shift.

**Q3: Why not add "Adaptability" and "Ethics" dimensions?**

Adaptability isn't an independent dimension—it's the derivative of the five dimensions with respect to time. When someone's Command, Delivery, and Leverage scores continuously improve between AI paradigm shifts, they demonstrate high adaptability. The same logic applies to "reflection loops"—the meta-skill of using AI to analyze and optimize one's own work patterns. It manifests as the upward trajectory of the five dimensions over time, not as a static score in a sixth column. Ethics is a baseline constraint, not a performance indicator—ethical violations should result in direct disqualification, not a 15-point deduction.

**Q4: Dimensions have causal relationships and cannot be precisely attributed.**

Acknowledged. See "Relationships Between Dimensions" above. The framework pursues richer observation, not mathematical orthogonality. Where attribution is ambiguous, the three-layer evidence architecture provides raw data for evaluators to judge themselves—this is precisely the framework's design intent: it doesn't replace human judgment, it gives human judgment better materials.

**Q5: The framework focuses on individuals, but the highest value contributions in the AI era are often shared cognitive assets.**

This is a genuine limitation of v1. When someone builds a prompt library that the entire department can use, designs a workflow that doubles everyone's efficiency—this network effect indeed cannot be fully captured by individual dimensions. The Influence dimension partially addresses this, but not sufficiently. A team version framework—measuring how a person amplifies system capacity not just their own—is the most important evolution direction for v2.

**Q6: The author using himself as a case study is survivor bias.**

Methodologically completely valid. A framework's designer would naturally select evidence favorable to themselves. The response isn't to deny bias, but to make bias structurally unimportant: every claim in the case study below can be independently verified through public URLs, GitHub repos, or third-party analytics APIs. This case's value isn't statistical—it's engineering. It provides a complete, reviewable, replicable blueprint that anyone can run with their own data. Think of it as an open source release, not a clinical trial. Initial versions inevitably reflect the creator's context, but their value depends on whether the community finds them worth forking, attacking, and improving.

## The Cost of Not Measuring AI Productivity: Capability Gaps Are Invisibly Expanding

Why does this matter? Because the cost of not measuring is greater than measuring wrong.

The Anthropic Economic Index documents a deskilling effect: AI preferentially takes over high-skill components of work, leaving low-skill parts behind. If we cannot identify who truly excels at AI collaboration, high-capability individuals will be systematically undervalued, while those who interview well but produce poorly will be overvalued.

Productivity gaps are real and expanding. College-level tasks are accelerated 12x by AI, high school level only 9x. This isn't a small gap that accumulates slowly over time—this is structural divergence. And it's currently completely invisible to labor markets because no measurement system can reveal it.

Regarding this topic "How should productivity be calculated in the AI era?", using multi-model cross-validation, several honest limitations became clearer:

**Individual vs. System Value.** The framework measures individual output, but the most valuable contributions in the AI era are often shared cognitive assets—prompt libraries used by entire departments, automation workflows that accelerate everyone. Measuring how a person amplifies system capacity—not just their own—is the most important extension direction for v2.

**Reflection Loops.** The highest-order skill in AI collaboration might be using AI to analyze and optimize one's own work patterns—a meta-cognitive ability that drives growth in all dimensions. The current framework captures this indirectly through score trajectories over time but doesn't explicitly measure it. Future versions could treat self-iteration speed as a first-class signal.

**Cross-Organizational Portability.** AI collaboration occurs in vastly different cultural, legal, and economic contexts. Collectivist cultures might systematically suppress self-assessment scores. GDPR limits automated data collection. Small organizations cannot afford AI verification infrastructure. A framework that only works in well-resourced English-speaking tech companies isn't truly universal.

**Fairness and Accessibility.** Those with access to the latest AI tools naturally have advantages in this framework. If the measurement system itself amplifies digital divides rather than revealing capabilities, it has failed.

These aren't flaws to hide. They're research directions for the next iteration round.

But the alternative to an imperfect framework isn't a perfect framework—it's having no framework at all. It's continuing to assess AI collaboration capabilities through resume keywords and interview impressions, in a world where "familiar with AI tools" and "can use AI to single-handedly do an entire team's work" differ by an order of magnitude.

What can be quantified can be valued. What cannot becomes invisible.

AI Collaboration Portfolio is the first attempt to make the invisible visible. It's an imperfect map. But the alternative isn't a better map—it's running blindfolded through a new continent.
