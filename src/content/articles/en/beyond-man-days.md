## 40 Minutes to Complete 15 Man-Days of Work: What Can Man-Days Still Measure?

*If 40 minutes of deep thinking can create output equivalent to traditional 15 man-days—then are our methods of measuring work already obsolete?*

---

Three AIs running simultaneously. One handling strategic analysis, one writing code and deploying, one managing documents and operational tasks. The operator spent about 40 minutes—giving commands, reviewing output, making decisions, adjusting direction. When finished, on the desk lay a market analysis report, a refactored database, and a literature review.

The traditional approach? Five people, three to four working days. 15 to 20 man-days.

But those 40 minutes weren't idle time—they were high-density cognitive labor: decomposing vague goals, orchestrating AI division of labor, real-time error correction, integrating scattered outputs until results took shape.

So what exactly do "man-days" measure here? They measure time, not value. When measurement methods lag behind reality, what we're measuring isn't productivity—it's just whether people are present (attendance).

[Anthropic's Economic Index published in January 2026](https://www.anthropic.com/research) provides empirical foundation. They analyzed over one million Claude conversations and found that prompt quality correlates with output quality at 0.92. Tasks requiring university-level comprehension show 12x acceleration with AI; high school level only 9x.

AI doesn't uniformly strengthen everyone. It exponentially amplifies capability gaps.

Two people's resumes both say "familiar with AI tools," but behind this phrase lie vastly different capability gaps.
One person can ship a full-stack application using AI over a weekend, while another spends eight hours using ChatGPT to modify an email.
The problem isn't just the capability gap itself, but that until now, we still lack a way to recognize, measure, or even name this gap.

## From The Mythical Man-Month to AI Collaboration: Why Attention Duration Isn't Enough Either?

In 1975, computer scientist [Fred Brooks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fred_Brooks) published [*The Mythical Man-Month*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Mythical_Man-Month), with the most classic insight being the debunking of the "man-month" myth: people and time were never interchangeable variables. Each additional person not only increases capacity but simultaneously increases communication, coordination, and integration costs, and these costs are sufficient to consume the originally imagined efficiency gains. So when a project is already behind schedule, adding more people usually isn't a remedy—it makes things slower.

Brooks was talking about human teams. But the AI era has validated his argument from the opposite direction: AI has reduced communication costs to zero. No onboarding needed, no alignment meetings, 24-hour continuous work, zero context switch costs. When coordination costs approach zero, parallel execution truly becomes efficient for the first time—but measurement systems built on the assumption that "coordination is expensive" simultaneously lose their meaning.

Some have tried to replace man-days with "attention duration." This concept divides human cognitive investment in AI collaboration into four categories:
• Activation attention: translating vague requirements into precise instructions;
• Supervision attention: checking AI output and real-time course correction;
• Integration attention: coordinating multiple AIs' scattered outputs;
• Incubation attention: letting unconscious background thinking settle and catalyze inspiration.

Such categorization is helpful, but "attention duration" is essentially still an input-side indicator. It tells us how much cognitive resources were invested, but doesn't tell us what these resources actually produced, their quality, or how much value was created compared to traditional approaches.

The current problem is: the input side has "attention duration," the macro side has the [Anthropic Economic Index](https://www.anthropic.com/research), estimating AI's contribution to US labor productivity annual growth at approximately 1.0 percentage point; but at the personal level output side—the level where hiring decisions occur, where freelancers are evaluated—it remains almost entirely blank.

The measurement vacuum isn't at the periphery, but at the very center.

## Global AI Capability Framework Survey: Why Is No One Testing Personal Output?

This vacuum isn't because no one cares. Over the past two years, governments, international organizations, and academia worldwide have produced massive amounts of AI capability frameworks. But they're all solving the same problem: Can you use AI? None are asking: What have you created with AI?

Anthropic's [AI Fluency Framework](https://www.anthropic.com/research), developed in collaboration with Rick Dakan and Joseph Feller, defines four core capabilities (4D): Delegation, Description, Discernment, Diligence. This is probably the most mature capability model currently available. But it describes "what good AI collaboration looks like," not quantifying "what it produced."

Anthropic's own Economic Index cuts from another angle, analyzing millions of real conversations to estimate AI's impact on labor productivity. The data is extremely valuable—but it operates at the statistical aggregate level, not the personal portfolio level.

The [US Department of Labor](https://www.dol.gov/) released an AI Literacy Framework, the [UK Turing Institute](https://www.turing.ac.uk/) launched the third edition of "AI Skills for Business Framework," [UNESCO](https://www.unesco.org/) has AI capability frameworks for students and teachers, and academia has also proposed scales like Collaborative AI Literacy and Collaborative AI Metacognition.

It seems there are many frameworks, but most are answering the same type of question: Do you understand AI, can you use it reasonably, do you know its limitations? In other words, they test literacy.

What's truly missing is performance-level measurement: What have you actually produced using AI, what's the quality of the results, how much efficiency and value improvement compared to traditional approaches?

Put existing frameworks on a 2×2 matrix—horizontal axis "literacy vs performance," vertical axis "personal vs macro"—and you'll see the left side crowded with people (UNESCO, DOL, Turing, various academic scales), upper right corner has [Anthropic Economic Index](https://www.anthropic.com/research). The lower right corner—personal level, performance-based, verifiable—is empty.

Everyone is testing AI literacy. No one is testing personal AI productivity. This article proposes a framework to fill this gap. It's on the same trajectory as my previous article [AI Era Capability Gap](/articles/ai-capability-gap-2026)—that piece discussed the existence of the gap, this one discusses how to quantify it.

## AI Collaboration Portfolio Five-Dimensional Model: What to Measure and How

AI Collaboration Portfolio is a five-dimensional model measuring what individuals actually produce through AI collaboration. Design principle: each dimension must have theoretical basis, quantifiable indicators, and verifiable data sources from third parties.

### Command (25%)

Can you make AI do the right things?

Corresponds to Delegation and Description in the 4D Framework, and "activation attention" in attention theory. Anthropic found prompt quality correlates with output quality at 0.92—this may be the most critical single variable for AI collaboration effectiveness.

Quantified indicators: number of reusable skills/workflows/system prompts, number of automation pipelines (CI/CD, cron, GitHub Actions), AI tool integration breadth, complexity of multi-step task decomposition.

### Delivery (25%)

After AI collaboration, what did you actually ship?

Not what you tried, but what went live. Corresponds to task completion rate in the Economic Index.

Quantified indicators: commit frequency and volume, number of deployed services/tools, published content count, complete zero-to-live projects, code quality metrics (PR merge rate, issue resolution speed).

### Leverage (20%)

How many times did the same cognitive investment amplify?

This is the core of "40 minutes vs. 15 man-days." Directly answers enterprises' most pressing question: After you join, what's the team's capacity multiplier?

Quantified indicators: actual cognitive investment vs traditional estimation ratio, number of parallel projects, number of AI tools orchestrated, automation coverage rate.

### Quality (15%)

Can your output withstand scrutiny?

In an era when AI can mass-generate content, quality gatekeepers are unprecedentedly important. Corresponds to Discernment in the 4D Framework and reliability adjustment in the Economic Index—they found that when task success rates are factored in, productivity improvement estimates shrink by about one-third.

Quantified indicators: user/traffic numbers, system uptime, number of quality control mechanisms (automated testing, verification SOPs, review processes), external citations/shares.

### Influence (15%)

Are your methods being learned or adopted by others?

The transition from individual contributor to organizational enabler. The highest leverage form of value creation.

Quantified indicators: open source project interactions (stars, forks, contributors), skill/template adoption count, educational content reach, external methodology citations.

### Relationships Between Dimensions

To be honest: these five dimensions are not completely independent. High Quality is often a precursor to Influence. A powerful automation pipeline (Leverage) is itself a form of Delivery. Causal relationships exist between dimensions, and precise attribution can sometimes be blurred.

This is a design feature, not a defect. Real value creation is inherently multi-dimensionally interwoven. Revenue, gross profit, and net profit in financial statements also have causal relationships, but we don't only look at one number because each number reveals different facets. Five dimensions are five observation lenses, not five orthogonal axes. The goal is richer observation, not perfect decomposition.

## Anti-Fraud Mechanism: Automated Capture, Structured Self-Assessment, AI Validation

The most common challenge to any capability framework is whether it will become a gaming exercise.

This concern is completely valid. Goodhart's Law long ago pointed out: when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure. No quantification system is exempt from this rule. The real question isn't whether it can be gamed, but whether the cost of gaming is high enough to make manipulation uneconomical.

AI Collaboration Portfolio responds with a three-layer evidence architecture:

**Layer One: Automated Capture.** After user authorization, the system directly pulls data from third-party platforms—GitHub commit records, repo structure, CI/CD workflow files, web analytics, package downloads, social metrics. This data is recorded by independent platforms and cannot be manipulated by users. However many commits you have on GitHub is however many you have. This layer is the "hard evidence" baseline of the Portfolio.

**Layer Two: Structured Self-Assessment.** Parts that cannot be automatically captured (number of parallel projects, traditional man-day estimates, AI tool integration methods) use structured forms for guided completion. Each self-assessment field has an adjacent "evidence link" field. Those with attached links are marked as *Evidenced*, those without are marked as *Self-reported*. Anyone viewing your Portfolio can see this marking and judge credibility independently.

**Layer Three: AI Validation.** After users authorize GitHub access, AI analyzes repo structure and code patterns, independently suggesting scores for each dimension. AI suggested scores are displayed alongside user self-assessments. If you self-rate 90 but AI suggests 50, this gap is visually flagged.

Three layers working together: automated data cannot be faked, self-assessments have transparent evidence marking, AI provides independent reference. This doesn't eliminate gaming—it raises gaming costs to uneconomical levels.

More critically, the framework's ultimate defense isn't internal verification but external reality. Quality and Influence dimensions require evidence from outside user control—user adoption rates, community interactions, customer feedback, market results. Teams can internally collude to inflate Jira ticket counts, but can't force markets to buy or fake genuine open source community adoption.

This framework isn't an automatic judge. It's a forensic accounting system—an auditable ledger that shifts evaluation from "who tells the best story" to "whose evidence chain withstands scrutiny."

## Six Strongest Challenges and Responses: From Evidence Forgery to Survivorship Bias

We put this framework through a multi-model adversarial debate engine for three rounds of stress testing. Here are the six most powerful attacks and our responses.

**Q1: AI can help people forge evidence chains.**

AI can forge maps, but cannot forge the memory of walking that path. In framework-guided depth interviews, interviewers pursue not "what did you do" but "how did you make decisions"—why abandon that approach? How did you consider token consumption trade-offs? How did you handle model hallucinations? People who actually did the work can answer three layers of follow-up. Those performing from scripts collapse at the third layer.

**Q2: Leverage's baseline (man-days) is something you said is obsolete, using it as denominator is circular reasoning.**

Leverage shouldn't be interpreted as a static multiplier against a fixed baseline. Its core value is the rate of change over time. An engineer achieving 10x leverage with GPT-4 in 2024, still achieving 10x with Claude Opus 4.6 in 2026—this itself exposes adaptation stagnation. The framework tests growth slope, not absolute value. AI tools evolve, baselines shift (Red Queen effect), what truly has predictive power is your speed of adapting to each paradigm shift.

**Q3: Why not add "Adaptability" and "Ethics" dimensions?**

Adaptability isn't an independent dimension—it's the time derivative of the five dimensions. A person whose Command, Delivery, Leverage scores continuously improve across AI paradigm shifts demonstrates high adaptability. Same logic applies to "reflection loops"—the meta-skill of using AI to analyze and optimize one's own work patterns. It manifests as upward trajectories of the five dimensions over time, not as static scores in a sixth column. Ethics is a baseline constraint, not a performance indicator—violating ethics should directly disqualify, not deduct 15 points.

**Q4: Dimensions have causal relationships, precise attribution is impossible.**

Acknowledged. See "Relationships Between Dimensions" above. The framework pursues richer observation, not mathematical orthogonality. Where attribution is ambiguous, the three-layer evidence architecture provides raw data for evaluators to judge independently—this is the framework's design intent: it doesn't replace human judgment, it gives human judgment better material.

**Q5: The framework focuses on individuals, but the highest value contributions in the AI era are often shared cognitive assets.**

This is a real limitation of v1. Someone building a prompt instruction library for the entire department to use, designing a workflow that doubles everyone's efficiency—these network effects truly cannot be fully captured by individual dimensions. The Influence dimension partially touches on this, but insufficiently. Team version framework—measuring how a person amplifies system capacity, not just their own—is v2's most important evolutionary direction.

**Q6: The author using himself as a case study is survivorship bias.**

Methodologically completely valid. A framework's designer will naturally select evidence favorable to themselves. The response isn't to deny bias, but to make bias structurally unimportant: every claim in the case study below can be independently verified through public URLs, GitHub repos, or third-party analytics APIs. This case's value isn't statistical—it's engineering. It provides a complete, inspectable, replicable blueprint anyone can run with their own data. Think of it as open source release, not clinical trial. Initial versions inevitably reflect creator context, but their value depends on whether the community finds it worth forking, attacking, improving.

## The Cost of Not Measuring AI Productivity: Capability Gaps Are Invisibly Expanding

Why does this matter? Because the cost of not measuring is greater than measuring incorrectly.

The [Anthropic Economic Index](https://www.anthropic.com/research) records a deskilling effect: AI first takes over high-skill components of work, leaving the low-skill parts. If we cannot identify who truly excels at AI collaboration, high-capability individuals will be systematically undervalued, while those who interview well but produce poorly will be overvalued.

The productivity gap is real and expanding. University-level tasks accelerated 12x by AI, high school level only 9x. This isn't a small gap that slowly accumulates over time—this is structural divergence. And it's currently completely invisible to labor markets because no measurement system can reveal it.

On this topic "In the AI era, how should productivity be calculated?" using multi-model cross-verification, several honest limitations became clearer:

**Individual vs System Value.** The framework measures individual output, but the most valuable contributions in the AI era are often shared cognitive assets—prompt libraries used by entire departments, automated workflows that accelerate everyone. Measuring how a person amplifies system capacity—not just their own—is v2's most important extension direction.

**Reflection Loops.** The highest-level skill in AI collaboration may be using AI to analyze and optimize one's own work patterns—a meta-cognitive ability that drives growth in all dimensions. Current framework captures this indirectly through score time trajectories, but doesn't explicitly measure. Future versions could treat self-iteration speed as a first-class signal.

**Cross-Organizational Transferability.** AI collaboration occurs in extremely different cultural, legal, economic contexts. Collectivist cultures might systematically lower self-assessment scores. GDPR limits automated data capture. Small organizations can't afford AI validation infrastructure. A framework that only works in resource-rich English-speaking tech companies isn't truly universal.

**Fairness and Accessibility.** People with access to latest AI tools naturally have advantages in this framework. If the measurement system itself amplifies digital divides rather than revealing capabilities, it has failed.

These aren't flaws to hide. They're research directions for the next iteration round.

But the alternative to an imperfect framework isn't a perfect framework—it's no framework at all. It's continuing to evaluate AI collaboration capabilities using resume keywords and interview impressions, in a world where "familiar with AI tools" and "can use AI to do an entire team's work alone" differ by an order of magnitude.

What can be quantified can be valued. What cannot becomes invisible.

AI Collaboration Portfolio is a first attempt to make the invisible visible. It's an imperfect map. But the alternative isn't a better map—it's running blindfolded across a new continent.
