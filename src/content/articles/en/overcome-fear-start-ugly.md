2016, my first Demo Day at AppWorks accelerator. I'd prepared for three months, revised the presentation countless times, and my hands were still shaking five minutes before going on stage.

The result? No one remembered what I said that day. Including myself.

But I remember one thing: the fear before going on stage was ten times larger than anything that actually happened after. That was the first time I realized that the volume of fear is completely disproportionate to the volume of real threat.

This discovery was repeatedly validated throughout my entrepreneurial journey. CircleFlow, Ban Mu Tang, SDTI—every transition, what held me back was never insufficient capability, but being paralyzed by fear before I even started.

## Fear is Outdated Firmware

The fear system was originally a good thing. Tens of thousands of years ago, you saw a wild beast, adrenaline spiked, you ran for your life, and survived. The problem is, where are the wild beasts now? But our brains are still using the old firmware of "being abandoned by the tribe means starvation" to process a work email, a rejected proposal, an awkward social situation.

This is a serious misalignment between biological instinct and modern society. Except for life and death, all other fears are illusions manufactured by the brain. Fear of failure, fear of rejection, fear of what others think—these aren't real threats, they're your brain magnifying the world's difficulty by ten times, then deifying the rules and trapping yourself.

Psychological overload is the invisible prison of mediocrity.

## Demystifying the Strong

The habit of seeing others as big and yourself as small is the main cause of confidence drain.

I've met many "legendary" people in the startup world. After close contact, I discovered that any strong person's rise is essentially the result of timing opportunities combined with foundational resources. Strip away the halo and everyone's human, everyone screws up sometimes, everyone has moments of not knowing what to do.

Don't let others' light extinguish your own lamp. Everyone has their own rhythm. View others as equals to regain your sense of home court advantage.

## Make Garbage First, Then Talk

The difference between winners and the mediocre lies in 0.1 seconds—the mediocre wait for "foolproof," winners choose "make garbage first, then talk."

My best example is this website. The first version of paulkuo.tw was so ugly I was embarrassed to show it to people. But I pushed it online. Then during those 12 days I wrote about in "[Super Individual Case Study](/articles/super-individual-case-study)," I used it while improving it, improving it to this current version. If I'd waited until I was "ready" to start, this website would still be in my head.

Once you start moving, 50% of problems automatically disappear, and the remaining 50% will grow their own pathways during the process. Resources aren't waited for—they're attracted by your energy when you charge forward.

## Spiritual Independence is the Ultimate Trump Card

Switching from "seeking approval" to "self-upgrading"—once this switch is flipped, fear automatically dissipates.

The I Ching speaks of this realm: even if not understood, the heart remains untroubled. I spent fifteen years in seminary, and the biggest gain wasn't knowledge, but learning to keep walking when there's no audience. When your life's main thread changes from "pleasing others" to "self-evolution," many things become clear—dare to express, because you no longer fear judgment; can enter the game, because you no longer obsess over gains and losses; dare to persist, because you know destiny is forged through collision.

## From Individual Psychology to Systems Thinking

This is the same as entrepreneurship. Circular economy has a concept called "minimum viable cycle"—you don't need to wait for a perfect recycling system to start, run a rough loop first, optimize while operating. Psychological construction works the same way: don't wait until you're "ready" to set off, get yourself spinning first.

This is even more true in the AI era. Something you spend three months planning might see the world turn thirty times over. Acting first isn't compromise—it's the only rational strategy.

Correction in motion is a hundred times more effective than planning in stasis. Seeing through fear's underlying logic—it's outdated survival programming, not your actual situation—is the opening move for breakthrough.
