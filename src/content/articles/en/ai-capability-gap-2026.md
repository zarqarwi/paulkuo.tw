Late last month, a chart showing global AI usage distribution in 2026 exploded across social media. It's been circulating in my friend circles for several rounds, and each viewing brings different insights.

This chart spreads the AI usage status of 8.1 billion people across a mosaic of colored blocks. The visualization is intuitive, the impact direct: 84% of people have never truly used AI. About 16% have used free chatbots. Those willing to pay $20 monthly for AI service subscriptions account for only about 0.2% to 0.3%. And those actually using AI programming development tools—globally around 2 to 5 million people—represent less than 0.05%.

![2026 Global AI Usage Distribution Chart: Each dot represents approximately 3.2 million people, 2,500 dots = 8.1 billion population. Gray represents never used AI (84%), green represents free chatbot users (16%), yellow represents monthly $20 AI subscription payers (~0.3%), red represents AI programming tool users (~0.04%).](/images/articles/ai-adoption-2026-global.png)

The most important aspect of this chart isn't just the shocking disparity in proportions. It forces you to confront several questions: Which color block are you in now? How do you plan to move to the next one? Which block are your customers in? Are you prepared to enter this extreme world?

This isn't just an AI market infographic. It's a capability map of sorts.

## The World of Spectators

Many people's understanding of AI remains in a "spectator state." They know names like Gemini, OpenAI, Sora, Manus, Perplexity—like knowing the title of a popular movie without actually going to the theater to watch it. Everyone can recite tool names, but very few actually use them long-term or integrate them into their workflows. Many still view AI like Clubhouse from a few years ago—a trending topic that will cool down and be forgotten.

It's not like that.

A colleague asked me the other day: If we're interviewing for an IT director, what questions should we ask? My suggestion was simple—ask three things directly. Are they a paying AI user? Which AI tools do they currently use? Have them demonstrate actual AI projects they've run. Just by looking at what they've actually built, you can roughly tell if they're truly on this learning trajectory and whether they have the ability to lead a team forward.

## The Acceleration Itself Is Accelerating

In November 2022, my partners and I decided to go All In on large language model industrial applications. Three years later, I originally thought young people would be more willing to learn new tools. But after diving deep into production lines and the industry, I found it's not that simple. People willing to keep up seem to have certain personality traits that don't correlate positively with age.

AI's changes aren't linear. It's not "falling behind a bit today, catch up next month and it's fine." It's more like a system where the acceleration itself continues to accelerate. Enter one step late, and the world you see might already be a different version. The constant experience of running programs and interacting with models feels like it gets smarter than last week. This feeling is so intense that from February until now, I spend at least five hours daily interacting with AI—opening terminals, setting permissions, connecting APIs, making requests, validating results, fixing bugs, then entering the bug-fixing cycle again, accompanied by various API billing notifications. Daily pitfalls, but loving every minute of it.

While jogging the other day, I thought: What would be the difference between a Paul who's experienced this scorching AI baptism and one who hasn't?

Honestly, quite different. These two Pauls would have trouble understanding each other. The Paul who hasn't gone through this process would struggle to understand why anyone would spend hundreds of dollars monthly, what's meaningful about making custom software for yourself, or what's the big deal about producing McKinsey-level high-end research reports. "The world still seems to be turning, nothing's different."—Yes, that sounds perfectly reasonable, but those inside know it's not like that. AI isn't just an efficiency tool; it's more like a thinking amplifier. I wrote about similar views in "[Breaking Through the AI Storm: Personal Advantage Strategy Map](/articles/personal-strategy-in-ai-storm)"—the key isn't the tool itself, but what you use it to amplify.

## K-shaped Divergence: Not Just an Economic Phenomenon

After COVID, the concept of "K-shaped recovery" began widespread discussion—economic shocks don't hit everyone equally. Some ride the upper arm of the K upward, others slide continuously down the lower arm, with the middle hollowed out. AI's capability divergence is following the same path.

The ascending group uses AI to strengthen thinking abilities, multiplying their output. The descending group uses AI to avoid thinking, or doesn't touch it at all. The result is widening capability gaps that become increasingly hard to catch up with. This K-shaped divergence isn't just happening at the enterprise level—it's happening to individuals. Is this a hidden social risk? I believe it is.

## Taiwan Isn't the Global Average

Back to that chart. Directly applying the "global 8.1 billion population" AI usage distribution to Taiwan would underestimate Taiwan's actual situation. Taiwan has world-class density in technology and semiconductor industries.

According to 2024 industry statistics, Taiwan's semiconductor industry employs about 330,000 people, computer, electronics and optical products manufacturing employs about 250,000 workers (DGBAS salary and productivity statistics), and information software and services industry employs about 287,000 people (Institute for Information Industry MIC "2025 Information Software and Services Industry Yearbook"). Including telecommunications, systems integration, and IT departments within various enterprises, the broadly defined tech workforce reasonably falls between 900,000 to 1.1 million people.

According to iThome's "2024 CIO Survey," Taiwan's top 2,000 enterprises have about 140,000 IT professionals, with about 64% being developers, estimating core development personnel at about 84,000. This doesn't include SME engineering teams, startup companies, freelance contractors, and data scientists and technical professionals who frequently code. Taiwan's overall developer population reasonably falls between 200,000 to 400,000 people.

Those who truly enter the "using AI programming development tools" tier are highly concentrated in this tech-intensive group. If we estimate from a developer base of 200,000 to 400,000, assuming 5% to 15% have integrated tools like GitHub Copilot, Cursor, or Claude Code into their daily workflows, Taiwan's actual active users of these high-level development tools reasonably number between 10,000 to 60,000.

If we simply applied the global average of 0.04%, Taiwan's 23 million population would have only about 9,200 people using AI programming development tools, which obviously underestimates. Combining the "global average model" with the "Taiwan industrial structure model," I conservatively estimate: currently, Taiwan has between 10,000 to 30,000 people who've truly entered the AI programming development tools tier, representing about 0.04% to 0.13% of the total population.

Using the same logic to adjust Taiwan's overall AI adoption landscape: "Never used AI" drops to about 70%, "free chatbot users" rises to about 27%, "paid AI subscriptions" rises to about 2.5%, and the top tier "using AI programming development tools" rises to about 0.1%.

## The Few's Frontier, Everyone's Challenge

Taiwan is indeed entering the AI era earlier than the global average. But those truly standing at the cutting-edge tool chain with foundational creative capabilities—whether 30,000 or 60,000—remain a minority.

If you're already on this path, don't stop. If you haven't started yet, starting now isn't too late, but wait longer and you'll be chasing not distance, but versions.

Fellow bosses, entrepreneurs, CDOs, department heads—how do you view your enterprise's transformation? If the knowledge dividend required for transformation keeps widening? How do you plan to respond?
