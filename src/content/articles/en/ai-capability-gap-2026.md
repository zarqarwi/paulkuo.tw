Late last month, a chart showing global AI adoption distribution in 2026 exploded across social media. It's made several rounds in my circle of friends, and each viewing brings different insights.

This chart spreads the AI usage status of 8.1 billion people across an entire mosaic of color blocks. The visualization is intuitive, and the impact direct: 84% of people have never truly used AI. About 16% have used free chatbots. Those willing to pay $20 monthly for AI services make up only about 0.2% to 0.3%. And those actually using AI programming development tools number around 2-5 million globally—less than 0.05%.

![Global AI Usage Distribution Chart 2026: Each dot represents approximately 3.2 million people, 2,500 dots = 8.1 billion population. Gray for never used AI (84%), green for free chatbot users (16%), yellow for monthly $20 AI subscription payers (~0.3%), red for AI programming tool users (~0.04%).](/images/articles/ai-adoption-2026-global.png)

The most important aspect of this chart isn't just marveling at the disparity in proportions. It forces you to confront several questions: Which color block are you in now? How do you plan to move to the next one? Which block are your customers in? Are you ready to enter this extreme world?

This isn't just an AI market infographic. It's a capability map of sorts.

## The World of Bystanders

Many people's understanding of AI remains in a state of "bystandership." They know names like Gemini, OpenAI, Sora, Manus, Perplexity, like knowing the name of some popular movie but never actually walking into the theater to watch it. Everyone can recite the tool names, but very few have truly used them long-term or integrated them into their workflows. Many still view AI like Clubhouse from a few years ago—a trending topic that cooled off quickly, something to forget once the fever passes.

It's not like that.

A colleague asked me a few days ago: If interviewing for a CIO position, how do you judge whether someone has kept up with AI? My suggestion was simple—ask three things directly. Are they a paying AI user? Which AI tools do they currently use? Have them demonstrate AI projects they've actually run. Just by seeing what they've actually built, you can roughly tell whether they're truly on this learning trajectory and whether they have the ability to lead a team forward.

## The Acceleration Itself Is Accelerating

In November 2022, my partner and I decided to go All In on large language model industrial applications. Three years later, I initially thought young people would be more willing to learn new tools. But after going deep into production lines and industry, I found it's not that simple. People willing to keep up seem to have certain personality traits that don't positively correlate with age.

AI's changes aren't linear. It's not "temporarily behind today, catch up next month and everything's fine." It's more like a system where the acceleration itself continues to accelerate. Enter one step late, and the world you see might already be a different version. The constant feeling of running programs and interacting with models is that it seems smarter than last week. This feeling is so strong that from February until now, I spend at least five hours daily interacting with AI—opening terminals, setting permissions, connecting APIs, making requests, verifying results, fixing bugs, then entering the bug-fixing cycle again, accompanied by various API billing notifications. Daily pitfalls, but I enjoy it immensely.

While jogging one day, I thought: What would be the difference between Paul who experienced this scorching AI baptism and Paul who didn't?

Honestly, quite different. The two Pauls would have difficulty understanding each other. The Paul who didn't experience this process would find it hard to understand why someone would spend hundreds more dollars monthly, what's meaningful about making custom software for yourself, or what's the big deal about generating McKinsey-level high-end research reports. "The world still seems to be turning, nothing different."—Yes, that sounds completely reasonable, but those inside know it's not like that. AI isn't just an efficiency tool; it's more like a thinking amplifier. I wrote similar views in ["Breaking Through the AI Storm: Personal Advantage Strategy Map"](/articles/personal-strategy-in-ai-storm)—the key isn't the tool itself, but what you use it to amplify.

## K-Shaped Divergence: Not Just an Economic Phenomenon

After COVID, the concept of "K-shaped recovery" began widespread discussion—economic shocks don't hit everyone equally. Some ride the upper arm of the K upward, others slide down the lower arm continuously, with the middle hollowed out. AI's capability divergence is following the same path.

The ascending group uses AI to strengthen thinking abilities, multiplying their output. The descending group uses AI to avoid thinking, or simply doesn't touch it at all. The result is capability gaps growing larger and increasingly difficult to catch up with. This K-shaped divergence doesn't just happen at the enterprise level—it happens to individuals too. Is this a hidden social risk? I believe it is.

## Taiwan Isn't the Global Average

Back to that chart. Directly applying the "global 8.1 billion population" AI usage distribution to Taiwan would underestimate Taiwan's actual situation. Taiwan has world-class density in technology and semiconductor industries.

According to 2024 industry statistics, Taiwan's semiconductor industry employs about 330,000 people, computer, electronic products and optical manufacturing employs about 250,000 (Directorate-General of Budget, Accounting and Statistics wage and productivity statistics), and information software and services employs about 287,000 (MIC "2025 Information Software and Services Industry Yearbook"). Including telecommunications, system integration, and IT departments within various enterprises, the broadly-defined technology workforce reasonably estimates between 900,000 to 1.1 million people.

According to iThome's "2024 CIO Survey," Taiwan's top 2,000 enterprises have about 140,000 IT professionals, of which about 64% are developers, estimating core development workforce at about 84,000. This doesn't include SME engineering teams, startup companies, freelance contractors, and data scientists and technical professionals who frequently code daily. Taiwan's overall developer population reasonably falls between 200,000 to 400,000 people.

Those who would truly enter the "using AI programming development tools" tier are highly concentrated in this technology-intensive group. If estimating from a base of 200,000 to 400,000 developers, assuming 5% to 15% have integrated tools like GitHub Copilot, Cursor, or Claude Code into their daily workflows, Taiwan's actual active users of these high-level development tools reasonably number between 10,000 to 60,000.

Simply applying the global 0.04% average would suggest only about 9,200 people in Taiwan's 23 million population use AI programming development tools, which clearly underestimates. Combining the "global average model" with the "Taiwan industrial structure model," I conservatively estimate: currently, Taiwan has between 10,000 to 30,000 people truly entering the AI programming development tools tier, representing about 0.04% to 0.13% of the total population.

Using the same logic to adjust Taiwan's overall AI adoption landscape: "Never used AI" drops to about 70%, "free chatbot users" rises to about 27%, "paid AI subscription" rises to about 2.5%, and the top tier "using AI programming development tools" rises to about 0.1%.

## The Few's Frontier, Everyone's Challenge

Taiwan indeed entered the AI era earlier than the global average. But those truly standing at the cutting-edge tool chain with fundamental creative capabilities—whether 30,000 or 60,000—remain a minority.

This article isn't meant to create anxiety. Rather, I want to say: If you're already on this path, don't stop. If you haven't started yet, starting now isn't too late, but waiting longer means you're not chasing distance—you're chasing versions.

Fellow bosses, entrepreneurs, CDOs, department heads—how do you view your enterprise's transformation? Which color block are you in?
