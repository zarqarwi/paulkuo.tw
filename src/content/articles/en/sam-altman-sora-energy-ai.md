In a recent A16Z interview, Sam Altman did something that made a deep impression on me: an AI company CEO spent nearly half the time talking about energy.

Not discussing how powerful GPT-5 would be, not about when AGI would arrive, but about electricity, nuclear fusion, and infrastructure.

This reveals something many haven't yet realized: **The ceiling of the AI revolution isn't in algorithms—it's in infrastructure.** More precisely, it's in electric power.

## The Flywheel: Reducing the Cost of Intelligence

Altman describes OpenAI's business logic as essentially a three-stage flywheel.

The first stage is frontier research lab—creating the most advanced AI models. The second stage is large-scale infrastructure—deploying models at a scale usable by massive numbers of users. The third stage is personalized applications—enabling everyone to use AI for their own purposes.

These three stages form a self-reinforcing cycle: research produces better models, models bring more users, users generate revenue, revenue funds larger infrastructure and more research.

What's the core driving force of this flywheel? **Continuously reducing the "cost of intelligence."**

Think about it: ten years ago, getting a deep analysis in a professional field required paying consultants. Today, you can get at least an 80-point answer from ChatGPT within minutes. The cost of intelligence is declining at an astounding rate.

The lower the cost, the more widespread the usage. The more widespread the usage, the greater the returns. The greater the returns, the more infrastructure investment possible. The more infrastructure, the lower the costs continue to fall.

This is the power of the flywheel.

## Sora: More Than Just a Video Generator

Altman's positioning of Sora in the interview differs from most people's understanding.

Most people see Sora as an "AI video generation tool"—you input text, it outputs video. But Altman believes Sora's true significance lies in: **it's the beginning of a world simulator.**

Why? Because to generate realistic video, AI can't just "draw" images—it must understand the causal laws of the physical world. How does a ball move after being thrown? How does light refract on water surfaces? How does the center of gravity shift when a person walks?

These seemingly simple things mean AI must construct an internal model of the physical world. It's not "generating video"—it's "simulating the world."

Where does this direction extend? Altman mentioned "AI scientists"—future AI won't just analyze data, it can participate in the process of scientific discovery: proposing hypotheses, designing experiments, predicting results. If AI can truly achieve this, the pace of scientific progress will undergo qualitative change.

In my piece "[AI Always On: The Economic Orders Being Restructured](/articles/ai-always-on-economy-restructure)," I discussed how AI is restructuring economic operating logic. But Altman's vision goes further—he sees not just economic restructuring, but the restructuring of the scientific method itself.

## Energy: The Ultimate Bottleneck

This is the most sobering part of the entire interview.

AI's computational demands are growing exponentially. And what's behind computational power? Electricity.

Training a large language model consumes electricity equivalent to several months of a small city's power usage. And as models get larger and users multiply, electricity demands will only continue to soar.

Altman's energy strategy spans three time scales. Short-term relies on natural gas—not environmentally friendly, but reliable. Medium-term relies on solar plus energy storage technology—increasingly cheap, but with intermittency issues. The long-term ultimate bet is nuclear fusion.

Why nuclear fusion? Because nuclear fusion has the potential to bring order-of-magnitude reductions in energy costs—not 30% or 50% reductions, but down to one-tenth or even one-hundredth of current costs. If nuclear fusion is truly achieved, the constraints on AI computational expansion would be completely removed.

This is why Altman personally invested in nuclear fusion company Helion Energy. He's not doing charity—he's buying insurance for AI's future.

In my piece "[Comprehensive Analysis of Sovereign AI](/articles/sovereign-ai-digital-autonomy)," I discussed how autonomous AI development requires technological sovereignty and data sovereignty. But Altman reveals a more fundamental sovereignty: **energy sovereignty**. Without stable and sufficient electricity supply, all AI dreams are just empty talk.

## Sunrise, Not Explosion

At the end of the interview, Altman used a metaphor: AGI's arrival won't be an instantaneous explosion, but more like a sunrise.

Light doesn't "snap" and illuminate the entire world. It gradually, slowly seeps out from the horizon bit by bit. You won't notice some specific instant when "the sun rose"—you'll only realize when looking back that the world has become bright.

This metaphor is important. Because many people's imagination of AI is either the panic of "it will instantly replace everyone" or the dismissive "it's just a tool, no big deal."

The reality may lie between the two: AI's impact is gradual, continuous, and irreversible. Society will have time to adapt, but "having time" doesn't equal "will automatically adapt." You must actively adjust your position to align with the rhythm of this transformation.

## Alignment, Not Opposition

The greatest insight I gained from Altman's interview wasn't any specific technical detail, but a way of thinking: **The AI revolution is not an event, but a process.** It requires not just technological breakthroughs, but energy, infrastructure, social institutions, talent development—the entire system's supporting elements.

A thriving AI ecosystem needs stable, predictable, trustworthy platforms. This is an engineering of trust, not a technological arms race.

Before sunrise, the most important thing isn't predicting when the sun will fully rise. It's positioning yourself correctly—ensuring that when the light comes through, you're standing on the side that welcomes it, not turning your back to it.
