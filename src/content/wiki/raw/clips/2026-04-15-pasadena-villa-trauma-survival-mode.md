Pasadena Villa frames "survival mode" not as a lifestyle problem but as a nervous-system state. If trauma isn't processed, the autonomic nervous system can stay stuck in fight/flight/freeze even after the external threat is gone — producing the pattern colloquially called "steady-state survival."

The clinical reframe: survival mode is not permanent. It shifts when the nervous system receives the right input — somatic regulation, co-regulation with safe others, gradual re-exposure to safety cues.

Three practical intervention categories discussed: body-based (breath, movement, vagal-tone practices), relational (co-regulation with trusted others), cognitive (re-narrating the threat story so the nervous system updates).
