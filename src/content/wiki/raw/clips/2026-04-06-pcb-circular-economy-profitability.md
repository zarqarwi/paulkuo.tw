PCBs are the only category of electronic waste for which recycling is consistently profitable — irrespective of the technology employed — due to the intrinsic value of the metals they contain.

One ton of high-grade PCB scrap can contain 100–300 grams of gold, several kilograms of copper and silver, and traces of palladium, with total metal recovery value potentially exceeding US $10,000–15,000 depending on composition and recycling efficiency.

In 2022, the estimated gross value of metals contained in e-waste was 91 billion US$, with copper representing the highest value at 19 billion US$, followed by iron, gold, nickel, and aluminum.

Recovery rates for base metals are excellent, while precious metals show lower yet still significant yields, with gold recovery approaching 80%.

Recycling one ton of PCB scrap can save approximately 1.5–2 tons of CO₂ emissions by avoiding intensive mining, transportation, and smelting activities.

Establishment of local recovery facilities would reduce dependence on imported critical raw materials, thereby strengthening the resilience of national and European supply chains.
